## **Combat Support**

**Now let’s talk about combat support. Whenever you’re going to use drones for combat support, keep in mind the drawback I mentioned earlier about drones: They are fragile and expensive. Serious combat drones are extra expensive, with the Steel Lynx starting at 25 grand—and that’s before you load it up with weapons and ammo! That’s why I prefer to use combat drones in a fre-support role—the key word being support, which means they are supporting other combatants rather than shouldering the burden of winning a frefght on their own. I prefer to attack from hiding or around cover that I can easily dodge back behind. Your best bet is to use weapons with a very long range, so you can outrange most of your opponents. Sniper and assault rifes, grenade launchers, and machine guns are your best tools. Stay at least 500 meters away from your targets if you can, so pistols, shotguns, and SMGs can’t even hope to retaliate. When you must get closer, use clutter and buildings to block line-of-sight between your shots and never pop out of the same spot twice. If your opposition has a scary mage and street sam to worry about, they won’t have time to deal with your drones, which lets you pick your shots and your moments carefully. When it comes to using heavy combat drones, mod them out for survival as best you can. Using**

**the example of a Steel Lynx, I’d recommend adding increased structural integrity and layers of ceramic plate, and since I’d want to protect such an expensive investment, I might also spring for chameleon coating. Don’t use it in sustained combat, though, as it can only handle so much. Pop out and lay waste with a shock-and-awe attack, then get it back into its drone rack or whatever other concealment you can manage. For the nuyen, though, I’d rather go with the BAE Ajax, as it already comes stock with the weapon mounts—including missile tubes!—increased structural integrity, and ceramic armor—and it fies. Getting a Steel Lynx up to the stock weapon mount and defensive mod specs that the Ajax has would cost you more than the Ajax does off the shelf. I’d much rather have something that can fy so it can get in and out of trouble with ease. The MCT Roto-Drone is an old standby, but these days I love to make use of the Hawker-Siddley Harrier (p. 108,** _**Double Clutch**_ **). It’s cheap, fast, has a surprisingly decent stock dog-brain, and comes with a standard weapon mount right out of the box. All you need to do is mount an assault rife (the AK98 is perfect, as it’s both powerful and inexpensive) and load it up with ammo, and it’s good to go— don’t forget to buy autosofts for it, which you’ll ideally be running on your RCC. I’d suggest loading a copy of Evasion and Targeting: AK-98 onto the drone itself for when it has to run autonomously.**

**I have a few honorable mentions for combat support drones. The frst one I already mentioned— the MCT Hornet has a toxin injector, which really comes in handy sometimes. Load that up with Narcoject, Seven-7, or gamma-scopolamine, and it can be very effective—as long as the target doesn’t see it coming. Flying Eyes can mount grenades, but they’re too expensive to use liberally—but the Evo Roller-Bombs barely double the cost of a grenade. Totally worth it. Last, there’s the Whispering Death with its monoflament lines and stealth features—if only it were faster, it would be the perfect drone for recon, surveillance, and combat support. As it is, most metahumans could outrun it if they saw it coming. And I almost forgot the Proteus Krake II, which is cheap and incredibly effective at taking out any threat in the water. Just one of these can put plenty of holes in even well-armored boats. Unfortunately, the water interferes with Matrix signals, so unless you run a cable to one, you won’t be able to jump in or rely on your RCC’s autosofts, and the dog-brain isn’t very good. For serious use, I recommend upgrading the pilot system and loading it with a Targeting and Stealth autosoft.**
