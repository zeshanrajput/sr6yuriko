## Post-Blackout Highlights

The blackout left downtown in shambles. Much of the DBC still hasn’t recovered. Power is spotty and usually provided by generators (professional and makeshift), the police have abandoned providing services in the area, the infrastructure is slowly collapsing as it is stripped for valuable wiring and parts, the corporations have locked down their facilities in the region in order to protect them, and the heart of the region has almost no access to the Matrix, despite the zone being only three kilometers across. To add insult to injury, the megacorporations are using their massive influx of insurance money to build elsewhere, rather than recovering here. The same can be said of the insurance and aid money (garnered before independence) received by the government.

St. Louis is about to be reborn. With huge influxes of construction and design jobs, politicians will quickly obfuscate the huge number of people who are about to have their land stolen out from under them as they rebuild on the other side of the river. The ARCHology is likely just the first project in St. Louis that will be built over the river, mark my words.

The lack of sprawl cops in the DBC means lawless groups, like gangers and crime syndicates, are moving in en masse. They’re bringing in resources and shaping power grabs all over. This is opening power vacuums and creating turf wars as their rivals take advantage of how thin they are spread. It’s altogether possible one of these small groups could manage the “March Across Asia” made famous by that old flatboard game Risk, where forces are spread thin on a continent to defend the borders, and if their front line is broken, a rival can march across the map. It’s usually a short-term gain, but that’s sometimes all you need to break a competitor for good.



Power (literal electricity) vacuums are being filled by deliveries of junkyard castoffs and old timer mechanics. The gas motor from a 2025 Dodge Viper (I know it’s a two-cylinder) pumps out plenty of juice to keep the batteries in that supercar running indefinitely, and it does the same for locales all over the DBC. Older, even more powerful, engines are coming in, and old-fashioned gas and diesel are now hip to import again. Quantities aren’t huge, but a single tanker run is a bulky payoff for the smuggler and power (haha) for the local syndicates for months. Sure, that’s not the usual, but runners often look for the big retirement score, and that very well could be it.

DocWagon and CrashCart contracts are being honored, but the entirety of the DBC is HTR territory. Higher premiums and prepayment from Basic contract holders are the usual corporate tax, but some of the crews have found a side hustle in taking the job with smaller fees paid straight to them. Make friends with your local DocWagon drivers and save not only your hoop but your wallet.

Mentioning corporate efforts reminds me that I should also mention that the megacorps’ territory is still extraterritorial, but the lack of power makes it hard for them to lock their territory down. Now, I’ve heard the sob stories too, but make no mistake—corporate employees were left behind and have plenty of nuyen accessible on the outside. If you escort them out, it’s easy enough to hold them accountable once outside the CBZ in order to get your payment. Remember that plenty of them have security forces on the ready, but killing them will just drive the point home further that you should be paid.

The corps are also looking for scads of runners to pull out data, tech, and the occasional bit of trained meat from the CBZ. Fleeing after the blackout did not go smoothly, and with the new corporate rebuild efforts going on across the river, opportunities to reacquire key bits of tech and data to use across the river is pushing plenty of work and money into the shadows. Cheaper to send a team of runners into a former electronic security hub filled with cyberdecks than to buy new ones.

As long as you trust the runners!

As for the Matrix issues, well, you’re stuck with those. No one is putting any effort into the rebuild. The number of operating commlinks is slowly growing and allowing a mesh network to grow, but the destruction of local grid boosters and megacorporate terminals and equipment are leaving this area with spotty Matrix reception.

About the only bright point coming out of all this is the development of several new urban brawl zones inside the DBC. The St. Louis Archers are going to have several practice pitches that get used for real. Home-field advantage is about to move them up the ranks for sure. Not to mention the massive influx of nuyen coming in from the nation

of St. Louis, the rebuilding corps, and the team’s new owner, the Sea Dragon.

On the shadowy side of life, the DBC is gaining a reputation as the place to doss and build a place to launch runner efforts from. It kind of makes sense that shadowrunners are all going to find their way into a place that is pretty much all shadows, all the time.

A lot of shadow denizens don’t care that power needs to be hauled in with batteries or run from generators or stored by pulling a suncell off an old Spirit and hanging it off a balcony. Even without consistent power, runners have found a place whee official authorities don’t come looking, and it’s right in the heart of this sprawl. It’s easy to run in and get away, though it takes some skill or a little teamwork to slip out. A network of eyes and ears have set themselves up to make some nuyen by guiding runners out of the DBC. It’s smart to keep them on your good side, though, because crossing them will let you exit right into a trap.

Being able to set up your runner HQ in the DBC is all the more important because having the option to run out on ops straight from the heart of downtown is making a lot of politicians and corporate executives in the outer districts quite happy. Keeping the riffraff focused on a part of town most now consider just a dead zone is helping districts on the periphery. They were expecting to become barrens and runner havens, but they are getting a chance to draw developers looking for somewhere outside the shadow of the ARCHology and downtown. That move toward the periphery is helping St. Louis expand across the turf it claimed upon its departure from the CAS. Many are already starting to think that if St. Louis doesn’t bulk up and fill out its new wardrobe, the CAS may come in and annex some of its lost land. Though to be fair, just as many think the CAS is waiting for St. Louis to build up before coming in to take it back over.

With all these changes, the districts of the sprawl are going through some growing pains of their own. Power brokers shifting their support around is changing not only the physical landscape but also the political and cultural identities of the districts. Downtown has taken a big political power hit, and they’re looking for new sources of support. They still have the ARCHology and the grey zone around the DBC, but across the river in the East St. Louis District, megacorporations are building a whole new skyline based on similar rules to old St. Louis. Nothing can be taller than the ARCHology. It’s a ridiculous new limit, and the corporations are glad to build themselves a new strong foothold in the center of North America after the losses of so many cities on the continent.

These new moves are offering plenty of new struggles in the political arena as political powers make friends with corporate powers and all work together to keep their designer shoes on the throatsof the citizens of St. Louis. The maneuvers aren’t just limited to inside St. Louis, as the UCAS is not happy, but they’re also not in a place to put up a fight. They’ll be maneuvering in the shadows for the time being, but expect their efforts to be precise. They can’t afford to sow massive waves of discord but must instead stoke the flames of particular dissidents that are the most likely to burn down their opposition. Work will come from forces in the UCAS government at times, but more will come from the state of Illinois. Chicago is a roller coaster they have needed to get off for awhile, and powerful connections into a free St. Louis will benefit them far more than trying to juggle the mercurial disaster zone that is Chicagoland.

While these politicians make their deals, there is a powerful coalition pushing for more corporate control. They don’t want another Manhattan, but shaping St. Louis into a midwestern Seattle is closer to their ideal. All of the Big Ten are looking for a place in independent St. Louis and a plethora of AA corps are seeing the benefits of getting in on this action. Working their way in after the city is set could be easy for an MCT or Saeder-Krupp, but AAs like Maersk, OmniStar, and Erika are going to need to get in early and build a strong foundation. Overall, it is just leaving a lot of power brokering going on, and everyone expects the control landscape of St. Louis to look a lot like a kaleidoscope image.

That is, if another force, a massive force, does not come in to consolidate power for themselves. Anyone who has paid attention to the action around St. Louis and up and down the Mississippi knows the Sea Dragon is working on gaining control from the shadows. She uses corporate fronts, all small and independent, to avoid answering to the Corporate Court and protects them with her seemingly unending supply of resources. She’s a player here too and while I’m sure she has the ability to simply push her way into control like another dragon did on the continent, I foresee her weaving a web of control over the city that includes the apparent power players all acting as various chess pieces to the queen of the sea.
