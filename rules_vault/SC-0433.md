## Sniper

## Backgrounds

**>** Being “good with a rifle” doesn’t make you a sniper.

Snipers are, of course, good with a rifle, but the distinction between a sniper and a sharpshooter lies in the other ninety-five percent of the job snipers do besides pulling triggers. A sniper is a trained observer and gatherer of intelligence. A sniper is a mathematician who performs computations too complex for smartlink technology to factor out. A sniper is a master of illusion and can move as silently as a shadow. In fact, a sniper is a master of not only the parameters of the physical world but also the capabilities and limitations of the Matrix and the astral plane. A sniper is a psychologist who knows the enemy’s mind.

You might even say being “good with a rifle” is one of the least important things about being a sniper. **>** Tsét’soyé

Many shadowrunners view snipers the same way the public does—through their perennial depictions in fiction. The concept of a death-dealing bullet arriving with preternatural precision—from an impossible distance, outrunning the telltale crack of the rifle—is both terrifying and thrilling for an audience. They are commonly understood to be morale-sapping terrors on the battlefield and the enders of crises when hostage negotiations fail. The image of the deadly assassin with an enormous rifle graces the title screens of more movies and trideos than can be easily counted. While rooted in kernels of truth, these depictions are usually as distorted from truth as are the shadowrunners in _Karl Kombatmage_ . Professional snipers, as opposed to action heroes and villains, are consummate team players. Their training is rooted in one or both of two broad categories: military and law enforcement. Either way, snipers act in support of and in concert with a larger team.

No matter the background of a sniper’s training, their archetype-defining feature is marksmanship. Although their niche far exceeds the role of putting holes in targets, the rest of their abilities are ultiBACKGROUNDSmately for naught if they don’t hit what they shoot at. Snipers are another breed entirely from the better-known gun-toting archetypes such as street samurai and weapon specialists that they might superficially resemble. Reaction speed and graceful, fluid motor control are paramount to such infighters, but they are almost secondary in concern when firing your weapon from a virtually motionless supported or prone position. Mental agility can be far more important than physical agility, as extremely long-range shots require compensating for factors completely ignored during close infighting, such as the rotation of the earth. Because snipers spend much more time waiting for the moment to fire than sending ordnance downrange, they also require mental fortitude and practiced discipline to combat eye fatigue, maintain wakefulness, spot concealed targets, and generally remain alert. Furthermore, taking aim for long periods of time draws directly on willpower and training.

Every hunter knows that marksmanship is only a fraction of the entire skillset required to bring down a critter. The arts of stealth, particularly camouflage, and a keen understanding of both their target and the terrain conditions must also be employed correctly to even get the shot in the first place. This is just as true for snipers but can get quite a bit more complicated when they are hunting sentient prey. They must rival a covert-ops specialist in stealth, but snipers will often be forced to rely more heavily on camouflage than silence. Snipers are intimately focused on understanding their environment, both to maximize their concealment and to anticipate their targets’ actions. For a sniper, mastering camouflage and perception are two sides of the same coin.

A key difference between a sniper and a hunter lies in intelligence gathering. Every sniper is a trained observer, and as such they put their mastery of perception to full-time use. Given a suitable vantage point and surveillance equipment, a perceptive sniper will learn security procedures and identify weaknesses therein, discern numbers of personnel onsite, infer the purpose of structures through usage patterns, and frankly any number of extremely useful pieces of information shadowrunners would find invaluable. While observation over time comes with the inherent downside of needing time to pass, it can provide intelligence that hackers or faces might not ever discover via their own avenues. And at the very least, a sniper’s gathered intelligence can augment their legwork or even stand in for it entirely, if a team lacks those kinds of runners.

The sniper archetype isn’t defined solely by their skillset. There are several key abilities that snipers must acquire, whether via magic, augmentation, or equipment. The most prominent tool in their arsenal is their weapon. Sniper rifles are built for precision and range rather than portability and rate of fire.

Snipers are always rewarded when enhancing their weapon’s accuracy by whatever means are available to them, whether technological or magical. Modern firearms can employ a wide array of different types of ammunition, which permits snipers to employ their primary tool to accomplish more tasks than just putting holes in things! Snipers will also require significant vision enhancements to properly fill their niche. Optical magnification and night-vision options provide obvious advantages, but there are so many more possibilities beyond that. Endoscopes or drones allow a sniper to gain visual access to walled-off spaces otherwise shielded from line of sight. Thermographic vision can potentially allow a sniper to discern targets’ locations through opaque barriers as well, depending on conditions. Even though snipers will often remain stationary for long periods once in position, getting into that position usually requires more than just skill. Depending on the mission at hand, snipers may need climbing gear, lock-breaking equipment, or even demolitions. And last but certainly not least, snipers lean heavily on equipment, augmentations, or magic that enhances their ability to remain undetected. Snipers may need advantages beyond camouflage or invisibility; for example, a sniper with internal air tank cyberware can go for hours without their breath giving them away in cold weather or to thermographic sensors.

As mentioned previously, snipers tend to have some sort of a military or law enforcement background. While the role of the archetype remains the same between the two types of snipers, they focus on different dimensions and usually employ fairly different tactics. Military snipers focus primarily on scouting and intelligence-gathering. They commonly employ sophisticated surveillance equipment such as powerful vision magnification, laser microphones, overhead spy drones, and so on. When magically capable, these kinds of snipers find great value in remote-sensing capabilities such as clairvoyance or astral projection. The ability to use camouflage is paramount because they will typically achieve an observation point and then remain there more or less motionless for extended periods of time. They will typically employ ghillie suits or blankets because they are a cheap, effective, and best of all dependable option to blend in with their environment. More sophisticated measures such as ruthenium tech or invisibility magic are also highly desirable, but these can be subverted. Even worse, they can give away the sniper’s location to hackers or astral observers. When military snipers employ their rifle, they prefer to be at ranges as extreme as possible—beyond the range of their targets’ weapons, preferably. Their fire can, of course, be sent with the intent to kill; typically, they aim for center mass to minimize the chance of a miss at the extreme ranges they prefer. Military snipers, however, are perhaps even more often destroying materiel that is much more valuable than any single soldier. Their fire may

not even be intended to hit any target in particular—once the bullets start flying, military snipers are often employed to sap morale with nuisance fire or to prevent movement in undesired directions. Of course, when tactically necessary, military snipers won’t hesitate to kill. Ultimately, the best counter to a hostile sniper is a friendly sniper.

In contrast to military snipers, law-enforcement snipers lean less on intelligence-gathering and more on firing for effect. While both kinds of snipers act in concert with larger teams, law-enforcement snipers are typically operating in much closer proximity to their supported team. Where a military sniper prefers to engage targets at hundreds or even thousands of meters, law-enforcement snipers are usually no more than fifty meters away from potential targets. They have diminished need for extreme magnification; indeed, having a low zoom is more advantageous within one hundred meters because it allows a sniper to maintain a wider field of view while employing a scope. Due to their closer deployment to potential targets, law-enforcement snipers will often take shots that are not aimed at center mass. Severing the brain stem is a hallmark high threat response sniper’s tactic, because success will not only instantly incapacitate the target, it will also prevent them from reflexively firing a weapon or triggering an explosive. This closer proximity to their teammates and diminished need for powerful optics means law-enforcement snipers are relied upon less for intelligence-gathering. However, during standoffs or crises, they are the natural actors to supplement or even perform intelligence-gathering behind hostage-takers’ barricades and to keep tabs on terrorists’ movements throughout an occupied structure. Whereas military snipers are as familiar with explosives as any warfighter, law-enforcement snipers can rival an ordnance-removal team’s training. They will often be required to rig explosives to shatter windows or other barriers in concert with their shot, so that their bullet’s path is not deflected or even stopped outright.

Shadowrunning snipers will typically come from one or both backgrounds, even if their personal path is unconventional. For example, snipers who got their start as insurgents in a war zones or as hunters will resemble the skillset of a military sniper. Assassins or gang warriors will often employ tactics that resemble law enforcement snipers. No matter the background, snipers running the shadows must be proficient in and be able to combine both schools of tactics.
