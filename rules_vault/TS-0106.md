## **I Want My Phone Call**

**So the worst has happened. You were out minding your own business, trying to get some work done, maybe retrieving a piece of stolen property for a grateful employer, or helping a confused citizen fnd a new job, and next thing you know, you’re being told to drop your gear and lie on your belly by a heavily armored thug with a badge, all while staring**

**down the barrels of six different guns. The harsh spotlight from the circling helicopter or drone makes everything washed out and hard to see. You’re tired from all the work you’ve put in up to that point, and now they want you to drop your delicate and expensive gear on the concrete and lie on the ground in the rain? He says it again, and the guns pointed in your direction don’t give you much choice, so you lie down, right in a puddle of acid rain. You hope these cops have the money to reimburse you for the latest from Vashon Island, but you don’t get to think about it for too long before there’s a knee on your back and rough hands are grabbing your arms, wrenching them into handcuffs behind you.**

**Once you’re cuffed, they leave you there for a good fve minutes before one of them comes over, rolls you over, and picks you up by your already ruined clothes before patting you down in the most undignifed way, rifing through your pockets. They then walk you to an open car door and the plastic back seat of a black-and-white SUV, where the nicest thing they say to you in this whole process is “watch your head” as they stuff you into a seat smelling of disinfectant and faint traces of vomit.**

**The uniformed thugs leave you in this car while they yuk it up outside amongst themselves and wait for their specialists to arrive. Finally, a driver arrives in your car and they give you an accusing glare through the rearview mirror before starting the SUV and driving off to the precinct house. The drive is silent. This back compartment must be soundproof in a way, so as to prevent someone with internal comms gear from listening in. No one is interested in what happened, though. The only noise is the hum of the tires on the road.**

**You pull into the station and are met by a team of three more uniformed goons. They all have stun batons. The door opens and you are unceremoniously dragged out, while one of them with a stun baton gives you a snide nod as if to say “Do it. I dare you.”**

**You’re perp-walked through a scanner, made to point out and scream at any sign of augmentation, legal or otherwise. If they detect any, they disable it if they can; otherwise they pump you full of sedatives and wacky drugs to keep you docile. Failing that, you better hope you aren’t a magician. They have special masks for that, guaranteed to drive you out of your mind. Everything you own is stripped from you, put in neat little baggies and shuffed off to a locker somewhere in the building. They slap an inhibitor cuff on you to suppress the use of any cyberware, or a Grey-Mana-infused cuff if you are a magician before you come out of your drugged haze. If you’re in one of the higher-end establishments, you may even be issued your own nice set of temporary orange coveralls and paper shoes for the night.**

**then and there. Once all that is said and done, depending on what they think you did, the cops let you contact someone.**

**Outside contact after arrest is not a universal rule. It’s standard procedure in most municipalities in North America, South America, and Europe to allow a suspect to call someone who can help or set up legal counsel.**

**That’s where I get the call.**

**If I’m on retainer or if I’m being hired, I’ll make it a point to be there to provide that legal counsel as fast as I can. You may have to stew in a concrete room for a little bit, but remember, don’t tell them anything without your lawyer beside you. Generally speaking, they’ll haul you into a square interrogation room with a table welded to the foor and a oneway mirror. Usually two offcers enter, and they ask you a bunch of questions; some may seem inane and unrelated to the case. But don’t budge. If you do, any of these may be used against you down the road. Their goal at this point is to get as much information from you as they can before someone like me shows up and twists their plans into a knot.**

**They really don’t like it when someone like me shows up, because that means all their little tricks go out the window. No more can they lean on your ignorance of the law and specifc legal code to weasel out information they would otherwise not be privy to. If they did use any of those tactics, like trying to scare you into giving something up, or admitting something you didn’t do, I’ll be all over them. They’ll always say cooperation with them makes your path ahead so much easier when all they really want is to make an arrest and move on to the next case. I’ll nail them to the wall for that. Anything that was said will be inadmissible in court, and any cop involved in that kind of coercion will be one more bullet in the chamber when it comes to having your case outright dismissed.**

**But before we go that far, let’s talk about getting an attorney like me on your side. The easiest way is to just have me on retainer to begin with. This means you and I have sat down in my offce and agreed that I will be representing you as your attorney in legal matters going forward. We’d sign a few papers, add a few thumbprints, and we’re off to the races. If you get arrested, just call my offce and I’ll be down to that precinct house faster than you can say “speedy justice.” If we don’t have a current arrangement, and you wish to hire me as your legal counsel, make sure you have my LTG number memorized. It’s one of the reasons I have such a catchy jingle, so you’ll remember it when they take your commlink away from you and you’re sitting in a holding cell.**

**Once I get to you, we can talk about what you have allegedly done, and who is accusing you of doing it. During our frst session, I’ll go over what is probably the most important part of our relationship: attorney-client privilege. This means that whatever you say to me, I cannot under any circumstances divulge this to anyone else. The information you entrust to me shall remain safe and confdential with me. This is one of the cornerstones the client-attorney relationship and privilege is built on. Without it, there can be no trust between us. Even in the face of threats from law enforcement, I cannot give up this information. The cops know it, and they hate it. They even have to provide us with a private room where we can speak to each other without them looking over our shoulder.**

**This expectation of confdentiality is recognized by national jurisdictions in most places around the world. There are some places where this expectation of privacy between an attorney and their client is much less absolute and recognized. For example, corporations tend to not care quite as much about this confdentiality, as they see it in their best interests to know what is being said. In a corporate environment, coded language or another way to convey information is sometimes necessary. Normally I can convince the corporate security to allow such privacy, but it can be a crapshoot as to whether they actually allow it or not, so I prepare for both possibilities. Also, some, well, less-civilized nations do not allow for this privacy, especially during their times of war. But those aren’t usually places where criminal defense attorneys will operate.**

**At this point, let me be completely transparent. Everyone knows lawyers aren’t cheap. Good lawyers cost even more. When we frst meet, I won’t charge you much; I’ll only request a token exchange to make our arrangement offcial. This can be as little as two nuyen or a single corp scrip; it doesn’t matter. It just shows that you and I made an agreement for me to become your legal counsel, and from then on you are afforded certain privileges, such as having me or my staff there during your questioning, daily or weekly meetings, and courtroom preparations. Once this is exchanged, we can talk about actual money.**
