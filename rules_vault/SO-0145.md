## Scaling

The Nuyen Box might be considered a standard for a con that requires getting gone as soon as it’s done. Whoever you were needs to vanish in quick order. The question of scale is geometric and obvious: bigger scores for bigger fish need bigger lures. Cashing in on the lazy and wealthy is great, it’s why whales were invented, but they’re one in a million and probably poached out already. You have to have a killer app to sell, and the client will want a demonstration. The brazen (who end up rich or, more often, dead) have been known to sell allegedly stolen prototype tech, the kind of things runners are paid to extract. If you were to undertake such a run on your own time and sell the goods (or double-cross a Mr. Johnson, if you want to burn your fixer and never work again), you’d be under the same pressure. In this case, it’s about making something that fits the bill. All the better if you’ve sprinkled evidence in databases belonging to the corp or organization you allegedly ripped it off from. In some cases, it’s demonstration enough when they do their own digging and the original owner’s “reports” indicate its success.

**>** Especially if you’re selling alleged bio-weapons. Frag me. **>** Kat o’ Nine Tales

The smaller scale is way easier because the stakes are significantly lower. The smallest versions are as clean as selling an alleged stolen credstick or commlink to someone on the cheap because of its inherent risk in use. You’re accepting less than it’s worth because you can’t handle the heat if you were caught. Your targets here are the greedy ones, especially if they’re the harder types or linked into smalltime strongarm outfits. You might see a little blowback if they’re local enough to come knocking when they realize you scammed them, so it usually works best if you’re operating outside your usual stomping grounds. Which is good policy for any scam, really.
