## Elements

The prop is part one. Something you can dazzle with, something that looks important and breaks easy but you have an excuse to be carrying.



- All it takes is a half-decent fab lab and you can make some pretty slick stuff. Complicated components grafted together might not pass muster when they’re assembled, but it you make it sufficiently delicate, it’ll come right apart when it hits the ground.

- Beaker

- The great thing about technology is that every fresh development is an evolution of previous tech, and prototypes often look like reconfigurations of extant parts. Broken into pieces, no one can say what it was when it was assembled. Throw in some false patents and liabilities, and the right mook will quake at the implication of litigation.

- Nightfire

Second, have a good story and act. Be ready to sell how much this thing is worth. Be careful with saying it belongs to a corp or something, because they’ve got insurance or people who aren’t going to just accept cash on the mistake. The idea that cash can make this right is important, and entities that don’t give a damn about cash aren’t going to make the sale.

Third, scale it to the mark. This is a small-tier gig, you’re not dropping Fabergé Eggs and hoping a wage slave can make it right. Aim for people who can afford to pay pay—and importantly, people who will pay.
