## Backgrounds

Here’s a fact: You know more fictional famous private investigators than real ones. Sherlock Holmes, Hercule Poirot, Philip Marlowe—all fictional. Now name a real one that’s as famous. Maybe you came up with Allan Pinkerton. Maybe some other name. Maybe no names at all. But whoever you thought of, none of them is as famous as the fictional ones.

Here’s a common explanation: The reason for that difference is that while fictional private investigators are noble souls who right wrongs and see connections and solutions the police overlook, real-life private eyes are grubby, ignoble creatures who peek in windows to gain evidence to support people’s divorce proceedings. Maybe some of them are noble, but those ones are broke.

That’s not entirely wrong, but also not entirely right. A lot of PIs are scumbags, squalid little creatures not imaginative to succeed at insurance fraud, so they play lapdog to those who are (and as we all know, the worst purveyors of insurance fraud are insurance companies). They don’t have any particular skills, just the tenacity to keep on a case and the lack of morals to gather evidence through questionable means. That’s not all of them, though. Sometimes, the reason you don’t know about the best private investigators is the same reason you don’t know about the best accountants. The really good ones aren’t there to draw attention to themselves. They’re there to get a job done.

Both the singular virtue and singular vice of the private investigator is that they work outside the system. It’s a vice when they don’t play by the rules that limit conventional investigators. For example, we all know that while cameras placed everywhere mean we’re watched every second we’re away from home, home is generally sacrosanct. The corporate bigwigs certainly don’t want people spying on what they do behind closed doors, and since they haven’t figured out a wayto exempt themselves from legal spying in a way that would stick, they allow laws putting at least some limits on domestic surveillance to stand. All that means a cop isn’t supposed to plant a camera in your home just because they feel like it. Private investigators, however, often feel no compunction about that. They will plant surveillance devices anywhere and everywhere they want. And they ignore other rules cops sometimes play by. Due process? Entrapment? Making secret recordings? To a lot of private investigators, those are completely valid techniques.

But on the virtue side is the fact that many of the rules normal law enforcement work with are complete bulldrek and should be circumvented. There is a long list of things their corporate masters don’t want law enforcement looking into. Corp-sponsored human trafficking? Rampant pollution? Wage theft and other financial crime? Those things and much more are elements the corps would prefer remain unexamined. Of course, they’re also things that regularly hurt people at the lower levels of society, so they can’t just be overlooked. This is where a private investigator can come in, trying to introduce some balance onto the scales of justice.

They don’t always need to be a licensed PI. They may work for a legal firm, an insurance firm, or for whoever can scrape a few nuyen together to get help with a matter the powers-that-be ignore. The main requirements are tenacity, good observational skills, and some ability to charm and/or irritate people you need to question.

So yeah, a lot of private investigators are grubby little window-peepers. But some of them are people who walk the mean streets but are not themselves mean and are neither tarnished nor afraid. They’re good friends to have and can be a valuable part of your team—if you can get around their loner tendencies.

Private investigators are not all cut from the same cloth. They bring different experiences and skills to the table, and that shapes their approach. The largest commonality is that they watch what’s going on around them and try to figure out connections they can make. Beyond that, the field is wide open.
