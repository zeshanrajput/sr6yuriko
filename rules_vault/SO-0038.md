I used to watch a lot of sci-fi shows when I was a kid, and there was one that really stuck out to me. This alien would say, “Understanding is a three-edged sword. One is yours; one is theirs. And the last is the truth.” While I have a hard time conceptualizing what a three-edged sword looks like, that line has stuck with me my whole life. The concept of perspective; that reality is subjective, and the magic that comes from thinking that way.

The consequence of dwelling on that concept was I spent a while trying to be a writer in my youth. In the tradition of Clark, Straczynski, and Imeh, I wanted to tell stories that took people away to distant places and implausible circumstances and have them believe. There was just one problem: I wasn’t very good at it. Everything felt devoid of reality, caught in the nadir between the unoriginal and implausible. An old teacher of mine once said that all fiction holds a kernel of truth, and that’s what draws us in. Great writers hold that flame close, the spark of the real that stems from true human experience. But I’m not a great writer. I got caught up in the truth, and the way people spin it. And that’s how I got here. I’m not looking for fiction anymore. I’m looking for lies.

A con is a lie, plain and simple, and all lies have goals. We lie to get something. Respect, access, wealth, whatever—we present something false to obtain something real. It’s easy to vilify a lie, but we all do it. And sometimes we do it for the right reasons (depending on your definition of right). You get out of a ticket, you cover for a friend, you push your résumé over the top to get the job you know you can do even if the employer doesn’t quite believe it if you stick to the hard facts. From surprise parties to first dates, every lie has a goal. Even empty lies may at least provide the thrill of getting away with something, a degree of unauthorized control over our own lives through the perspective of others.

If it helps, not everyone really sees lies for what they are. There’s the aspiration; you tell someone you run a couple kilometers a day, and you make a mental note to start doing that because then it won’t be a lie when you reflect on it—all you got wrong was the date you started. Or the justified; you tell the cutie at the bar you’re single. You aren’t, but your partner cheated on you and that might as well count as available, right? And the aforementioned qualifying lie; they’ll only give you the job if you have five years’ experience with their proprietary program suite. It doesn’t matter if the suite has only existed for two years, or if you helped design it, or if you have the skill or expertise to figure it out quickly, maybe even improve it. You tell them you have five years’ experience because you want a job, and you’ll get it done. If anything, you’re helping them get past their unqualified demands.

There’s a varying degree of scumminess to those examples, but every one of them is justified in the mind of the one telling it. Maybe it’s because lies and stories are very cozy neighbors. They draw from the same creative pools, speak to the same instincts and desires. Neuroscans of people who tell stories and people who lie show that a lot of the same parts of the brain light up, with the difference being the level of stress involved. Telling a good story has a much lower threshold of tension beyond the fear of judgment or public speaking. Whereas a lie triggers the fight-or-flight adrenaline thrill, the spike of cortisol. The fundamental difference? A story has permission to be told, while a lie violates consent.



An interesting thing happens when you change that perspective, however. Another classic piece of cinema (gotta tip my hat to Red for this one, good find) has a cop learning how to go undercover and points out the internal difference between a lie and a story. After all, a lie is tense, you’re sweating and crawling inside, waiting to be caught. A million little tells start to pop up for those who know how to look for it, and lots of people start to subconsciously pick up on something being off. But a story? You’re not a liar, you’re an actor. You’re playing a part. You can adjust who the audience really is. Maybe you aren’t concerned about the people you’re fooling because they’re comrades in on the joke. The point is, your stress is decreased by the idea that permission has been given somewhere along the way, and that means you feel supported rather than isolated or hunted.
