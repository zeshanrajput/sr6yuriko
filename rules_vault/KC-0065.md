_Bungo:_ What thing was that?

_Kenzie:_ Right here, when he asks you if you’re saying this thing was just aimless vandalism. It’s early in the conversation, but right from the start I noticed a lot of tension in his face. Look at his lips, his jaw, his brow. There is tension all across his face, and then he’s trying to not let it show, so that manages to make it both better and worse. His expression is blank, but he has forced all of the tension into his upper lip and nose. Then he asks the vandalism question, and you give the answer that sort of agrees with the premise and sort of doesn’t. While you give that answer, the tension drains right out of his face. Watch the nose. It raises a little and widens. That’s because of the way he was holding his upper lip. His jaw relaxes, too. The tension doesn’t come back into his face for the remainder of the conversation.

_Bungo:_ Do you think that’s genuine?

_Kenzie:_ It’s impossible to be sure, but when people fake gestures, they go bigger. They want the fake gesture to be noticed. Look at his hand gestures when he’s saying he will contact you if your services are needed. Those are for your benefit. He wants to seem warm, sincere, and open, so he breaks out gestures that are larger than he had performed in the rest of the conversation. That’s what you do when you are faking a gesture or doing something you don’t really feel. The nose thing, though, was so small, it could have been overlooked in the conversation. Did you notice it when it happened?

_Bungo:_ No.

_Kenzie:_ There it is. I can’t say he’s not playing a very subtle game, but my opinion is that was a genuine reaction.

_Bungo:_ So what does it mean?

_Kenzie:_ It means relief. He was worried about something, and what you said relieved the worry. That’s all I can say—I have no idea _how_ what you said relieved him.
