## **Tactics**

**The French Touch can be seen as a powerful lobbying force, a well-oiled legal machine, and an intelligence-gathering outft all merged into one. Its tactics are therefore, not surprisingly, heavily based on negotiation and intimidation. If the time for those maneuvers has passed, and concrete actions like retaliation are on the agenda, then the consortium delegates that part to the members best equipped to deal with it.**

- **A funny side effect of this is that, often, corp B is asked to retaliate in the name of corp A. But corp B has no real stakes in the game. So corp B tends to be very aggressive in its retaliation implementation and takes the shortest path to be “done with this matter,” and when corp B is way bigger than the entities targeted for retaliation, you can imagine how this goes.**

- **Mr. Bonds**

**Since the end of the Megacorporate Audit, the French Touch has seldom resorted to retaliation (other than the legal kind, I mean). In fact, ninety-nine percent of the power wielded by the consortium is akin to “aggressive lobbying” (backed by the shadows, if needed). Which also means that, in the last years, the Grand Tour has become more and more strategic for Fauchon and Saurat’s monstrosity.**

**Why? Because there is no better place to negotiate, intimidate, collect blackmail, and even do the blackmailing itself! You can also have backroom discussions, unoffcial agreements between parties**

**So both the summer and winter editions of the Grand Tour have become, over the years, an integral part of the French Touch strategy. Because the consortium is not a corporation, it has no boardroom to plan its action and lay out its goals. So the informal meetings and discussions during those events become their meeting rooms.**

**Let’s say a few execs from Louis Vuitton meet with people from Index-AXA at the opera in Vienna. There, they casually discuss some interests both their corporations share. They run into each other again, a few days later, for a wine tasting on a yacht near New Monaco and simply carry on with the discussion. It all looks like chit-chat and small talk, but in fact, between meetings, both teams strategize within their hierarchy and broker an entirely informal agreement. Down the road, those deals become (unoffcial) policies of the French Touch, and the consortium does its best to support them, despite (or thanks to, depending on how you see it) the complete lack of a paper trail.**
