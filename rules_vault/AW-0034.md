## Vehicles on the Metaplanes

First problem with using vehicles in the metaplanes is going to be getting them there. Astral projection-type travel relies on recreating tech from

memory, and for some reason most vehicles don’t want to manifest that way. Metaplanar passageways allow vehicles through without issue, but you need a portal big enough to get one through, and most are just too small for anything past a motorcycle. Of course, some of the megas have made portals big enough to get cargo haulers and mining equipment through, but don’t expect to get access to any of those.

Second big problem when it comes to vehicles, you’re going to have to bring your own fuel. There are some gas stations to be found on the metaplanes, but they are rare, and not all of them have the kind of fuel that you’re going to need. The best vehicles to bring along are muscle-powered, like bicycles and rowboats. I’ve never heard of anyone trying to bring a horse-drawn carriage, but I bet that would work pretty well—assuming the horses don’t freak out too much. With some exceptions (such as the cities, or many parts of the Plane of Kin), you aren’t going to have the luxury of roads. You can forget about xGuide and your roadside assistance service. If you get lost, blow a tire, get stuck, or run out of juice, there’s not going to be anyone coming to help you. If you didn’t plan ahead well enough, you’re going to have to abandon your ride and any cargo you can’t carry. For that matter, the astral gateways created by free spirits are rarely large enough to admit anything much larger than a motorcycle or ATV. If you see a helicopter or a convoy of trucks, you can be sure that those belong to a megacorporation with access to a gate of their own. Their security escorts tend to be very trigger-happy. Don’t expect them to be happy to see you, and don’t expect them to lend you a hand if you’re in trouble.

Depending on where you are going, you are almost certain to see locally manufactured vehicles in use. In the Hudson Valley, boats are incredibly common—most are rowboats or wind-powered, but steamboats with huge paddle-wheels are not an unusual sight. On land, carriages are in wide use and are often pulled by unseen spirits far more quickly than a draft animal from Earth could accomplish. Trains connect most of the towns and cities on quite a few metaplanes, though there is no metaplanar express I know of. Airships are a strange sight to see, though they are put to abundant use in the elemental planes (particularly the border spaces near the Plane of Air). Many of them resemble large sailboats from ancient Earth, but others are small cities floating on a disk of stone. Apparently something the spirits call “true air” is woven into the airship structure, which makes it buoyant in the air. At the helm are controls that allow an operator to adjust this buoyancy to adjust their altitude. I’d love to try hacking one of these things, but I never got close enough. They don’t seem to be doing whatever the equivalent of wireless mode is here, so I’d probably need a directconnection. The airships of the Plane of Fire tend to be much more compact and made of an obsidian-like inflammable material. These move quickly, powered by flame-producing jets that can be used as weapons as well as a means of propulsion. If you ever get to ride in one of these, be sure to strap in. These things are as maneuverable as a t-bird— even the big ones can turn faster than you would think they could.

That brings us to drones. Most of us are so used to our drones automatically wirelessly recharging themselves on the Matrix, we’d be stunned if they ran out of juice. But remember that there is no Matrix on most metaplanes, and so there’s no wireless induction recharge mode. Most drones operate on their own battery power for about six hours, while energy hogs like roto-drones might last less. Make sure to bring along spare batteries to recharge your drones, but also be sure to pack an electronics hardware kit. Solar power still works in most places that are well-lit, and there are other sources of power that can be found and used to recharge your batteries, drones, and other devices. If you’re handy with a soldering iron and know a few tricks, you can get by. Power is easy to come by in or near metaplanar cities, but you might have to get creative when on the Plane of Plants or Beasts.

Drones often have a shorter-than-normal range. Unless you’re being actively jammed, you can usually count on a range of up to a hundred meters even in a Matrix dead zone. That range limit doesn’t seem to be in effect on most metaplanes. On the elemental planes, there is no detectable signal degradation due to distance, effectively allowing your drones to have unlimited range (provided they have the fuel or power). This varies more on the Plane of Kin. There is some indication of signal loss due to noise or some unidentifiable but subtle interference, but I have been able to operate my drones at distances of up to ten kilometers without significant difficulties—I didn’t have to do much more than run signal scrub software, and everything worked just fine. Low-tech metaplanes do weird drek to your RCC and drones, but there’s no limitation on range there. You just have to deal with the fact that your combat drone turned into a bear and your Fly-Spies are now songbirds. The planes of Beasts and Plants are the exception to this—these places seem to barely tolerate technology. They won’t translate your gear into a different form, but there’s a subtle background noise that messes with electronic signals and degrades their range. When in the open, you’re probably good out to a hundred meters or so, but in places of dense foliage you shouldn’t send anything out of your sight that you expect to see again. Drones that lose signal access on the metaplanes get confused very quickly and are likely to crash and burn if they don’t shut down completely.
