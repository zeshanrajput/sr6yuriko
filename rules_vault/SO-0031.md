## Heist Point Designations

Your team’s legwork activities will fall into a specific category depending on what type of information or prep you’re looking for—these categories are called Heist Point Designations. You can only earn one Heist Point in each designation. If your team performs well enough on a test to earn another point in that designation, you still gain information as you normally would, but it won’t provide an additional Heist Point for that designation. We’ve listed six designations in this base set. Some of them may not apply to every run, while certain scenarios masy involve additional unique designations.

**Physical Security** . This covers information about sensors, drones, metahuman guards, critters, guard shifts, and other aspects that protect a physical location or item. For some runs, this could also represent the response time for law enforcement and routes based on the location they’re coming from. For runs that involve a moving target, this would include the route taken, in addition to the security on each side.

**Astral Security** . Checking the astral space around a location during legwork will highlight the use of wards, alchemical preps, spirits, and other astral threats. In addition, this could include information on any organizations they contract their ward maintenance to, who the mage is, and whether they rely on magically augmented guards or mundanes. If the other side uses paracritters or other magical creatures, it would be included in this classification.

**Matrix Security** . The best time to check on Matrix devices and hosts is before you need to interact with them. Understanding the network topology, host ratings, spiders, types of IC, and what devices are connected to which locations allows you to anticipate the next steps in a run. In addition, knowing if the spider is onsite or remote provides you with a secondary attack vector.

**Ingress** . Planning your entrance involves thorough research on the various types of security within a location, but it should also include understanding other aspects of the surroundings. This could involve the people who work in and around the area or finding floor plans for the facility. If someone knows enough about the people, they can pose like they belong there. Knowing who brings in the laundry, handles food, or does the cleaning could also provide a way in. And when in doubt, plan a distraction to cover the team while they go in.

**Egress.** After it’s done, it’s time to get out. Planning for a quick and stealthy exit will depend on gathering the right information on local streets and traffic patterns, vehicle options, obstacles, and potential response routes from local law enforcement. After you get out, you’ll need a location to lie low, tend to any medical needs, rearm, and regroup. You’ll also need to determine if the exfiltration was successful or if you were tracked.

**Oh Drek.** Something always goes wrong. Maybe the planned entrance is closed for repair or there’s another team hitting the target at the same time. Perhaps someone accidentally tripped a sensor and alerted security. This category represents those plans you make for when things go sideways. This can be Plans B–Z, any bolt-holes you’ve identified ahead of time, secondary teams to provide support, or simply knowing there’s something soft at the bottom of that trash chute you’re about to jump in.
