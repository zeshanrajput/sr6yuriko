## Cadence

Any visit to the Plane of Battle is highly likely to be fraught with danger at every turn. Unless you find yourself in one of the yurt-style tents belonging to an elder minotoad, you will be facing danger from the lava bombs in the valley as well as Ghost-knows-what as opponents. And trust me, most people who go to the Plane of Battle take interfering in their rites and trials very seriously, often to the point of both sides joining forces to attack you for interfering. And they won’t care if it’s where the vagaries of the metaplanes plop you down as you first arrive, or even if you’re just falling down the mountain while trying to navigate the perilous paths to a minotoad settlement.

Then you have the mature minotoads to contend with, who might be going about their daily life hunting for food and take umbrage at you for being in front of them, or perhaps you’ll stumble upon a mass melee of them trying to settle some dispute.

Roughly one in eight of the trips I’ve been on has resulted in bugs dying to my claws. The damn things seem to come back every few months to try to take some minotoads to use as hosts if nothing else, so this is one situation where joining a fight in progress will not result in you becoming a target of both sides: it seems whoever comes to the Plane of Battle knows that the bugs are the greatest threat and consider everything else secondary. Once the bugs are dead, then you need to be careful.

The Plane of Battle is a hot environment, as it’s essentially a long, thin strip with many active volcanoes in the mountains on either side of the valley. For those projecting to the metaplanes via an astral quest, that’s not a problem, as you’ll be materializing and thus protected. However, if you take another route to the plane, which you would have to if you wanted to bring mundanes or drones or something, then you will need to have some way of cooling off as well as some fire resistance. The lava bombs that fly out of the volcanoes are nasty fraggers, so you’ll want some acid protection, too, just to be on the safe side.

There is also one other thing that travelers need to be aware of when going to the Plane of Battle— the cadence. That’s the term I use for the phenomenon I experienced the first time I was there, as well as a few times since. The cadence typically comes into play during large set-piece battles, but I’ve seen it happen to smaller team fights as well. Essentially, whenever there are four or more people per side in a battle, there is a chance that the strange magic of the plane will take hold. When it does, each person in the team will start synchronizing their attacks in a way that keeps their enemy off balance. The cadence builds as time passes, almost like a positive feedback loop, where attacks lead to more cadence, and that leads to more attacks and so on, until you reach full synchronization. And that’s

when you break out in song. Which song is down to the individual or group; I’ve seen renditions of operatic classics, troll rock, lazrpop, classic rock, even Gregorian chants and religious hymns and the like. So long as one or two members of the group know the entire song, the magic could take hold of it and use it for the cadence.

Another facet of the innate magic of the plane is that fatigue and tiredness only matter outside of combat. On my first trip, the reason it felt like we’d been fighting the hoard in the valley for days was because we literally had been! Hunger, thirst, tiredness, fatigue—all are irrelevant while fighting. They will, however, bite you in the hoop the minute your heart rate settles down after the fighting is over, so make sure you’re ready with the stuffers or whatever else you plan to use to keep yourself going. When projecting, obviously hunger and thirst are non-factors, but running and fighting for hours or days on end will still tax anyone’s capabilities, without any way of getting respite. You will be gassed, so plan accordingly to put yourself in a safe space once your heart rate comes down.
