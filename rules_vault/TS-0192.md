## **Posted by: Nick Ryder**

**haven’t caught up with them** _**yet**_ **. Other times they happen to know a guy, and on rare occasions,** that guy happens to work for the very company responsible for hauling him in.

Despite what people may think, local law enforcement and folks in the community meet on a pretty regular basis. And it’s not just when the cops are hauling someone off, even though that turns out to be most of the time. The most common reason for an offi cer to venture into the neighborhoods is to build and maintain their relationships, to build that personal network of contacts, and work to prevent crime when they can (which isn’t often). This means connecting with those folks who’re the most likely to commit future crimes, just to get a better read on what’s going on.

It feels like a deal with the devil, and in many ways, it is. But sometimes, rubbing shoulders with some of the most dangerous, craven, and vile creeps in a sprawl is the only way to catch the bigger fi sh. By giving LEPs what we need, we can feel more comfortable about turning the other cheek when it comes to some of the pettier operations.

- Vile? He’s really selling that fact that a lot of us do more for the community than any law enforcement provider.

- Old Crow

It can make you wonder: Are the criminals you work with really that good, or really that stupid? A slag who’s had more than a few scrapes with the law, maybe even did some time; how are they still alive, let alone in business?

- Sure, there’s a few good runners who care, but let’s be honest here. Most of the runners I’ve worked with have been sociopaths. Destroying local infrastructure, stealing from some mom-and-pop shop, killing innocents with some errant gunfire; the list goes on. So come off the high horse.

Sometimes these kinds of people are actually that good at what they do, but that’s rare. Most of the time they’re simply lucky, or their choices

- LSPD



**Of course, not all of them are that bad. Some are just ex-cons who got in too deep and are looking to keep their lives straight. Other times, they’re actively looking to enact revenge or frag over the competition. And still others may be somebody who bailed your hoop out of a bad situation.**

**There is a difference between a snitch and an actual informant. Snitches don’t even have to be criminals in their own right, but they’re usually the least petty ones. Drug dealers, users who we’ve picked up, even a mean drunk who got separated in the tank can be a snitch. These are the people who’re close enough to get an earful, but not deep enough to get a full breakdown. They know the word on the street because they’re the closest to it. These are the people who can tell you if something big is about to pop off, or if the local syndicates have called in out-of-towners for something big. They’ll know the newest drugs on the street, and which gangs are slinging them. They may also know if a gang or organization is out for blood, and who they’re gunning for.**

**Snitches have to be careful, though. Say too much to the wrong people, and they get a target on their back. Hell, say too much at all, and other people start noticing. And these are the kinds of people who don’t like being talked about.**

**On the surface, some of this information appears superfuous, and it is. On its own, some of it doesn’t mean a thing, but take a step back and it becomes another part of the larger puzzle.**

**If I were to tell you the difference between a snitch and an informant, it would be between two factors: discretion and how deep they are in a given organization. Someone classifed as a confdential informant would need access to privileged information not available to anyone who happens to be listening at the right time. They would have a measure of trust in them within their organization. And they’d have to be willing to betray that trust in order to provide select information to the cops.**

- **Typically, the way I look at it is that confidential informants are smart. They’re people you’ve chosen to invest in and are likely to help out. A snitch is someone who isn’t worth the time or investment. They’re going to sell you out on the first shot or they’re just looking to get an edge. That doesn’t mean you shouldn’t use a snitch, just saying how much I’ll trust one.**

- **> LSPD**

**These organizations can range from a street gang all the way up to the inner circle of a crime syndicate and beyond. Some informants operate within corporate circles as well, but their impact is lessened due to extraterritoriality.**

**Still, having the information is valuable when many of these corporate slags think they can get away with anything because they live under a corporation’s protection. So they go out and kidnap people in broad daylight, supply their drugs to a local gang, or any other number of monstrous actions they think they can undertake with impunity. If any of these drekheads takes one step outside their precious corporate housing, we nail them to the wall so hard they won’t know what hit them.**

- **We had a guy who was putting lots of guns on the street by pulling product off the line as “defective.” Now, what he pulled from the line wasn’t really broken. It was missing (or had removed) a key part that was easy to install after the fact. Bribes were paid and the ring kept going. There wasn’t a good way to nail them all because they basically had corporate immunity; all we could do was hit the mules. Eventually, we brought enough evidence to the corporation’s security, but all they did was nail the peons while ignoring the big dogs.**
