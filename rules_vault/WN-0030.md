## Manhattan Districts

Despite its small size, New York is a layered city. Her denizens move along specific pathways through their own sections of the city, often linked only by stops on the elevated rail surrounding Manhattan. While people can easily travel fromone part of the city to the other, they’re often so culturally different that it feels like stepping into another country.

There are fifty-seven distinct neighborhoods in Manhattan. To complicate matters further, the neighborhoods themselves continue to change over time as ownership of the lands, homes, and businesses within them changes hands.

According to the mayor’s office, Manhattan is divided into thirteen districts. Each of those districts has one or more police precincts and an elected councilperson who serves on the city council. These districts largely exist in name only, or as small parcels of land—such as in Greenwich Village where the publicly run Washington Square Park is located, but the surrounding real estate is owned, operated, and leased by CitiGroup. City Council representation in these defined areas serves primarily as a liaison to the people who actually make the decisions. Corporate ownership lines are messy, crossing district boundaries and snaking around each other like pieces on a Go board. For the sake of consistency, I will break things down the way the UCAS government prefers we see things, but with a little extra info to clue you in on how things truly work.
