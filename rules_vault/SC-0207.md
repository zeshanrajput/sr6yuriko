## Backgrounds

You tested off the charts on the Matrix aptitude tests at a young age and were picked out by corporate talent scouts. Yes, the headhunters tend to start as early as they can, locating promising young candidates from among impoverished demographics in order to acquire them for proper training and indoctrination as inexpensively and effectively as possible. In most cases, corporate-sponsored educational services are used to gather information to target certain students for special programs. Your parents weren’t exactly impoverished, but they always struggled with finances. You can remember the late-night arguments that always seemed to be about not having enough money. A few days after a recent and difficult round of standardized testing, a corporate talent scout contacted your parents. They celebrated, as if you’d won some wonderful award, and then you were shipped off toa corporate boarding school far from home. “Full scholarship” seemed to be the password that bypassed all of your parents’ defenses. You didn’t get the chance to see them again for years.

Training was intense and competitive, pitting groups of students against one another in Matrix exercises. You happened to be very good at it, with an apparent knack for denying and countering attempts to mess with you and your teammates. Many of your fellow students washed out in the first few weeks, but you thrived and rose to the top of your class. You were rewarded with your first implant—a simple datajack—but it was a huge moment for you.

As the years went by, most of your education focused on topics of Matrix security and defense. A few times it seemed that you might need to take some time off from school to earn some nuyen to pay for tuition, but a corporate contract always swooped in to save the day every time. You got a cyberjack implant, a cyberdeck of your own, and an expensive education, all the while never seeing the grip tighten around you. Your peers all seemed to be doing well and never seemed to question the situation, and so you rarely did as well.

When it came time for graduation and employment, you couldn’t help but notice that your options were narrow — restricted by the contracts you had signed over the years. Though the pay and benefits were good, the lack of freedom became starkly apparent to you. You went back over the contracts you had signed so eagerly, finally reading in detail everything you had agreed to, and realized that you had signed your career options away for the foreseeable future. So what, though, right? It’s what you had been planning to do anyway, getting a high-paying job doing what you’re trained and equipped for. Why should it bother you? And yet, you could never get quite comfortable after that realization. It festered and grew. You did your job, but your performance reviews were often tagged with behavioral recommendations. You couldn’t help but feel trapped, and it brought out some of the less productive aspects of your personality.

Then something happened. Perhaps something went wrong under your watch and your career took a dive, maybe you were framed by an enemy or just because you were the expedient target, maybe you were extracted by a team of shadowrunners, or lured out of your megacorporate life by an organized crime syndicate. Whatever the event was, it was the catalyst for your escape from corp life, and you took it. You also took some expensive hardware with you, and you plan to use it to make a lot of nuyen. Enough to build a new life born from your own choices, and one that no one else can ever tear down or take away from you.
