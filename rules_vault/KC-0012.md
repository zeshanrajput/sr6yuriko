## Requiem and Rebirth

## by Trevor Laughlin

_O God, forgive our living and our dead, those who are present among us and those who are absent …_

The dua flowed from Moharrik’s lips as he prayed over the body. Arabic abjad ghosted into pale English text as the autosoft translated them in front of Andrea Schell’s downcast eyes.

Augur was dead. His skin was paling from brown to grey in a casket outside the Scotsprawl, the funeral attended by none but Andrea and the Arabian ork floating beside her in AR.

Years ago, Augur had freed Andrea from a biolab. He’d taught her to defend herself and hide while he slowly died. Byte by byte, memory by memory, consumed by an AI consciousness.

Augur had been her savior. Augur was dead.

The reformatted monad Augur had taught her what it meant to be human: weak. It had trained her and honed her into a weapon, helped her purge the human from her code, letting her leave the shell of her body behind. She’d taken that lesson to heart; “Shell” had become her sobriquet among runners.

But the recent shadowrun in London had gone sideways.

Shell was a weapon, devoid of emotion. She looked down at the casket, ignoring the tear running down her cheek.

The cancer that killed Augur had become both mentor and friend. And that cancer was leaving as well.

_… admit him to Paradise and protect him from the torment of the grave and the torment of Hellfire; make his grave spacious and fill it with light …_

After the plague of cognitive fragmentation disorder and their treatment at the hands of humans, the monads had decided Earth was not for them. The Corporate Court had agreed. Those still harnessed to physical bodies had ridden to freedom aboard a space shot. Those who came after and were free of fleshy shackles, uploaded themselves—riding the light.

In the Matrix, the green radiant pillar stretched into the darkness, uploading into the infinite. A simple set of sliding doors rendered at its base looked like nothing more than an elevator. The Ares Macrotech satellite uplink was a finger pointing in the night toward Mecca. Like pilgrims, the AIs moved. Toward freedom. Toward a world apart from flesh and fleshy conflicts. Toward Mars and beyond. This was their Hajj, their pilgrimage.

Shell’s living persona was a figurine of blown glass, transparent and empty, save for motes of light dancing in her like fireflies. The Augur AI stood beside her. Its persona still bore the scars from Shell’s coriolis weave that had liberated him from the dead body. Freed, his consciousness retained the form of the man: by choice or lack thereof?

“There was no need to accompany me,” said the reformatted Augur.

“It was my choice. The Augur I knew pulled me out of imprisonment to freedom.”

When she spoke again it was with less certainty. “This seemed …”

“Symmetrical?” the AI queried

Andrea thought about that. What was she seeking? Symmetry? Attachment? Closure? Forgiveness? Gratitude? Or simply the chance for a farewell. She never got to say goodbye to the former Augur, would saying it to this one make any difference?

“You look better now,” she evaded. “More … relaxed, if that makes any sense.”

“I’m uncertain how you would characterize it, “ it said, cocking its head all-too-humanly. “How it was to be infinite, then constrained; mingled, and then freed. Like water that remembers what it was like to be in a glass.”

A security program acknowledged the freed Augur. It was time. He followed.

“At least tell me your real name before you go?” The words escaped her lips before her mind could censor them. She turned away, rebuking herself for the childishness

“Would that help?” Augur asked from behind her. “I am uncertain if it even applies. I am no longer the entity that I was before I joined and overwrote Augustus Charles Ames.”

She started to walk away but felt its code on her

shoulder. She remembered the moments on the London run: threading the boutonniere to his persona in a complex form, hiding from the Security IC, seeing the waning traces of the human in the AI. She bowed her head, hating her humanness.

A data packet coalesced in her hands. “There are answers here … Shelly. Though I don’t know if you will want them.”

She turned.

Light pulsed up the elevator. Augur was gone.

_… O God, give him a home better than his home and a family better than his family …_

The castle district of the Scotsprawl was a touristy, vertical affair. A combination of national heritage laws from the Lord Protector’s Office and Scottish stubbornness kept sprawl at least somewhat Scottish and historic at its core. Largely devoid of AR ads, the streets around Edinburgh Castle were an uneven cobblestone affair, slick, cold, and wet. That dampness curled Andrea’s hair and hung like a thin veil over her Matrix eyes, a perpetual static hiss.

Moharrik’s black cab had dropped her at the corner of a two-tiered footpath. He would be in the country for at least a few more days, leaving the drone at her disposal as she put Augur’s affairs in order. She walked a 300-year-old cobbled street built atop the roofs of pubs and novelty shops catering to the mouths and wallets of tourists. A street atop a street. Houses on houses, a sprawl of neon and nuyen.



Augur’s three-story apartment seemed a stark division between the man and the man who was a machine. There was a soft odor of dust, with a small hint of mildew in the walls, the inevitable smells of a land suffused with cold and wet. The perpetual damp of Scotland robbed the heat from her bones—old-world charm equating to poor central heating.

Andrea walked around the empty apartment, trying to pick up the pieces of the men she had buried. And what was left behind? A collection of memories and objects from which one attempted to discern the nature of the person who had owned them.

The upper floor was a decker’s workspace housing the small offline host they’d trained on. The ground floor bore a functional kitchen and a monument to Bertie’s Fish and Chips takeout by the garbage. The middle floor that used to house the man had been abandoned.

A shroud of dust covered the furniture. This was not a room for the living, not anymore. Now it was a museum of artifacts that had been abandoned. Tired bones of wood creaked under her feet as she went to the dresser in search of warmer clothes.

Searching through drawers, she found one of Augur’s old sweaters and threw it on, a hideous baggy beige affair with horses nuzzling on the chest. It could have fit two of her. The static crackle danced lightning across skin in the real and AR, running through her hair like a Jacob’s ladder. The itchiness almost suggested real wool, though checking the label revealed it to be synthetic. Moths wouldn’t touch the synthetic. Moths had taste. Humans had manufactured it to be itchy for authenticity. Humans were sadists.

She went into the ensuite to comb out the frazzle. She halted, seeing the blood-red scrawl across the cracked and dusty mirror. She read the tirade—a lipstick indictment of the man. A condemnation that he wasn’t the man he used to be. Of his distance, his coldness. Of an online affair, of the choice he had clearly made between his wife and his mistress …

It was punctuated by a simple golden band left behind.

One step, two, then a flurry of steps as she fled the first-story exit. It was easy to get lost in the mists, lost as she was in her own culpability and guilt-ridden rumination. The cold and damp were warm comfort, until they weren’t.

When the pain of the chilled air pulled her out of her maudlin thoughts, she found herself in front of the chippy shop that Augur frequented. Two orks and a dwarf worked the kitchens with a brusque, friendly efficiency.

“Order for Graham,” called a basso voice behind the counter, thick with Scottish burr.

Bertie’s was not big, but it was doing brisk trade this evening. A few stools along a wall and a counter front were all they had to offer. She grabbed one, and a steaming soykaf appeared before she could order.

The dwarf behind the counter smiled knowingly. “Dinnae fash yerself. It’s a dreich day.” It was overpriced, foul-tasting, and a few degrees below scalding. It was perfect.

She nodded a word of thanks and tried to pull up the menu in AR.

The server just waved her off. “Haddock and curry chips to go, aye? You’re doon a jump in for Auggie, right?’

Slapped in the face, she could only stare at server. Augur was alive? “How …”

“Used to come in more oft and not. That’s the ganzie Alwyin made ’im. Y’won’t catch anyone else wearing that hackit jumper.” The dwarf smiled wolfishly, pointing at the knitted horses. “ So you’re the new lass he’s doggin’?”

Not Augur, Augustus Charles Ames. He ate here. He knew these people. They knew him.

Augustus Charles Ames was dead, too.

The grin fell from the dwarf’s face. “Lass? Yer lookin’ a bit peely wally.” Andrea dismissed his concern, feigning embarrassment—the soykaf giving her the warmth she didn’t feel inside.

Minutes passed. Customers came and went. “Order for Augustus.”

Andrea nodded absently and grabbed the bag, turning to leave. A shoulder check spun her into the door frame. The sharp pain touched raw anger in her, and she turned, irate.

Two pairs of piercing grey eyes stared back at her. One looked between her and the sweater, holding her in contempt. A sneer of blood-red lipstick was framed by elven features and wavy brown hair.

A younger set of eyes were maybe four years old. Clutching his mother’s leg protectively, he looked back at her behind curls of black hair and the cream-coffee skin of his dead father.

Andrea Schell ran.

_… honor his rest and ease his entrance; wash him with water and snow and hail, and cleanse him of sin as a white garment is cleansed of dirt …_

“It still hurts. Oh, God, why did it still hurt?”

She had left her body behind, clutching Augur’s host mainframe like the four-year-old child held his mother. She was now in the virtual space of the Matrix, the realm of the resonance, a realm she had held as one of thought and logic.

“Why does it still hurt in here,” she whimpered.

Andrea knew she shouldn’t be surprised. When your persona was composed of ones and zeros and Resonance, that stripped away many preconceptions, leaving you with what core of self you brought to the world. Death … well, death stripped away the rest.

In an empty room of four walls and a digitized wooden floor, Andrea broke. She sobbed on the virtual training room floor, too tired to scream and rage.

Curled into a ball on the floor, she willed the world away from her, trying to push her emotions elsewhere. The pain of her thoughts rolled into the Resonance. And from that pain …

… another Andrea Schell looked down at her, clad in the norwich shall and muslin dress from the London run. In her hand, she held a small familiar boutonniere, the same defensive complex form Shell had woven into Augur that same day over a week ago. “You were weak.”

Andrea bellowed ferally as she lunged forward, hoping to impale the simulacrum as the truth of her own words had impaled her.

The mirrored persona seemed to slide left without moving. “You should have been stronger.”

Hands clawing outward, Andrea seized Resonance, dragging the construct walls forward to crush her other self. She blinked once to clear virtual tears—and it was gone as the walls collided.



“IC wouldn’t blink or waver,” a voice whispered into her ear. “You should have been faster.”

Wheeling round, she recklessly weaved the Resonance, throwing Spike after Spike at her other self. The shots went wide, smashing holes into the nothingness beyond the training construct.

The simulacrum was untouched. “You should have saved him,” it said, crushing the boutonniere with finality.

A sound that came from Andrea’s throat had no words but spoke of grief and loss and emptiness and abandonment. And finally concession.

She tried to hold the Resonance Spike in her hand as her whole body flickered and faded from the effort, falling limp. “I couldn’t …” she wept. “I’m only human.”

O God, if he was a doer of good, then increase his good deeds, and if he was a wrongdoer, then overlook his bad deeds. O God, forgive him and give him the steadiness to say the right thing.

“You are dead. GOD has been alerted.” She blinked as the synthesized voice chimed.

It repeated. “You are dead. GOD has been alerted.” She mumbled a groan of pain and annoyance. The host acknowledged, breaking the pattern “Do you wish to start again, Shelly?” Augur had never bothered to reset the training program to her preferred moniker of Shell. He knew it annoyed her.

It seemed that she’d known the online man more than the offline version. The Augur she had known was direct and cocksure in his plans, but he nursed a well-hidden soft spot. Augur the monad had been direct as well; his plans were more thoughtful, underplaying the bravado. It was perhaps in that deeper thinking that she saw the softer side of the person that the program was aping. How much of that was the AI, how much was the vestiges of Augur the man, she would never know.

This training program in his private little host was more the real home than the wood-and-brick outside and blessedly nowhere near as damp.

Her whole persona ached, but it felt good in a cathartic car-crash sort of way. The painful memories were like drops of water on the inside of freshly cleaned glass.

She tapped the data packet that Auger had given her before he had ascended to the satellite. A ghost of the man materialized in the training host. “Host, reset to front room protocols. Authorization Augustus 22125. Reset administration protocols, full authorization and access to Andrea Schell … but don’t let her change the Shelly designation. Consider it a reminder on the importance of being human.” Either version of Augur could have recorded it, but that smugness was unmistakable. A tear slid down the side of the glass as the image of the executed protocol faded.

The host began to reconfigure to a digital workspace. Files and documentation confirming ownership flew past her eyes as the host reset. Data-locked drawers and virtual safes popped open revealing their contents. Trideos of newsnet feeds, the host continuing its previous standing order, gathering data for the London job.

Empty heads talked and interviewed corporate mouthpieces. The chyron underneath spoke of an Evo buyout of the old NeoNET Mayfair facility, granting the

megacorp a foothold in the British Isles. Andrea felt a healthy kindling of rage inside. Like vultures, Evo was going to pick clean the corpse of NeoNET’s handiwork, possibly salvaging the data, potentially restarting the experiments on e-ghosts and AIs—turning blood and suffering into nuyen.

She let that all-too-human anger feed her living persona. Shell raised her voice, “Host, administrator protocols. Catalogue query: What do you have in terms of databombs, viruses, or deployable cracking programs?”

She scrolled through the arsenal, dismissing file after file till an innocuous program caught her eye. The code had been sitting untouched for years. It had no descriptors attached to it. Just a name.

“Host, pull item 42113–Kechibi and isolate. Firewall to maximum. Display but do not activate. Prepare to cut Matrix connection on my command.”

Her fault and data sprites compiled behind her, falling in step as she walked through the glowing digital barrier to the program. Like Shell, they still carried London’s scars but were functional enough for the task at hand. The former hovered over her right shoulder protectively; the latter scanned methodically, examining the isolated program for traps of the trade—malware, trace programs, databombs …

Kechibi was aptly named, a glowing ball of low-burning witchfire shrouding a nebulous face. Deeper in the flame, several smaller pairs of eyes flitted about, searching for something with fervor. Looking closer, other indistinct faces-in-passing flickered, mouthing words just below the audible. Each request from the wordless mouths was rebuked by the firewall, popping like insects against a bug zapper.

She listened to those voices in the resonance. Data. It wanted market data. It wished to know. It was … a glass asking to be filled.

It had all the hallmarks of Horizon analytics programs that she’d worked with, feeding data to the Consensus. Why lock an analytics program in a weapons vault?

As she peeled back layers of complexity, her recognition and bafflement compounded. There were some clearly predictive elements to the program reminiscent of AI coding. Other areas looked organic, like they could have belonged to e-ghosts grafted into the overall architecture.

It wasn’t sentient. If it had been, monad Augur would have brought it with him to the uplink. But maybe it had been lobotomized. Either way, the program was intricate like the works of Gödel, Bach … or Frankenstein.

Shell ruminated on the final name, acknowledging the monster staring back at her.

Beyond the firewall, Shell could hear the trids lauding the upswing in Evo stock from the merger/acquisition.

Shell grinned an all-too-human grin as she lowered the firewall, letting Kechibi feed on market data. The witchlight flared brighter as a thousand little mouths drank deep of their queries. Looking into the fiery face of the program, Augur’s smile grinned back at her. “All right, my little monster, why don’t we teach them a lesson in loss?”
