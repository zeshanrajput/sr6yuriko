## **Bases and Hideouts**

**For twenty years, Bishop operated out of The Rook , the Ares Macrotechnology skyscraper in Philadelphia. Built by Silas Crow in 2038 from money he made through his investments in Fuchi, Corvid Tower was a throwback in a world of post-modern buildings—“only” 300 meters tall and featuring gothic stone facings with art deco designs, topped off by Crow putting his personal touch everywhere through the crow-themed gargoyles and glass etchings. While “The Crow Building” was a more common name used by the average citizens, “The Rookery” was a nickname used by the wealthier city patrons, a name that came into formal use after Crow’s death in 2047. Fuchi was the primary client, of course, and they continued to pay rent to the Crow family until the company’s stunning collapse in 2060. While Richard Villiers picked up much of Fuchi’s old assets in the feeding frenzy that followed Fuchi’s dissolution, Ares picked up the Rookery’s offce space, and is rumored to have picked up quite a bit of paydata in the process. With Knight Errant serving as building security, their chess-styled theme resulted in “The Rookery” being shortened to just “The Rook,” a name which caught on widely enough to be the norm. It might have served as the inspiration behind Bishop’s own name of choice.**

**> Crow’s family has magical connections, and the Rook itself was built with some odd astral effects. In particular, the normal lightning rod protections are augmented in some way, drawing more lightning than would ordinarily strike the tower, and this extra energy attracts electrical elementals during storms. After Crow’s death, overnight employees would report on spotting his ghost now and then, and a few other kindred spirits have moved in since. I’d love to dig through the blueprints, but they vanished a year after the building was completed, and there’s no record of what they looked like or where they went. There’re probably other secrets built into it, like what it’s using that extra energy for, but I’d need a year of deep study to even start unravelling it. Brilliant work!**

- **Clockwork**

**These days, Bishop splits his time between Phoenix Tower and Camelot , depending on if he’s on the clock or not (as much as a salaried CEO ever is). Phoenix Tower is the towering skyscraper from Ares Macrotechnology that was designed as the CAS HQ but now serves as the global HQ, with the original Ares HQ in Detroit’s loss. It’s a properly modern building, with all the glass and steel that you’d expect, a gleaming icon that isn’t the tallest building in the city (that honor goes the ridiculously tall Cord Mutual Finance building that Saeder-Krupp now owns), but is in the top ten and is by far the newest. Phoenix Tower is the showpiece, flled with datapushers, accountants, executives, and marketing personnel, all dedicated to taking Ares products and turning them into nuyen. All those men and women in proper suits, sitting at desks, taking meetings … brings a tear to my eye.**

- **Ecotope**

**Camelot’s the other half of the equation, the Ares private compound that’s outside of Atlanta proper, sprawling over several kilometers of campus. Living quarters, R&D buildings, cafeterias, medical facilities … if you unfolded an arcology and put the different foors side by side with some greenspace gaps, you’d have a good idea of what it looks like. By keeping the executives and business side in downtown Atlanta, they can corral the creatives into an environment more conducive to their needs, as well as keeping them protected without them getting upset over being confned like they tend to be in an arcology. Why you’d choose to waste your lunch break by lying down in the grass or playing with fying disks instead of powering down food in your offce while preparing for the quarterly reports, I’ll never know, but there you go. The living quarters start small for the normal workers, getting larger as pay increases, but even the smallest duplex is still two or three times as large as an apartment in the city.**

**Lose your job, you lose your home, of course, but the prices are low enough that even the janitors can afford to live in Camelot. The executive homes get progressively larger and nicer, with the largest being designed for the president of Ares CAS, now occupied by Bishop instead. Compared to the estates of Richard Villiers, Damien Knight, any random Shiawase family member, and so on, it’s not that impressive, but it’s far more than Bishop needs. He’s talked about moving to a smaller residence, but the board won’t allow it, as they think it’d be a hit to his prestige.**

**> There are three layers of security. The outer layer’s not that tight, as they get frequent visitors, deliveries, and prospective new hires. You just can’t lock things down when you need that many vehicles each day. There’s a massive parking lot for visitors (those who live in Camelot use shuttles, or glorified golf carts for execs), and a series of service roads that are out of sight of the general population for big trucks, with a third “golden road” for executive cars (and security!) to use. Individual buildings have their own light security, or stronger forces for R&D facilities, then a second wall that separates the work areas from the private residences. In that second ring, you can find schools, daycare facilities, shopping, and so on, that all close down by ten p.m. The entertainment businesses stay open until two a.m. and are grouped together in the outermost part of the ring; the homes nearest these tend to be filled by the lowestranking employees, since it’s noisy, but some like having a bar that you can stumble home from, so they’re popular, but not too popular. The third ring is also residential and is where the execs live. Security’s much tighter at that gate, but at the same time there isn’t the omnipresent camera coverage like in the general worker population. Having your every movement watched and recorded is fine for the peasants, but the nobles want their privacy. If you can get that far, you can get around fairly easily. Good for affairs, good for us.**
