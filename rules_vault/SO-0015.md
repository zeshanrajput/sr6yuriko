## Dealing with Organized Crime

One of the best ways to interact with criminal organizations is based on respect. With the Yakuza, for example, you’ll need to learn about _nemawashi_ . The basic idea here is to lay the groundwork to build a relationship on. In practice, this looks like meeting with the local oyabun and asking for permission to work in their territory, for which they may ask for a service in return or simply demand a payment.

- Showing respect to those who have earned it is only proper. This has led to many jobs for my team and myself. I do wish that other groups would follow the example set forward by the yakuza.

The Mafia has a similar sort of concept. Before operating in their territory, I call and ask for a meeting. In most cases, they don’t ask for specifics, but they’ll typically want assurances that their business interests won’t be disrupted. Occasionally, I have to deal with someone, typically a hotshot up-and-comer, who doesn’t know the unspoken rules. They want to know details, and this leads to awkward discussions about why I can’t tell them and why it’s better they don’t know.

- I’ve met a few freshly made types who don’t realize respect goes both ways. In most cases, there’s not a lot you can do, because going over their heads makes things worse, and not working with them eventually blows back on you. Fortunately for us, they also tend to burn out fast.

- 2XL

- It’s the family princes and princesses who almost always cause the most problems. Conversely, the ones who actually climb the ladder are some of the savviest and most well-connected people you’ll meet. Those are the ones you cultivate relationships with.

- **>** Juice Box

The rules for specific organizations and territories will vary, so you’ll need to learn them before accidentally making a mistake. I’ve known teams who never learned the rules go to pay their respects only to end up owing a bunch of unnecessary favors, or worse, become dead. Whoever you’re working with or for, remember that every group follows their own code of honor.

You’ll also need to determine if a territory is contested. Should you check in with one group, or both? There’s no one-size-fits-all answer for this, and if you’re new to the area, your legwork will determine the approach to use.

- In some neighborhoods, the territory changes regularly, so don’t get cocky. Check out the affiliation occasionally, because it’ll save you from an awkward situation at best, or a nasty situation with the boss at worst.

- A word of advice on the Vory: They’ve got zero problem leading with violence. It’s a bit of a stereotype, but one they’re happy with. Keep your guard up or you’re liable to end up dead.
