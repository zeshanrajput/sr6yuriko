## basic definitions

To keep things clear, let’s start with definitions of the terms used in this chapter. If there is doubt about exactly what rules apply to something printed in a previous book, match up the description with the one given here.

**Astral/Metaplanar \[Gateways, Portals, and Rifts\]:** Astral passageways (astral gateway, astral portal, or astral rift) cause forced projection for everyone within their area of effect. Metaplanar passageways allow for physical transference directly to a specific metaplane, the Astral Sea, or a physical transference entrance to (or exit from) the astral plane.

**Gateways:** Temporary passages that are created with ritual magic or by a critter power.

**Portals:** Permanent physical constructs that generate passageways similar to gateways.

**Rifts:** Naturally occurring phenomena that operate as passageways similar to gateways.

**Astral Form:** The astral body of someone (or something) projecting into the astral plane. The astral form of metahumans and spirits derives from their mental attributes. Spells and foci have astral forms that correspond to their power and type. The time that can be spent in an astral form is very limited; exceeding that limit usually results in death.

**Astral Plane:** Also known as astral space, the astral plane is a separate plane of existence, part of which overlaps the physical world, with the non-overlapping part containing the Dweller on the Threshold. “Outside” of the astral plane is the Astral Sea and the metaplanes. The metaplanes themselves also have versions of the astral plane that overlap the physical reality of the metaplane. These versions of the astral operate exactly at the astral plane overlapping the physical realm, though there is no metaplanar version of the Dweller on the Threshold—at least not one that anyone has ever encountered.

**Astral Projection:** This is the spellcaster’s ability to separate their astral form from their body and travel the astral plane. This ability in itself does not grant passage to the metaplanes, though astral quests can be undertaken to gain access.

**Astral Quest:** The original method of traveling to the metaplanes through astral projection. It requires crossing the barrier that is represented by the Dweller on the Threshold, then traveling to a metaplane where some predefined goal can be accomplished.

**Astral Sea:** The layer of reality outside of the closed structure of the astral plane. This is the seemingly boundless astral realm that contains and connects all metaplanes. Access to the Astral Sea can be achieved through the Dweller on the Threshold or by methods that take the traveler “off” the astral plane in a different direction than can be achieved with normal travel.

**Forced Projection:** A phenomenon similar to astral projection, forced projection is always caused by an outside force or agency and affects everyone in the vicinity. A mage capable of astral projection may still use forced projection to gain direct access to a metaplane or the Astral Sea, bypassing the necessity for an astral quest and confronting the Dweller.

**Metaplane:** These are separate realities connected by the Astral Sea. Some are the home planes of the various types of

spirits, while others are reflections of the physical plane, manifestations of a particular emotion, belief structure, or culture. Metaplanes also exist that are self-contained worlds with no apparent ties to our reality. There are several thousand known metaplanes generally ranked by a subjective measure of “distance.” In addition to intact metaplanes there are also:

- **Fractured Metaplanes:** An area of a metaplane that has a different reality from the rest of the plane due to the influence of a second intersecting metaplane. Fractured metaplanes may be isolated from both metaplanes, act as a bridge between the two intersecting metaplanes, or both at different times. The Seelie Court is a perfect example of a fractured metaplane as it exists somewhere between the Faerie Metaplane and the physical realm.

- **Hyper-Metaplanes:** Some metaplanes seem to be collections of loosely connected areas or realms within a single metaplane that are similar to each other but different enough to have a distinct identity. The Metaplane of Kin contains numerous well-documented examples of hyper-metaplanes, as it incorporates countless realms across its expanse, each one loosely based on an individual metahuman culture, society, heritage, religion, legend, or Jungian archetype.

- **Bubble Metaplanes:** Similar to hyper-metaplanes, these constructs are closely associated with astral quests. They may only be a construct of the astral quest itself and have no separate existence outside of the quest. Bubble metaplanes are tailored to the requirements of an individual quest, and the area visited cannot be returned to after the conclusion of a given quest, lending support to the idea they are temporary constructs.

**Metaplanar Distance:** Not all metaplanes are equally accessible, and the difficulty involved in traveling to a particular metaplane is typically described as distance. This relates to the number used in tests that involve traveling to the metaplanes; 0: astral plane, 2: adjacent (includes the Astral Sea), 4: near, 6: intermediate, 8: distant, 10: deep, and 12: very deep.

**Metaplanar Form:** Astrally projecting into a metaplane will cause the traveler to manifest a dual-natured reflection of their physical body, adapted to the requirements of the specific metaplane. Items with their own astral form, such as foci or spells carried into a metaplane, will also manifest a metaplanar form. Physical travel to a metaplane through a metaplanar passageway with physical transference moves the traveler’s actual body and does not create a metaplanar form.

**Metaplanar Translation:** The reality of a metaplane will make alterations to the physical form of any travelers who enter it by physical transference. The process, also known as attunement, is similar to the adaptations made to a metaplanar form when manifesting. The traveler’s body and gear are altered to conform to the reality paradigm enforced by the metaplane. The level of translation can run the spectrum from major physical adaptations to survive in extreme environments all the way to minor alteration in the traveler’s clothing so that it’s in line with the local fashions.



**Passageway:** A generic term used to describe any opening, in either the astral or physical realm, that leads to another metaplane.

**Physical Realm:** Also called the physical plane or the prime plane, this term is used to describe the mundane world. When compared to the metaplanes, it can mean the combined reality of the material world and the overlapping area of the astral plane, separate from the Astral Sea.

**Physical Transference:** Unlike astral projection, methods of travel that use physical transference take the body along with the mind. These methods transport the traveler physically to a metaplane, the Astral Sea, or

the Dweller on the Threshold. Passing the barrier creates a conduit to the metaplane—or perhaps to a bubble metaplane—where the astral quest occurs. Most passengers tagging along in this way do not encounter the Dweller and don’t notice the lost time. However, other initiated mages, even if they are just traveling along as helpers, will have to deal with the Dweller on the Threshold just like the primary quester, and they must pass its trial in order to continue to the metaplane.

So, what is the Dweller on the Threshold and why does every path to the metaplanes using astral projection lead through it? What the Dweller is does not have a simple answer, and each gamemaster will have to decide that for themselves. It may be a reflection of the mage’s own doubts and fears brought to the surface by the magic required to generate the astral quest, or perhaps it’s an actual entity guarding the gateway between our world and the Astral Sea, or maybe it is just how the metahuman subconscious deals with the barrier between the astral plane and the metaplanes. Whatever it is, the Dweller will test any mage seeking to access the metaplanes, forcing them to confront their flaws and shortcomings. If they pass the test, they pass on to the location of their specific quest. If they fail, they return to their body.

Why all paths lead to the Dweller is a simpler question to answer. Think of astral space that’s accessible to a projecting mage as a two-dimensional plane curved through three dimensions, forming a sphere. The physical plane overlaps the astral plane for most of this higher dimensional sphere, but on the opposite side sits the barrier represented by the Dweller. No matter what direction the traveling mage moves “away” from the physical, while staying on the astral plane, they will encounter the Dweller on the Threshold at the point furthest from the area of physical-astral overlap.

Outside of the sphere of astral space are the Astral Sea and the metaplanes. The barrier represented by the Dweller acts as a doorway, granting passage to the metaplanes. The desires of the traveler, or rather the idea of what they are seeking on their quest, augmented by power the Dweller repeven the astral plane. This type of travel requires another passageway capable of physical transference in order to make the return trip.

**Reality Paradigm:** The version of reality that exists within a particular metaplane. Anyone traveling to the metaplane will be altered to conform to this reality while on the metaplane by a process known as attunement or metaplanar translation. Sometimes this will be minor changes, like being able to talk in the local language. Other times, it will radically alter the physical form of the traveler, adapting them to a harsh environment. Attunement can be inhibited or even prevented with certain spells, artifacts, and rituals.

To use the two-dimensional plane curved into a three-dimensional sphere illustration again, an astral gateway, portal, or rift takes the traveler off the two-dimensional astral plane, breaking away at an angle other than the one the surface of the sphere follows.
