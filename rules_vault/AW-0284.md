## The Catacombs

It took some time to defeat the team of six golems at the foot of the stairs—and make no mistake, they were a team. The steel and silver golems not only worked as pairs, but the other two supported them well—the obsidian flitting between myself and Torch to try to hamper us, and then targeting Covenant and Exile a few times with lethal intent: geek the mage first sort of loses itself when everyone is a mage, but the two hanging back casting supporting spells were still considered priority targets.

The huge door, made of a material the color of wrought iron but much denser, took all four of us to open. What greeted us was a massively long corridor that went on for as far as we could see. It was big enough for four steel golems to walk abreast, as it was easily ten meters wide by eight meters high. After roughly fifty meters, we saw an intersection, with more of the huge corridors leading left and right.

Looking down the left and right passages, we could see more intersections every fifty meters or so as far as the eye could see. The pattern repeated itself if we’d continued straight, but on this passage we found doors in between each of the intersections. Large enough for a steel golem or two to use, they were nowhere near as large as the main door into what we dubbed the catacombs.

We continued exploring with our guard up and defenses ready. We went left for a few kilometers, and the corridor continued. We backtracked and did the same going right for a few kilometers, and

then straight on for another few. We must have passed hundreds of rooms, suggesting thousands, maybe even millions in the whole place. Each door looked identical.

After what felt like hours and hours of trudging back and forth, we decided to pick a door at random and open it. Yes, I know, not very scientific, but we at least tracked where we were in relation to the door out of the catacombs.

At least, we tried to open it, because as soon as one of us touched the door it dissolved into kind of a wall made of wood and metal, with a pair of eight-by-eight squares of white tiles that looked to be made of faded bone. Above the squares was the phrase “par decem” in dark letters, as if branded into the wood. Well, drek. Looks like we had a puzzle. We assumed that the words were in Latin, and thus translated to “match ten.” I wasn’t sure what we were supposed to match ten of, but Covenant quickly figured that we needed to match symbols on the tiles in each grid. Make ten pairs, and we’d be allowed through.

Standing there in that passage, the four of us trying to match ten pairs of symbols on those tiles, was like an exercise in pulling teeth. Not only were the symbols all very similar, but every time we got a wrong pair, we’d lose a pair we’d already matched. Thankfully, that made it a memory game, which played more to our strengths. But it still took a long time.

What we saw when we finally completed the puzzle and the wall faded from the doorway looked like a vault. It was a ten-meter-cube room made of the same stone as the keep, only these walls weren’t lime-washed. At the back of the vault was a raised ledge running the whole width of the room. On it was a scroll case made of leather dyed a rich, dark brown, almost mahogany. I recognized the case almost as soon as I saw it: These were the scroll cases that rewards from astral quests came in when you sought knowledge. The only difference was the scroll case was just sitting on the ledge as opposed to being presented in a nice wooden box in the reward chamber of a tower.

Another (thankful) difference made itself known when we opened the scroll case and looked at the faded parchment. Normal rewards, unless it’s a spell or ritual formula that takes time and energy to learn, appear in your mind as if you’d always known them. It’s a strange feeling to see something like the true name of a spirit suddenly appear in your memory just like you’d known it all your life. This didn’t happen in this instance—the scroll in question definitely looked like a true name and had a picture of a spirit (this one looked like a two-meter-long lizard with four legs and four arms). At the bottom of the scroll was the word “destrui” in big, thick red letters. We took that as meaning the spirit mentioned in the scroll had been destroyed.
