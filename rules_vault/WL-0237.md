Well, not exactly, but anyone who tries to prepare you for the des-** ert and only focuses on the heat of the midday sun is setting you up to die from exhaustion as you get no sleep in the cold nights. While it may not be freezing, or even cold by the standards of anyone who lives somewhere with a real winter, the temperature range is what truly does you in. As your body becomes partially accustomed to the fi fty degrees Celsius during the day, it’s tough to handle the forty-degree shift as it drops to ten throughout the night, before rising rapidly again once the sun breaches the horizon. Even though one truly wants to pack light while moving through the desert, you have to allow for necessities like water, a tent, and a thermal blanket.

Everything living in the desert is adapted to survive on less water and long stretches without any. There are small mammals that never drink water—they get it all through their food. Flora and fauna of the desert are few and far between, but when they are found in abundance, you can guarantee there is a good source of water nearby. The fl ora are hardy breeds, and many Awakened variety have become predatory in order to enhance their survival chances.

Focusing on the environment, deserts are defi ned by their lack of precipitation and limited access to water. When I talk about it here I’m focusing on the hot deserts—the Sahara, Gobi, Painted, and Atacama, just to name a few. There are arctic desert environments, but they are so different, they can’t be covered in the same section. These places get less than twenty-fi ve centimeters of precipitation each year. This precipitation quickly disappears into the earth and air as the sands suck it up, the hard pack runs it off, and the hot air evaporates it, sometimes just as quickly as it is dropped. Most rains in the deserts are storms. Small rains evaporate in the hot air before hitting the surface, so rain in the desert usually comes with an awesome light show from the lightning and a constant risk of fl ash fl oods as the water fl ows quickly over hard pack to gulches and gulleys.

Transiting the desert is a more complex process than one would think. Sand dunes, gulches created by fl ash fl oods, and a hard pack and rocky terrain leave you watching every step and making sure you have spare tires and maybe even a spare set of shocks for all the bumps your ride is going to take. Flying isn’t any better, as sand is lightweight and not good inside any form of engine.

Hire a guide. Stay frosty. And remember, everything in the desert is trying to survive at the expense of everything else.
