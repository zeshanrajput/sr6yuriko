## **Working For the Man**

**If you chose to make a deal with the prosecution in exchange for your freedom, congratulations, you’ve got a new run to carry out. Here are some examples of what that might look like.**

**Law enforcement corps might not care about justice, but they do care about sticking to the terms of their contracts—like the one that says their actions must be carried out in accordance with local laws. Those laws include things like rules about what evidence is and isn’t admissible in court and the ways that evidence has to be collected in order to be admissible (which is why laws about “unreasonable search and seizure” exist).**

**Every jurisdiction has different rules about that, but one thing that most of them have in common is that evidence that’s freely handed over to the police by a private citizen will be admitted to court, even if the private citizen broke the law to obtain it.**

- **Wait, what? What about all that “fruit of the poisonous tree” bulldrek you see in the trids?**

- **Sunshine**

- **That only applies to illegal searches by police officers. If a private citizen breaks the law to get evidence and gives it to the cops, the cops thank them for their help and then turn around and charge them with breaking whatever laws are relevant.**

- **> Hard Exit**

**One of the most common jobs a runner will get from the police is to “acquire” evidence that they can’t get legally. This lets them avoid any potential problems with whether a search warrant was legal, because as far as the law is concerned, that evidence was handed in by a private citizen. Teams who have shown they can work quietly and not leave any tracks are most likely to get offered this kind of run.**

**By the same token, police corps will sometimes hire runners to plant evidence that a particular suspect carried out a particular crime or destroy evidence that would support a suspect’s alibi. Outright fabricating evidence is less common; forensics experts from both sides of the case will have the opportunity to examine it, and most prosecutors don’t want to take the risk that the defense’s expert is better than their forger.**
