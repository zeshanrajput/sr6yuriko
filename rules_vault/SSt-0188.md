## Chemistry Is More Important than Skill ... to a Point

I’ll start with a point that’s likely to be controversial. Instinctively, when we’re gathering a team, we say “I want the best.” We want the best hackers, the best spellslingers, the best shooters. If we have the best skills on our team, the rest will take care of itself.



**==> picture [19 x 765] intentionally omitted \<==**

**That’s not really right. Or at least, it’s only kind of right. You want the best decker for your team, to be sure, but that doesn’t mean you want the person who is best at decking. You want the person who will best fll the role of decker on your team.**

**best option. They’re going to be confused and miserable for most of the run, and they’re going to clash with the rest of the team. It’ll bog the whole run down. Working with the culture of your team is hugely important.**

**You’ll note that I said at the outset that chemistry only trumps skill to a point. If you have a great friend you’ve known forever, a person you get along great with, and they’ve never picked up a gun, then there’s no way you want to make them your shooter. Even if they have some basic frearms experience, that’s probably not enough. There is a certain foor of experience and skill you need on your team, and if a person doesn’t have it, they shouldn’t be on the team, regardless of how much you like them.**

**That means the whole team sets the context for how the other members will operate. The best decker for that team is the one who functions best in that context, not the one who has the fastest time hacking into a host where megacorporate payroll is held.**

**Let me talk a little bit about how that context works. Some teams, especially if they have more than one former merc in their number, lean toward a more military-style operation. They’re brisk and effcient on the comms, follow a clear plan of operations, and frown on too much improvisation in the feld. If the situation on the ground changes and you have to change your actions accordingly, you damn well let others know what’s going on so they know how to adapt and are not taken by surprise. People in this type of unit** _**hate**_ **to be taken by surprise.**

- **One of the hazards of being part of a corporate team is you sometimes get someone pushed on you. Sometimes they’re a favorite of one of your superiors, or sometimes they’re selected for budgetary reasons. Either way, team chemistry is not a consideration, so you get some really odd fits.**

**Let’s also remember that while chemistry is good, sometimes too much chemistry is … well, too much. Look, we’ve all banged someone who was on our team. The nature of the job is that relationships within our team can be intense, and relationships outside the team can be non-existent, so when you feel that spark with someone on your team, sometimes you just go ahead and get it out of your system. And sometimes you leave it at that, but other times emotions get involved and things get complicated.**

- **Hard Exit**

**Other teams thrive on uncertainty and chaos. They fgure the best way to keep people from knowing their plan is by not having one. They do their legwork and make sure they know the basics of the situation they’re going into, but they know any run will throw unknowns at them, and they want to be able to respond to those unknowns smoothly without having to work through any sort of process. That means they aren’t always fully aware of what the other team members are up to, but these teams operate on a high degree of trust—they fgure each team member is working on their goal or at least accomplishing something useful, so as long as they do the same, things will work out okay. And since their whole approach is about improvising when responding to unknowns, it’s okay if their fellow runners are just a few more unknowns to react to.**

**Now, I’m not going to be one of those guys who says the job comes before anything else, even personal relationships (I was drifting in that direction for a while, but I’ve tried to course-correct). The relationships we have with others are important, and sometimes if you have to abort a run to save a life, that’s what you do. But a sexual connection with a teammate can go beyond that, and the relationship between the two people can eclipse other matters, including practicality. Just remember to stay professional—even if getting the job done isn’t the most overriding concern you have, it’s an important one, and you owe it to your teammates to fnd a way to get it done. If that means your signifcant other has to do some dirty work, or they don’t get their most favored assignment, you let it go for the sake of the job, and you don’t get obsessed with trying to white knight that shit or whatever.**

The point is to say that a decker who is suited to one of those teams would not be suited to work with the other.
