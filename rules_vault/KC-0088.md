## Chastity Dubaku

## (Owner, Lagos Daily Times)

Chastity Dubaku is the youngest sister of Faith and Hope Dubaku, both former council members. Faith sat in the hot seat in the early ’70s, and Hope took over in ’74 when Faith disappeared under mysterious circumstances. Chastity gained the seat just last year after Hope was found floating face down in Lagos Lagoon. A dwarf like her siblings, Chastity has grown hard after the loss of her sisters.

She is the latest owner of the largest newspaper in Lagos, which is loved by many because they still print on paper, and paper can come in handy at other times. Her family connections and their political importance gave her the chance to sit on the council, and she plays at acting as the corporate interests’ spokesperson like her sisters. The difference now is that she only plays at it, using her access to hunt for those who killed her sisters. The corps that support her are often at odds with each other, allowing her to play them against each other to hunt for clues.

Within the council she is a quiet force acting as a mediator more often than anything else, gaining power and influence in small increments, bringing everyone to agreement. She’s not troubled by Okoro’s efforts, because they only benefit her by making the corps focus attention on Okoro instead of on her investigation.

It should also be mentioned that the majority of Lagosians (more than 99 percent) live at what others would consider the poverty level. This means outsiders tend to look down on them or pity them, but to these Lagosians, the money they earn is not their livelihood. Their livelihood comes from their neighbors, friends, and community, as they exist in a perpetual barter system for common everyday needs. This reinforces the feeling that they don’t need the money from outsiders.

On a darker note, Lagosians often live in perpetual fear. They fear the Area Boys, the wrath of the gods, the hawalas, the local wildlife, the threat of invasion and war, their tribal rivals, and many other things. This fear does not make them cower and hide, but it makes them perpetually wary. They may not involve themselves directly with the suspicious activities they witness, but you can guarantee they are aware of it and have no problem spreading word of any problems to someone who could take advantage of it. They take care of their communities by quietly informing those with the power to make the changes they favor.

Magic runs through the city, with tribal _dibias_ (tribal sorcerers) and _olorishas_ (shamans) holding their societies together or profiting by their powers. Much of the city has ebbs and flows of mana, caused by centuries of metahuman suffering, misery, and overwhelming pollution. Dangerous Awakened creatures are often drawn to the tainted astral space and twisted by its nature. Local dibias learn to compensate for the difficulty of drawing mana, but visitors can be caught unaware.

The two largest tribes in Lagos are the Yoruba and Igbo. The Yoruba are the most populous tribe, and their kingdom actually surrounds the city. A significant portion of the Lagos oil pipeline runs through the Yoruba Kingdom. Their faith is the cornerstone of the tribe, and their olorishas lead them in that faith. Principal among the olorishas is the Oni, the spiritual leader and King of the Yoruba. Despite the kingdom’s immense natural resources and profitable exploitation of the oil pipeline, the average Yoruba exists at a subsistence level. Many rely on agriculture to survive in the rural areas of the kingdom. In Lagos, the Yorubas are found in every area of the sprawl and every tier of society. Yoruba gangs are more insular than the widespread Area Boys, though many Yoruba act as Area Boys rather than working in their own tribal gangs. Yoruba is one of the dominant languages of Lagos, and due to the Yoruba Kingdom’s strategic position in the area, it has been translated onto a linguasoft by Horizon, Wuxing, and other corporations. In general, the Yoruba are tolerant (if not welcoming) of various

metahumans and changelings, and women have mostly equal rights in the society except when it comes to magic (only the Yoruba men can receive formal training in their religion outside of a single secret society of priestesses).

The Igbo tribe was almost destroyed by VITAS and the Awakening. Some might argue it was destroyed completely; certainly, the tribe in the Sixth World bears little resemblance to the historical Igbo, who valued learning, democracy, and artistic expression. When the majority of their tribe died in the VITAS plague, a few remaining dibias opened the tribe up to any man who could pass their rituals. Survivors banded together during the years of chaos, merging into the Igbo of today. That version values strength of arms and physical prowess, and many residents of the feral city speak a little Igbo simply as self-defense. The ever-present Area Boys are filled with Igbo youth, and the gangs are a socially acceptable way for young Igbo men to get some experience and kills to their name before becoming a man in the eyes of the tribe. Throughout the Kingdoms of Nigeria, the Igbo are known as fierce and ruthless pirates and raiders, and their aggression keeps the Igbo at war with an ever-changing list of kingdoms. The Igbo welcome metahumans and changelings within their tribe, especially those who can prove their prowess; orks make up a disproportionate percentage of their tribe. Any man can join the Igbo if he can endure, unflinching, the dibias’ excruciating rituals. In some parts of the Igbo, women are considered property of their male relatives, with no rights in the tribe. Magic is reserved for men; Igbo girls who Awaken are often killed by their fathers (or sold to a corporate buyer). The Igbo are considered a minor tribe by most corporations, and their language has not been translated into a linguasoft form.
