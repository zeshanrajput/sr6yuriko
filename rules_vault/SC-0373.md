## GUN-FU street SAMURAI

## Gun-Fu Street Samurai

## Backgrounds

- Don’t think that because my preferred weapon simply shoots fire and lead that I am somehow weaker than other so-called samurai. Don’t think that because I embrace the current evolution of weaponry and combat tactics that my skill is less than those who came before me. Don’t think that because I can, and often do, strike from a distance that I am somehow cowardly. Don’t think that because my art is different from what has come before that I somehow am less of a warrior for embracing it. Don’t think that because my preferred weapon can be easily used by all that it is unworthy for a true warrior. And never for a moment doubt my resolve, for I will visit

- death on my enemies with just as much speed, precision, and brutality as those who still cling to the blade or only use their fists. When my death inevitably comes, my enemies will have to walk past scores of my victims and climb a mountain of spent brass and empty magazines to reach my corpse while the barrel of my weapon still glows red hot and smoke hangs heavy in the

air above me. This is the way of the gun, my path and my art. Disregard it and me at your peril.

You have been warned.

**>** Shockwave

Gun-fu street samurai are not simply experts in modern day firearms and gunfighting; they’re true artists who elevate gunplay into a deadly artform. They are the modern-day gunslingers, the dead-shots, the frontline warriors who wade into combat spewing fire and hot lead not with reckless abandon but with a deadly precision only a few truly dedicated souls can match.

While some warriors of the Sixth World continue to (stubbornly) embrace the paths and ways of the past above others, the gun-fu samurai are much more pragmatic when it comes to combat. While they see the value in more traditional paths and skills, they also recognize the threats of the modern era and the deficiencies many of the older ways have in the modern world. They acknowlGUNedge that fighting styles that favor blades or even fists have their value in certain circumstances. But most of the time, an enemy is more likely to rain dozens, if not hundreds (or even _thousands_ ) of armor-piercing or explosive rounds at their targets from distance. What good do blades or fists do when a warrior can’t close with an opponent? It is always better to fight, and defeat, an opponent using the most efficient weapons available (namely, firearms) at the highest skill level possible. To do anything less is a waste of effort at best and an eventual death sentence at worst.

This is the mindset and core of the gun-fu street samurai.

Like other street samurai or other shadowrunners, gun-fu street samurai come from myriad backgrounds. Most commonly, they were professionals before they entered the shadows. This is where the future gun-fu street samurai may have received extensive or advanced firearms training. Former mercenaries/soldiers and corporate security also fit the bill. It’s not also unheard of for gun-fu samurai to rise from the lowest part of the streets, finding, earning, stealing, or scrounging up enough nuyen to get their first firearm and making the most out of it because their very survival depended on it, learning their skills by trial and error but being lucky enough to survive. They also may have been lucky enough to find a teacher to instruct them in the finer points of gunfighting. Or maybe they simply had a love or knack for firearms, and when they were forced (or chose) to enter the shadows, they put their skills and knowledge to good use. No matter what their background, at some point the gun-fu street samurai got their hands on a firearm and simply knew that this was for them, much in the same way a master of the blade or a martial artist realized their calling.

The way of the gun doesn’t favor any specific metatype, although some races have slight disadvantages. Modern firearms and weapon manufacturers for example seem to favor the “average-sized” metatypes such as humans, elves, and orks in terms of weapon size; these races have little difficulty with finding firearms to fit their hands. Dwarfs and trolls, however, face some challenges.

With their smaller stature, most dwarfs tend to prefer smaller weapons such as pistols and SMGs—that is, unless they decide to rock out with bi- or tripods to employ larger or heavier weapons. It’s also not uncommon for dwarven gun-fu samurai to max out their strength via augmentations specifically to handle larger weaponry.

The metatype that until recently was extremely stifled when it came to firearms was trolls. Because of their stature, many were relegated to melee specialists because most firearms simply would not fit in their large hands without extensive modifications. Even things as simple as reloading could be difficult. With modern advances such as regulator

gauntlets that allow certain firearms to be worn on the forearms, and companies such as Krime making metatype-specific weapons readily available, troll gun-fu samurai have started to come into their own in recent years.

While many modern-day warriors and professionals make extensive use of firearms, it takes a special kind of dedication to follow the way of the gun. Much like martial artists or masters of the blade, gun-fu samurai eventually find a drive or passion that pushes them past the idea of merely being competent with firearms; they want to be exemplary masters, both feared and respected for their skills. Simply put, the gun-fu street samurai seek to elevate their skills to such a high degree that their fighting style becomes an artform.

Which is easier said than done. While anyone can theoretically pick up a gun and use it, it takes a lot of factors to make a shooter even mildly competent—despite popular opinion. Even with modern enhancements and targeting systems, a shooter’s grip, stance, and trigger pull (for those who go old school) are still determining factors as to whether the rounds find their target. Most think that all you have to do is point and shoot, and the gun will deliver instant death with very little effort. That it takes less skill to use a firearm than, say, a melee weapon. But that’s a completely unfair comparison. This persistent prevailing (and pompous) opinion continues to annoy shooters in general but is especially irritating to gun-fu samurai who (rightfully) have a bit of a chip on their shoulders about it. One of the fastest ways to insult a gun-fu samurai is to say, anywhere in their vicinity, “it’s just a gun.”

For the gun-fu street samurai, their gun isn’t simply another weapon in their arsenal—it’s an extension of their bodies and executor of their will. So, it’s no surprise that gun-fu street samurai tend to treat their firearms with a certain kind of reverence that borders on (or gleefully crosses into) obsession. Above all else—maybe even above their own personal needs—a gun-fu street samurai’s weapons will always be fully functional, meticulously cleaned, calibrated, and ready for use at a moment’s notice. Because they know that something as simple as a magazine ejector that sticks and takes 0.001 seconds longer than normal to reload could mean the difference between life and death. A gun-fu, or any other street sam for that matter, would never let their weaponry fall into disrepair. They will, however, allow a few scuffs and scratches, because weapons, like people, should proudly display battle scars. And should one become lost or damaged beyond salvage, don’t be surprised if a gun-fu street samurai mourns the loss as much as a loved one or friend. Also, don’t be surprised if a gun-fu samurai treats and cares for their guns better than they do themselves.

To help keep their edge, they will spend obscene amounts of nuyen, second only to their augmentations, to buy the latest and greatest accessories, upgrades, and ammunition types. Anything that can eke out just a bit more performance is sought by the gun-fu samurai like it was the Holy Grail. This also includes modifications, because gun-fu street samurai know that no stock firearm is perfect—there is always room for improvement and customization. It’s also common for gun-fu samurai to ornately decorate their arsenal to reflect their personalities. After all, what’s the point of having all this skill and firepower if you can’t flaunt it?

What will slot off a gun-fu samurai off is someone who does not take the time or effort to properly train with and maintain their own firearms, or worse pass themselves off as knowing more about the subject than they really do. Mostly it’s a mild irritation with the person in question, until it becomes something that may jeopardize the gun-fu street samurai’s life. They will not hesitate to call out members of their team who don’t take their firearms preparedness seriously. Conversely, they’ll also be quick to help those who wish to improve their skills.

Because of their intense focus on their art, it’s not uncommon for gun-fu street samurai to spend most of their down time working on their arsenals or training with them. Gun-fu samurai are not so arrogant as to think that a slick gun coupled with state-of-the-art augmentations is all it takes. On the contrary, they know that even though the muscle may be artificial, it still must have the necessary memory to work properly in conjunction with the rest of the body, mind, and will. Skill is like life itself—it has an expiration date. If not used and improved on, skill will deteriorate, rendering it useless.

So, when not actively on a run or fending off a bunch of ganger punks when all they wanted was a quick soydog from the Stuffer Shack, the gun-fu samurai is constantly training, working to perfect their skills. And when they’re not training, they’re religiously researching the latest in tech and tactics (even if they’re garbage) if for no other reason than to know what an enemy may use against them. This kind of dedication to their art makes the gun-fu street samurai a bit one-noted in the social department at times. But then, it’s not their job to be social or even liked. Their job is to be the muscle and dish out death whenever the situation calls for it.

And like other samurai, they are (or should be) professionals with a strong sense of honor, living by a code that dictates their actions and separates them from soulless killing machines and the real

monsters of the Sixth World. Killing may be part of the job, but it’s not the only part. Without a code, the gun-fu samurai is nothing more than a weapon, a tool to be used as the unworthy see fit. In this, they share a sort of kinship and camaraderie with other street samurai.

But in keeping with their pragmatic nature, the gun-fu doesn’t feel the need to waste time worrying about what methods of combat are better. In their minds, the best ways are the ones that get the job done first in the most effective way possible. Even though they may be looked down on for their choice in methodology, the gun-fu street samurai shrugs it off, letting their actions, and victories, speak for themselves.

Despite their obsessive tendencies toward their arsenals, gun-fu street samurai also know that skill and dedication to their art are just parts of the overall equation. As much as they sacrifice their time to perfect their skill—and their nuyen to improve their arsenal—they also spend their bodies on augmentations. Knowing they must defeat threats such as massive trolls, insanely fast adepts, and magicians able to tap into the forces of nature, the gun-fu street samurai knows they need any advantage they can get to both deliver and take the pain.

Faced with these sobering realities, the gun-fu street samurai know that they’ll need to find the right balance between skill, body, and augmentations in order to survive and thrive in the shadows. To play to their strengths, gun-fu street samurai will typically focus on increasing their speed and agility first; allowing them to (hopefully) get off the first shot and end a threat before it does any real damage. To take full advantage of modern targeting tech and software for their arsenals, any gun-fu samurai worth their ammo will also opt to have smartlinks installed, if they haven’t already incorporated them into their new cybereyes. Optic enhancers such as vision magnification and rangefinders will make linking up with their weapons easier than ever.

Cyberlimbs, specifically cyberarms, also offer the gun-fu street samurai plenty of opportunities and flexibility in tactical situations. Hidden compartments are great for smuggling small weapons or a few more clips of ammo, while cyber-implanted guns ensure that the gun-fu street samurai is always armed.

Since these samurai typically act as the frontline offense on a shadowrun team, they need a good set of armor to boost their survival odds. Implanted armor, while having its drawbacks, provides a few more layers between enemy attacks and their vital organs. Additionally, if the gun-fu street samurai prefers heavier weapons such as MMGs, HMGs, or assault cannons, then they’ll need the ability to lift and wield them with ease. Factoring in the un-ghostly amounts of ammo they’ll need, itbecomes clear that augmented strength is never a bad call.

But what exactly is the right arsenal for a gunfu street samurai? That depends on the individual samurai’s preferences. Like different martial arts that focus more on specific strikes/attacks, gunfu samurai also tend to have preferences in the firearms they employ. For example, some prefer two-handed styles often using lighter weapons such as pistols, machine pistols, or SMGs, relying on their speed and accuracy to get the job done. This is often what most think of when someone hears the term “gun-fu,” a lightning-fast, dual-wielding gunslinger who dances in and out of combat with moves that look like they were directly ripped off from martial arts sims. But not all styles have to be this flashy. Gun-fu is just as valid with an assault rifle or combat shotgun—sweeping in quickly after deploying a flash-bang or other grenade to eliminate the threats, using a melee-hardened weapon or buttstock to deflect any hand-to-hand or melee attacks. There’s also the sharpshooter who uses their trusty long gun or sniper rifle to provide long-distance fire to cover the team’s movement. And don’t forget the heavy weapons specialist who uses their machine guns or assault cannons to cut a swath of destruction as they lay down suppressive fire. And there’s the generalist, who seems to have a gun for every range and every occasion and knows exactly where to hide them when the situation calls for it.

It doesn’t matter what their style or their choice of loadout is, for the gun-fu samurai, firearms are their kung-fu, and it is _strong_ .
