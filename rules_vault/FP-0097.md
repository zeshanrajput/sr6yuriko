## **The Denver Data Haven**

## **a.k.a. the Nexus**

**The architecture of the Denver Data Haven, also known as the Nexus, uses a number of security features common in other illegal hosts, primarily because many of these were originally created for the Nexus. First, as with all Shadow Matrix hosts, there is no direct connection to a legitimate grid. Instead, the Denver Data Haven uses a system of concealed gates that effectively make the Nexus a nested host hidden inside another low-rated and nondescript host. These may be simple shells created as a temporary front or a hacked connection to something like a Stuffer Shack. Either way, the entrance gates are changed frequently by the Nexus admins, and simply knowing where the current access point is becomes the frst layer of security. Of course, there are also a number of connections from other hosts inside the Shadow Matrix, but these are just as diffcult to locate and use.**

**Once past the killing jar, the sculpting of the Denver Data Haven can be seen. This truly impressive host takes the form of a massive black hole at the center of a vast, whirling accretion disk of glowing gas and arcing energy called the Ring. Hundreds of planets, moons, and asteroids race through the gas clouds and sheet lightning, all of it rendered with an ultraviolet clarity that borders on a BTL experience. The decades of archived data fles, known as the Heap, are represented by the ever-growing black hole, while the orbiting planetary bodies are used as the meeting rooms, chat boards, Matrix clubs, offces, storefronts, and auction houses of the Nexus. The actual structure of the Denver Data Haven is a massive system of nested hosts that can be accessed from the primary host containing the Heap and the Ring. Each planet, moon, and asteroid contains a gate to a nested host, while the central black hole leads to the dozens of separate hosts required to contain the Heap’s vast amount of data. While currently built on a Foundation host, the iconography of the Nexus has remained consistent through all the various iterations of the Matrix, an impressive feat for pre-Foundation technology.**

**The Nexus is literally and fguratively built around the Heap. Its hosts contain decades of accumulated data from all over the Matrix, much of it now existing nowhere else. However, fnding what you are looking for can be a challenge, as there is no formal organization to the data storage. Most packets are simply archived as they come in, while others stored there may stay hidden until triggered by an event—or lack of an event—and are dumped out into the Heap. The more current and valuable paydata is usually offered for sale in the data auction houses, with anything unsold ending up in the Heap.**

**> The same goes for unsold paydata from other data havens too. At Asgard we don’t keep anything after it’s out of date. If it doesn’t sell right away, we send it down to the Heap in the Nexus to be archived there.**

**An assortment of services is offered inside the Denver Data Haven. These start with simple access to the Heap for in-person data mining or for having your agent search it. The Nexus will also rent out agents for data searches and can broker deals with staff or other clients to carry out searches. Keyword search services are also available to scan incoming data, with alerts sent out for matches. Other services are secure messaging that includes one-way blind addressing and dead-drops, pirated news and entertainment feeds, secure fle storage (with or without orders to be released upon death or disappearance), access to discussion boards, and plenty of social settings for making contacts for other illegal services.**

**All regular users have a Nexus reputation score, set by the other users and attached to their icon. Offering up choice paydata to the Heap is the best way to increase a reputation score—the better the info, the better the increase. Other things like favors for the admins or other members can also add to a rep score. Downloads of data from the Heap and Nexus services can either be paid for with cash or a reduction in reputation, making the rep score a currency in the overall barter system,**
