## **Patrol**

**The core of any police force, the structure of any specifc organization’s Patrol Division will depend on population density, terrain, and the size of the geographical area covered. Patrol is usually divided up into districts and precincts with offcers operating solo, in pairs, or other numbers within an assigned precinct. Just like every other law enforcement resource, there’s a limited number of patrol units available at any given time. It’s just a fact of the modern world that some areas will receive better police coverage than others. The wealthy and affuent neighborhoods are usually saturated with patrols that can respond to a call**

**There’s one thing you need to keep in mind about patrol units. The current trend is for members of tactical and specialty divisions to serve patrol rotations while waiting for something serious to happen. That means the armored patrol cruiser pulling up behind you may just be a couple of bored SWAT offcers looking for some action.**

**> Or a pair of PK officers with a pack of hellhounds in the back of their van. Ask me how I know. > Stone**

**Street Patrol is the most common type of patrol unit and may be on foot, in a vehicle, or even mounted. These offcers are the ones out patrolling a beat, on the lookout for crimes being committed. Street patrols are the ones who dispatch calls**

**When most folks think of patrol, they think of a couple badges rolling up in a cruiser, with backup in tow, and a tactical squad on standby if things get nasty. Joe Schmo calls for emergency services and the uniforms arrive to assess and promptly deal with the situation by whatever “book” they adhere to. This is the reality in which most people live, whether they call a city or a corp for their cops. That is, unless you live in a territory contracting Eagle Security for their services.**

**If you live in a rural zone patrolled by Eagle, you know a very diferent reality. You usually get one Eagle badge at your door, and that’s only if something spicy happened. They roll up in an unmarked Toyota Gopher, armed to the teeth and wearing what looks like rancher gear. Said Eagle won’t be too interested in taking a report; they’ve got a huge area to cover and a backlog of calls that probably reads like an encyclopedia, and they won’t much care to make an arrest unless it’s absolutely necessary. You can get indignant and try to ask for their supervisor, maybe someone with chevrons on their shoulder, but the existence of such an individual is pure fantasy. If you’re a fortunate soul, you recognize this Eagle, which means they are competent or lucky, probably both. They haven’t been killed of yet, and as such they might be able to actually address your concerns with more than just a hand wave. More than likely, though, you’re going to get a green badge, a fresh-faced rookie who got placed on a beat after his predecessor was killed in a gunfght. So good luck with that.**

**If you’re an old soul, your ears will be ringing as images of old-world Western movies play across your mind where the town is protected by a sherif and maybe a deputy. And that’s about it. That’s what you’re getting with Eagle Security, and you better hope the Eagle is as much of a badass as that movie sherif, because if they ain’t, there’s nobody else coming.**

**—Anonymous Cheyenne Resident**

**If you live in North America, Lone Star Security Services is synonymous with the concept of law enforcement. When most people think of cops blaring down a street, lighting up the concrete with their red and blues, they’re thinking of a bomber jacket emblazoned with a star.**

**Lone Star patrols are usually well stafed, well organized, and well equipped. Roll call and briefng, then to armory, and then out to the streets and hopefully back at the end of the shift. That’s the day-to-day of a Lone Star cop. Your average resident living in a sector patrolled by LSSS can typically expect semi-reasonable response times and relatively competent badges.**

**The company’s love afair with pretending to be modern-day Texas Rangers, or rather their idea of what they were, leads to some heavy-handed tactics. This is where Stars have gotten the most heat. In fact, it’s so bad that if you hear someone talking about excessive force as it pertains to a Lone Star badge, that beating was probably as near to a war crime as you can get outside of a warzone. Because a simple thumping is par for the course if you live in a Lone Star-contracted area.**

**How do they get away with it? I bet you’re asking yourself, how do people let these badges beat some shoplifter to a pulp on the sidewalk? Well, you have to remember who most Stars are going up against. People who live in these high-crime areas will happily look the other way if it means they won’t get robbed on their way to work or be the victim of some perverted killer. Hell, most people like to see Lone Star badges tuning up those types.**

**“Good old-fashioned policing” is how a Lone Star source of mine once described their patrol division. For better or for worse, he’s right.**
