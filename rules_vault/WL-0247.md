## **(Echinocactus boomboni)**

**Murderous fora are on the rise. In a place like the desert, where life is precious and survival teeters on a razor’s edge, every advantage or variation counts. In the case of the boom cactus, they use something already abundant in the desert: heat. Through cells adapted to have extra-thick cell walls, this cactus species, Awakened from the golden barrel cactus, builds up pressure behind extra-dense needles through the heat of the day. Additional pressure is built up in a central chamber that releases small bursts when it senses movement nearby or on a region of the plant. This sends a burst of needles containing a paralytic toxin that immobilizes fauna, leaving them to either die in the sun or land close enough for the cactus’ shallow root system to reach them and begin to feed.**

**I went out to study the chimera, looking into its ability to hide. I just wasn’t settled on the studies done by some of my peers. I came to the same conclusions in the end, but I also picked up a great story about how a chimera almost killed me. As with several other desert lizards, I didn’t have the best experience studying with these 3.5-meter-long beasts. For those unsure which determination it was, I have settled into the concealment school of thought, as their ability to hide went beyond adaptive coloration. They do not just change color but instead wrap themselves in concealing mana that hides them from all perception, not just the simple vision.**

**The beast I studied was a classic specimen. Its brown hide was bland, but its neck ruff had strong fares of red. While it was smaller than other subjects I’ve studied, its horns were slightly larger and more pronounced. As with other subjects, it was fast and powerful, even with its bulk. The speed of these creatures is amazing—when combined with their ability to conceal themselves, their apex predator status is clear. While their usual diet is focused on small mammals and snakes, they can easily take down larger prey. The chimera is immune to most toxins and has little to no reaction after ingesting any form of poisonous or venomous meal.**

**As an Awakened cactus, boom cacti are not hard to differentiate from their mundane kin. Their green is far darker, and the sections they produce are far more abundant. Up close, the root system is easily spotted but also adds a dangerous angle of attack, as the roots possess needles as well, though they are less abundant.**

**This additional threat was a discovery I made while studying this species. I thought I had myself well protected with a thick modifed SWAT shield, so I moved in close and was checking out the needle density and spread pattern with a foam pad on the shield, when all of a sudden, I was hit in the hoop with a couple needles. My legs went numb, but luckily my magic does not require me to move much. Levitated my way to safety (as usual) but then had to wait to regain feeling before I could drive out in my trusty hamster ball.**

**Chimeras have a rather well-adapted social behavior for this. When encountering any larger individual, the chimera puts on a convincing threat display. This intimidation sets up their next move. If the creature stands their ground, the chimera will usually back off. If they run, the chase is on, and as mentioned earlier, the chimera is fast. To clear this up now, yes, I held my ground, but I don’t think it cared about little old me. Their aversion to scuffing with larger creatures does not include their fellow chimera during mating season. Threat displays are skipped, and males fght each other for dominance. The victor will impregnate the females and then chase them off violently. During this time, don’t expect a threat display. Expect to just get attacked. While the males chasing off the females is harsh, the females are not any more pleasant. They fnd a nice spot to lay their clutch, bury the eggs in dirt, sand, or stones, and then leave them. These egg nests stink, and even my basic olfactory senses were able to locate them by smell.**
