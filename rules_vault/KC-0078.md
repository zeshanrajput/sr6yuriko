## SUDDEN FORTUNE

## The Rewards of Virtue

## Posted by: Cayman

**>** I spent some time in Lagos a while back, and while the run was a rough one, I developed a weird affection for the city, partially due to some of the people I met there. I heard from one of them recently about some interesting developments in town, and it seemed like the sort of thing everyone here should know about, so I’m posting it here.

Note that I specifically asked my friend to be focused and to avoid digressions. For her, she did a pretty good job.

**>** Cayman

Listen to me! I have a story, and while I am not the only one who knows it, I am the only one who can tell it the way I will tell it, which is the right way, because it does not confuse simple facts and chronology with the truth. Those things are part of

the truth, but I will give you more. I will give you the _essence_ .

Lagos is my city, and it has seen _everything_ . Great wealth and extreme poverty. Independence and colonization. War and peace. Feast and famine. It has seen it all, and it has endured. It is not glamorous. It is not romantic. My friend Cayman, when he talks to me sometimes, he hears me complain about the misery, the pollution, the poverty, the death, and he says, “Why don’t you leave? Live somewhere better?” And I tell him that this is what life _is_ . The struggle. The challenge. The small triumphs and the many defeats. The fight for the people you call your own. If I leave my city, I leave my people. Then I could not live with even myself.

But I haven’t started the story. This is not what I was asked to do. I was asked for a story.

The story is Eniola Okoro.

To talk about Eniola Okoro, I have to talk about laws. We have laws to forestall chaos. We have laws because when every car thinks they have a green light, then everyone goes, then they crash,

THEand then no one goes. A single corp truck driver might wish they could drive as fast as they like, never stopping, and that would make them faster and more efficient. But if every driver does that, then they crash, and everyone is _slower_ and _less_ efficient. So the corporations talk to each other and agree that they will set certain limits, because that is better for business overall. Sometimes, that is just about a benefit to the corporations, but sometimes a benefit sneaks down to us all.

So then we look at our lagoon. Everyone would like to pour things into it. Whatever thing they do not want. Sewage, byproducts, waste, or whatever term you would like to use for a liquid no one wants (solids no one wants sometimes go in there as well, but they do not flow in as easily and thus must be pushed). We treat the water to remove some of these impurities, but they can be stubborn. There are cyanides that do not respond to our normal treatment, and if they are allowed to build up in the water, then people’s thyroids do not function properly, or their coordination suffers, or if things are truly bad then they die.

This is a traffic accident that locks up the whole system. People die, or they worry about using water, and even those at the top may not be safe, because they may drink only the finest bottled water, but will they cook with it? And make sure all restaurants cook with it? And bathe in it? And so on. Not having cyanide dumped into the rivers that provide our water is a public good that is also good for them, so they make laws about it. The intention may be to preserve a society in which profits can be made, but we still realize a benefit.

But even with the laws in place, there are still occasional drivers who think that they can exploit the structure, that they can be the ones who move quickly through the traffic while others obey the law. They will gain the benefit just for themselves. When they do this, who will stop them?

Many times, the answer is “no one.” The corps want the laws, but they also want to be able to bend or break the laws needed, and they do not want the effort and hassle of having to watch each other. So the laws exist but are not enforced, which is very much the same as not having the law.

And again I return to Eniola Okoro, because I keep meaning to talk about her and then do not.

Okoro works to enforce the laws. She cannot do it on her own. She is an attorney, not the government or a corporation. But she finds who is breaking the law and then she tells it to the courts. She issues lawsuit after lawsuit in an effort to compel the corporations to follow their own laws, to be accountable in the ways they constantly demand of their employees.

It is a hard fight. They are structures of iron, and for most of her time, she has had only a wooden ax with which to chop. She won a case here and there, and she found some donors who kept

her firm functional if not prosperous. But the corporations have lawyers that swarm like locusts, while she had nothing besides her will and the will of allies who would give to her fight. She fought greed with goodwill, and that is a war that has been fought many times in history, and her side has so seldom come out on top that we make legends about it when it does.

She established a pattern of chopping and chopping, and the corporations established a pattern of pretending not to know who she was while attempting to wear her down, when all of a sudden they turned around and she no longer had a wooden ax but an acetylene torch, and she had an army behind her. Where did the army come from? Did she drop dragon teeth into a field and have them sprout? If so, where did she obtain the teeth?

Eniola Okoro has much more power than she used to, and in the world we live in that usually means money. Many, many people want to know where the money came from and who directed it her way. The answers will direct a high amount of energy around the city.
