## **Franz Baader, Director of Visions**

**If you’re going to have an organization of seers, you’re going to have some weird titles, and Baader has the highest-ranking weird title in the organization. He is tasked with the job of herding all the seers connected to Convergence and trying to make their visions add up to one cohesive whole, which is exactly as diffcult as it sounds. It’s not just that the visions are often mysterious and fragmentary—it’s also that often, seers don’t** _**want**_ **their visions to ft with other visions.**

**seers are more arrogant than average, since the ability to glimpse the future gives them a certain swagger, especially when they have a piece of information they know someone else would consider very valuable. Each seer thinks they are particularly good at what they do, which means that combining their visions with those of other seers would lessen, even pollute, their strong, clear revelations. So while the offcial policy of Convergence is to get fully detailed write-ups of each seer’s vision when they occur, plenty of seers have been known to withhold information in these interviews or even avoid them entirely.**

**These are some of the complications Baader has to deal with. It’s hard enough to ft disparate divinations together, but it becomes extra challenging when you’re trying to sort the genuine information from the bulldrek. Then there’s the extra complication of managing people who don’t like each other.**

**So how does Baader manage all this? Subtly. He knows that you don’t deal with arrogant people by challenging them head-on—you do it carefully, friendly, and in a way that makes them think they’re just following their ideas. He makes people on his staff feel like it’s a good idea to confde in him and that their work will improve when they do so. He has to lay out a lot of fattery and bulldrek to do his job, but he puts the organizational mission over his pride, and that helps him get the job done.**

- **Some people see Baader’s mild manners and think that’s all there is to him, but don’t be fooled. This man has an inventive mind with a superlative gift for making connections, which is exactly what his position demands. Consider this: One of his seers had a vision of a ghost light hovering in a cemetery on the banks of a river, and another had a vision of a calamitous stock market crash. In the latter vision, at the end of the trading day, lights of the trading floor abruptly flickered off, leaving the defeated traders in the dark—except for one light high above the floor that emitted a soft glow for maybe fifteen seconds after the others went out. Baader not only connected the two visions together based on the light, but he identified the location of the first vision as Japan’s Kochi Prefecture. That allowed him to identify the ghost light as a spirit known as kechibi. He submitted a memo to Brown saying that kechibis would be involved in stock-market fluctuations in the near future. This was half a year before anyone heard of the Kechibi code.**
