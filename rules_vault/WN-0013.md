## Escaping to New York Passport to the Big Apple

New York wants visitors, but she wants the right visitors. The city relies on a pass-based system that is tied directly to NYCNet, the city’s isolated Matrix grid. In 2075, the MDC, backed by the mayor and the city council, voted to update the longstanding—and long-corrupted—entry card system. The new system, dubbed CityPass, was designed as a joint venture between Horizon and Renraku. Your free CityPass comes as a stylish, wallet-sized metallic card with bank-linking features and security systems similar to a credstick. Unlike a credstick, however, the identifying color strip cannot be scraped off. Here, your pass is your status. It is a reflection of your income level and the number and types of privileges afforded to you within the city. The more you make, the more access, credit, and prestige your pass offers. If you don’t have the right pass, some places just won’t let you in. Your average New Yorker walks around with a Copper-level CityPass. Want a higher rating? Add a zero to your income level. At the top end is Rhodium, reserved for the billionaires looking down their noses from the c-suites.

Beyond its “show me” value, your CityPass is hardwired to your biometric identity and has Matrix and AR credentials tied to your digital profile, broadcasting your information to all receiving scanners. A good hacker can shut these features off, but the byproduct of those actions means you will be treated as though you don’t have a pass at all.

You can’t get into the city without a CityPass. These virtual ID codes are built off the same principles as a SIN and effectively represent who you are within the boundaries of the island. The concept is promoted heavily by the Manhattan Transit Authority (MTA) tourism bureau’s motto, “Who you are here is who you are.”

CityPass is issued free of charge by the MTA, the group officially responsible for monitoring who moves in and out of the city. The MTA is separated into four main divisions: Tourist, Commuter, Student, and Resident.

Tourist passes are the most common and the easiest to counterfeit, and they remain valid for a pre-specified length of time. Commuter and Student passes are based on employment and enrollment, respectively. Resident passes are linked to your local address. So long as MTA’s records indicate you work, go to school, or maintain a home in the city, you are entitled to keep your pass.

Before the MTA issues a pass, they perform a criminal background check on your SIN. Officially, no one with a criminal record is allowed in the city. Unofficially, any of the MDC member corporations have the right to override that rule and force the MTA to issue a pass to whomever they want.

Non-corporate citizens looking to apply for a pass must appear at one of the MTA points of entry or make an appointment with the UCAS customs bureau. Alternatively, any MDC corporation has virtual offices that can process your MTA request and have a card ready for you when you arrive.
