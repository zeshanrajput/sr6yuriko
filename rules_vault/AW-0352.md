## Tandoran Civilization

The Tandorans speak their own language, which makes use of their unique physiology and is very difficult for non-Tandorans to pronounce. They also speak and understand Japanese, or rather a locally developed dialect of Japanese. Japanese characters are used for writing, though many Tandorans can only read and write a handful of kanji characters that are commonly used for signs in public. Each Tandoran is given a temporary name when they hatch. A Tandoran’s youth name is discarded when they come of age and receive their adult name. It has become the tradition in recent generations for this to be a Japanese name with a fitting meaning.

As a whole, the Tandorans are flamboyant and creative. They consider self-expression to be the highest form of art, tempered by respect for the self-expression of others. Everyone is expected to assert their own perspective, especially when it might be the cause of an argument. Imagine one of those reality talk shows where everyone is overthe-top and constantly trying to outdo each other. Now reimagine a version of that where each participant lets the others finish their sentences instead of talking over them. It’s a little bit like that, though I must admit that I found even the most obnoxious Tandorans to be much more likable than those people on the trid. Sometimes arguments get too heated or can’t be reconciled. In those cases, the Tandorans engage in formal duels. These duels are rarely to the death, though severe injuries are not uncommon. The nature of each duel is defined by the participants before it begins, but the most popular form involves what I like to call vine jousting. The duelists stand off from positions high up within nearby trees with numerous vines dangling in between them. When the duel begins, the duelists each draw a knife and leap for the vines. In order to win the duel, they must force their opponent to fall into a net on the forest floor. No direct contact is allowed—this must be accomplished by cutting vines. These types of duels are frequent enough that they serve as the most popular spectator sport among the Tandorans living in Arbora. Duels can take the form of nearly any type of contest, including races, dance-offs, musical performances, painting, creating sculptures, dinosaur taming, puppet shows—almost anything. The one thing that you won’t see in a Tandoran duel is straight-up fight. Duels involving direct personal

combat are discouraged to the point that they are considered not only dishonorable, but also something far worse in the eyes of Tandoran culture: uncreative. This isn’t to say that Tandorans never fight each other—there are occasional murders as well as skirmishes between factions, but it’s considered an aberration.

Tandoran leadership is matriarchal—only female Tandorans are permitted to hold positions of authority, and every female Tandoran is expected to become a leader. When it comes to governing a town, managing a business, captaining a riverboat, or leading a hunting expedition, you will always find a woman in charge. This is so deeply ingrained into Tandoran culture that no one questions it. When we first arrived at Arbora, only Nancy and Bandit were taken before the village council, while the rest of us had to wait on our raft. They were quite astonished to learn that neither of the women in our party were in charge of our expedition. The cause of this is likely the influence of the Great Mother, a female great dragon who brought their eggs from another world (probably Earth, but we’ll likely never know for sure), taught the first ones to hatch, and watched over them for ten generations. With such a dominant example of feminine wisdom and power, it’s easy to imagine that the Tandorans would accept female leadership as the natural order. I must admit that this seemed a bit too similar to a caste system for it to sit well with me, but I kept my eyes and ears open and withheld my criticism. The Tandorans themselves don’t seem to find it to be unjust, and I saw no evidence of systemic oppression. I also learned that quite a few Tandorans are transgender or genderfluid, with no stigma applied. Everyone is encouraged to express who they are, and there is an expectation that they will be accepted. A full gender and sex transition takes less than an hour, facilitated by a specific magic ritual. One riverboat captain that I met is male when off-duty and transitions to female when it is time to take charge of their vessel. Such frequent transitions are uncommon, but they happen enough for most people to have seen it.

Deeply embedded into Tandoran culture is a respect for the land, water, and the living plants, creatures, and spirits of their environment. They are conservationists at heart and are careful to disrupt the natural order as little as possible, and do what they can to make repairs or amends when it is not possible to avoid a disruption. In turn, the spirits of the island tend to respect the Tandorans. Despite the prevalence of massive and dangerous predators, it is incredibly rare for a Tandoran to be hunted and eaten by one of them. Tandorans live in harmony and relative peace with the plants and beasts that share the island with them, and often form relationships with them. Despite their bestial nature, the spirits of Tandora are sapient entities who think and act as beasts. They do not usewords or complex language, but they are highly intelligent and expressive beings. Most prefer to live out their lives as beasts in the wild, but many join Tandorans—you might think of them as “tamed” dinosaurs, but you would be missing the point I’m trying to make. These great beasts may act as draft animals, hunting companions, or use their muscle to operate machinery, but they aren’t just trained animals. These dinosaurs are considered full members of Tandoran society and are treated more like family than pets or work animals.

From the Tandorans’ respect for the lives of the beast spirits that inhabit their world, you might expect them to be vegetarians. They eat a lot of fruit, nuts, and other plants, but they also hunt the occasional dinosaur. Their favorite food is fish— specifically, fish prepared with a unique blend of spices (the special recipe for which is always a deeply held family secret) and fermented in submerged barrels for months—though some are aged for much longer than that. They call this delicacy “spice fish.”
