## **Posted by: Gertrude the Duck**

**Sometimes, a run goes bad, and you end up dumped in the middle of Death Valley. Why did they not just put a bullet in my head? Honestly, I think they wanted to see if I could make it. Not long after I got out, I got an offer for a job at ten percent higher than my normal rate, and while I wasn’t able to confrm it for sure, I think the people who dumped me made sure I got that job. I’d proved something to them.**

**We fed toward the makeshift dock. Bullets were raining down heavier now—better aimed, too, as a couple hit my body armor, which hurt like hell but is still way better than an actual gunshot wound.**

**At that point, they might have guessed what the plan was. They certainly had some remote controls over their ship. Our livelihood was down to a contest of hackers. I had hoped that maybe cold air**



**But that’s another story. As far as the story at hand goes, I at least had satellite Matrix access, so I was able to locate where I was. I was south of Last Chance Mountain, maybe ffty kilometers from any kind of decently traveled road.**

**> This whole story is an effective advertisement for satellite Matrix service. Information can save you from a lot of emergencies!**

**> Kay St. Irregular**

**You may ask, if I had Matrix access, why didn’t I immediately call for help? I did. One problem with the desert is, it’s far from everyone. People aren’t just a couple of kilometers away, waiting to help you. And the people you** _**can**_ **reach aren’t necessarily eager to make a dangerous trek into the heat to save you. I could wait, but it wasn’t clear how long the wait would be, and I didn’t know where I’d get water while waiting. Sitting and waiting to die of thirst is not my thing, so I decided to move.**

**The distance I had to travel was more than a marathon, and I wasn’t going to be able to just walk as the crow fies. There was a little mountain between me and the closest active road—not overly tall, not overly steep, but still a change in elevation.**

**A normal person is supposed to drink two or three liters of water a day. A marathoner should drink maybe a bottle of water every half hour or so—if I could maintain a pace of eight kilometers an hour (which would be quite fast, given the conditions), I’d make the trip in just over six hours. I wouldn’t be jogging, but I’d be in the desert, so marathon conditions were a good guide. Meaning I should have twelve bottles of water.**

**I had none.**

**I was at severe risk of having dizziness set in well before I got anywhere, and that was extremely unlikely to help me. Going faster wouldn’t help me. Even waiting for night wouldn’t help me, as that would just extend the time I’d go without water. So what’s a girl stranded in the desert to do?**

**First: Cover up. I was wearing a button-down shirt and a tank top. I took off the tank top, buttoned the shirt all the way up, and draped the tank top over my head, being sure to cover the back of my neck. Sun hitting skin meant sweat, and sweat is water loss.**

**Second: Evaluate the environment. And man, had they really screwed me. I was in sand dunes, which right off the bat made a quick pace tough to maintain. Plant growth was extremely limited, so there wasn’t anything that pointed me to local moisture. The map, though, showed me one thing that I hung my entire life on. The dunes narrowed to a canyon to the northeast. The terrain was rough on the other side, and this would make the journey longer. I hoped it would also make it survivable.**

**I hit the canyon at the low end, which meant it was the best place to look for water. I clung to the north wall and looked for low-lying areas with rock overhangs. Sure enough, there were small deposits of water. I was smart enough to not lick water off the rock with my tongue, as that could absorb enough sand and minerals to effectively neutralize the effect of the water. Instead, I took the tank top off my head, soaked it, and squeezed it into my mouth. Was I anywhere close to what my water intake should be? No. But it was more than nothing, so that was good.**

**Let me tell you the one thing I didn’t worry about: food. Digesting food requires water, and I didn’t want to dedicate any moisture to that task. So any rumblings in my stomach would be ignored until I was safe.**

**The good news is that once I hit the canyon, I was walking along a road of sorts. The bad news is that very few people drive that road, especially since parks services stopped taking care of the place. At least, though, it made for pretty good footing and decent walkability.**

- **That’s a good reminder to never count on any national or state park facilities working properly. Attention given to them ranges from neglect to abandonment—assuming a park service even exists in that particular jurisdiction.**
