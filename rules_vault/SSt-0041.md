## **Posted by: Marty**

## **Contract** Negotiations

Doing business involves working with people. This is every bit as true in the shadows as it is in other, more legitimate lines of work. While many situations are settled with a cyberdeck, a fi rearm, or a stealth suit, it is often much more effi cient to settle them with a conversation. This is why it’s so important to have a face on your team—someone who can navigate social situations, work with or manipulate people to achieve your goals, acquire key information and equipment by calling on a network of useful contacts, and, of course, negotiate for the highest possible pay when you take a job. Some of the most successful shadowrunner teams have a dedicated face, while many others treat it as a secondary role. Not having anyone on your team with formidable social skills puts you at a signifi cant disadvantage in several ways, not the least of which is the likelihood that your employers and other contacts will consider your team to be unprofessionals they can take advantage of.

The most obvious role of the face comes to negotiating contracts with employers. As a face, you are expected to take the lead during any meets with Mr. Johnson or a fi xer. In this situation, you have two primary objectives: gathering information and maximizing compensation. When hiring a team of shadowrunners, nearly every employer will leave some important information out—they are in the process of hiring a team of criminals for an unsanctioned task that would likely result in severely unpleasant consequences if their involvement in the matter were to come to light. This is why the terms Mr. or Ms. Johnson and other similar _noms de plume_ are utilized—they need as much anonymity as they can get. Even the most trustworthy and on-the-level Mr. Johnson will hold back vital information for the sake of maintaining this deniability, and it is up to the face to suss out as much of this information as possible. Most Johnsons are not terribly trustworthy, but they want the job done as smoothly as possible—as do the shadowrunners taking the job. On this basis, you can most easily establish common ground, as your purposes are aligned. The best way to convince Mr. Johnson to reveal as much useful information as possible is to convince them that your team consists of professionals who will be able to reliably complete their objectives, and that they made the right choice by hiring you. With this foundation established, you may press Mr. Johnson for all relevant information on the basis that it is necessary for the smooth completion of these objectives. This is often enough, but you must also learn to detect inconsistencies and other signs of subterfuge. No Mr. Johnson will come to trust you implicitly, and so they will always reserve any information that could be used against them. You must balance the need to press for vital information against the proprieties of anonymity—this is a complex dance. Establishing trust and confdence is one thing, but pressing too far might convey anxiety and paranoia, or you may end up annoying your prospective employer to the point that they withhold information or reduce your fee out of spite. Ideally, these negotiations should not be confrontational—things work out much better when you establish a cooperative relationship with them.**

- **I can’t stress enough how important it is to find out as much as you can before you take the job. Most runners think they have to take a job on the spot, but I’ve found you can negotiate a time period for gathering facts most of the time. My article “Filling in the Gaps” has some explicit examples of what can go wrong if you ignore this advice.**

**When the objectives of the job have been thoroughly explained, and Mr. Johnson has told you everything they are willing to reveal, it is time to settle on the payment—and the payment terms. Initially, Mr. Johnson will almost always offer a lowball sum—this is as much tradition and etiquette as it is good business sense. Be very wary of any Mr. Johnson who offers you an unusually large sum of money and is easily convinced to increase it, as this most often means that they have no intention of fulflling their obligation to pay it. In fact, the payment negotiations are also a key portion of your information-gathering efforts. The amount of money on the table tells you something of the scope of your work—small jobs with smaller stakes tend to involve lesser amounts,**

**while jobs that pay very well are likely to involve more diffcult complications and more dire consequences. The diffculty of convincing Mr. Johnson to increase their initial offer tells you how much control they have over the resources they are using to employ you. A Mr. Johnson who is fairly easily convinced to raise their price is more likely to be in greater control of the situation, while one who is highly reluctant to pay more is either strapped for resources or being held to account by someone with signifcant infuence over them. This doesn’t tell you much and relies on making assumptions, but it is defnitely worth taking into consideration. While getting paid more is critical, it is even more critical to get a feel for whether or not your Mr. Johnson intends to keep faith. If you are a very new team with no established reputation, you are signifcantly more likely to be misled, set up, or used. If your team has established some solid street cred, you are much less likely to be selected by a Johnson who intends to do you dirty—while there are risks involved in letting a team of runners who know too much live, the risks of taking down veteran shadowrunners are even higher. Most Mr. Johnsons are aware of this, and only the inexperienced ones will choose to take out a runner team in order to keep their secrets.**

**> I always negotiate for upfront payment. Partly because it will help you cover expenses, but mostly because it’s a test of Mr. Johnson’s willingness and ability to pay.**
