## **JOURNAL ENTRY NINE \<09-25-82/1518:58>**

The word festered in my mind like a wound: Disian. On the surface, there was nothing to it—a simple word, really. But my desire to know more grew. In a world where magic meets machine, a species that transcends Earth as we know it almost seemed like too much, too fantastical. And it was consuming me.

So I took a chance. It was dumb. If I were caught, the repercussions to my career, my honor, even my family would be inconceivable. Hamita-san said no more research—hard stop. And yet, I couldn’t help myself. Like a child in a room of sweets told to eat no more, I had to try just one.

I tracked the street doc down to a clinic in Redmond Barrens. The word “clinic” might be a loose term, because I’d never seen a more derelict structure in my life. Half the lights barely worked, and gangers littered the worn concrete like roaches in the back of a Stuffer Shack. And still, either through curiosity or stupidity, I went to see him in person.

When I told him I had seen the recording of him from some rigger’s drone preaching about Disians, his squinty eyes shone. And he told me things, ridiculous things too far-fetched to be believed.

Moreover … he told me about shadowrunners.

I’d always known about them of course. SINless dissidents intent on destroying all that the corporations have built. But he told me more about them. Sure, many of them—most of them, to be fair—did what they did for the nuyen. They were mercenaries working for any bidder, morals as loose as a shopping cart’s wheel.

But some were fighters, revolutionaries seeking to make a change in a world that didn’t so much leave them behind but ignore their existence. They worked to make a path in a society that needed them, yet shunned them anyway.

This spoke to me somehow. It opened up something within me I didn’t know was there, a yearning. I wanted to see and know all there was in the Sixth World, no matter how fantastical, because … simply put, our world was boring until then.

My life was work. Work meant success. Success meant honor. Honor meant reward. There was no time for danger or fantasy, and the allure proved too tempting.

And so, once more I went under the knife. Lured by the siren’s song of promise to see a life more extraordinary than my own, I let him install a second datajack. This one was hidden to all, so I can delve deeper into the Matrix. He even leased me an

old Spinrad Falcon with a few programs on it to get me started and helped me construct my persona. In return, I’d give him a little information, nothing much. He just wanted a snippet here and there from MCT. But I wasn’t totally dumb; I put a limit on it, negotiated so I couldn’t be blackmailed into doing it forever. The doc agreed.

Ronin-Belle was born.
