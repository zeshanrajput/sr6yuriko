## **Misdirection**

**I can remember a particularly diffcult extraction job in which our plans took advantage of what I’ve been trying to tell you. The target was an involuntary employee of MCT who had been extracted rather violently about six months prior, and she was valuable enough to her original employer to warrant hiring a team of shadowrunners for a counter-extraction to get her back. That’s where we came in. Since the target was a fight risk, we didn’t have the luxury of luring her out to a less-secure location—which is how I’d much rather handle an extraction. The location itself was a high-security isolated facility up in the mountains. If you’re familiar with MCT, you can probably guess that it was a Zero Zone. This meant that we’d be facing military-grade security with itchy trigger fngers and more guns, ammo, guards, and drones than we could hope to handle in a straight-up fght—even a quick engagement would bring the hammer down on us faster than we’d have time to mop up and disengage. The Matrix host was just as dangerous, which meant that our hacker was only able to pull off quick in-and-out breaches for the purpose of grabbing intel and wasn’t likely to be able to ride herd on their sensor network for long stretches. I’d never seen them so nervous about hacking a place. We**

**I’ll give you a quick rundown on what we discovered through our various legwork and observations. Overall surveillance coverage at the site was considerable—they didn’t have to worry about random passersby other than the occasional animal or interference from city ordinances. Most of their perimeter security was covered by drone patrols, backed up by hidden static sensor arrays. The ones closest to the site were hardwired, but the ones farther than 250 meters out were running wireless and in stealth mode. Visually they were so well-hidden that you would have no chance spotting them, but the hacker on our team is a technomancer with a knack for sniffng out hidden icons. Sensor arrays included visual cameras with lowlight and thermographic capabilities, directional microphones with select sound flters, and motion sensors. Heavily armed two-person guard patrols with full body armor and a trained hellhound roved the area, following semi-randomized patrol routes that usually stayed within that 250-meter perimeter but were sometimes observed ranging out as far as 500 meters. Anything farther out than that was covered by aerial surveillance drones. Most of those were the balloon-style Condors, but there were at least a half-dozen roto-drones in the air at all times as well, armed with a mix of sniper rifes, machine guns, and grenade launchers. Several reinforced guard stations were located at key positions roughly 500 meters from the site, each one staffed with the equivalent of a high-threat-response team. MCT is no slouch when it comes to their magical security, either. Bound spirits patrolled astral space surrounding the site, and multiple wards covered the building. The hellhounds I mentioned could alert the mundane guard patrols of anything suspicious, report it back, and have a couple of spirits there to back them up in almost no time.**

**Fortunately for us, we had several weeks to prep for the run, which gave us enough time to observe the security forces in detail. We’d also been given an upfront budget for expenses, and we used most of it to get as much information as possible— if our favorite information broker has kids, they probably won’t need to take out any student loans to get through college. There were some arguments among the team about how much we were cutting into our payout, but eventually everyone came to understand that we needed every bit of data we could gather if we were going to pull this off. This netted us some detailed, up-to-date foor plans that had been unavailable or too well-protected for our hacker to snag, detailed logistics data (including delivery and maintenance schedules), and dozens of after-action reports from runner teams that had recently dealt with MCT security up close. Most of those stories did not have happy endings, but**



**they helped us piece together a very good picture of their security response protocols.**

**We also gathered some of our own intel on that front using soft probes or dry runs. I’ll give you some examples. Our shaman did some astral scouting and was able to sneak around effectively up to within roughly 100 meters of the site, using things like rocks and trees to dodge the attention of patrolling spirits. Any closer to the facility was a wide-open killing feld with little to no cover, and they were spotted within seconds of approaching the mana barriers. They counted four total patrol spirits that covered the one-kilometer radius around the place, though one of them stayed very close to the building. Once spotted, our shaman would zip away instead of getting into a fght. They made several attempts and were spotted quickly each time. Another test made by our astral scout involved pestering the hellhounds. They hit the dog with a Sleep spell—it took them three attempts to knock the critter out—and the patrol spirits were on them in less than ten seconds. I wish mages could take a stopwatch with them into astral space, but we were forced to rely on our shaman’s best guess. They tried this three different times—the frst two times, they zipped away as soon as reinforcements arrived, but the third time they brought their own spirit backup and stayed to fght. Our shaman was able to quickly disrupt the frst two patrol spirits that showed up, but the next pair were more powerful and took longer to neutralize. Just as the shaman’s spirits fnished those off, he was forced to retreat when an MCT astral combat mage—armed with a glowing sword—showed up with their own team of four fre spirits. Our rigger had some very stealthy surveillance drones in the area (he got a kick out of using MCT drones for this part) and was able to observe the response of physical security to this intrusion. I won’t bore you with too many details, but let’s just say that the hornet’s nest was defnitely kicked over. Drones and personnel were dispatched to the location, and patrols were doubled for the next 48 hours. If we’d been planning on hitting the place soon, this would have been a problem, but as I said, we had weeks to prepare. And it’s not like we’re really going to make an MCT Zero-Zone security force all that much more paranoid than they already were.**

**As for their drone coverage, we made a few hitand-run strikes on the (relatively) unarmed surveillance drones to see how they responded. They had quite a few roto-drones on standby, which told us they were capable of launching at least two dozen of those fragile but well-armed things if needed. This would have cost most riggers a few combat drones, except that the one our rigger used was kitted out for stealth and mounted a suppressed sniper rife—given the ranges involved, our rigger’s talent for evading detection, and some technomancer backup, MCT didn’t have much chance of spotting**

**them as long as they only fred once. Good thing, too—the intel we were gathering had already been expensive, and reimbursing our rigger for the loss of their own drones would have stretched our expenses too far. Over the next month or so, we occasionally harassed the MCT security forces by knocking out their hellhounds, patrol spirits, and the occasional surveillance drone with little hitand-run strikes similar to the ones I mentioned. Since they were already always at a state of hyper-vigilance, we hoped this would have the effect of getting them used to the occasional events so that they wouldn’t expect what we were about to attempt when it came time for the extraction.**

**Our fnal plan was essentially a highly coordinated and incredibly risky smash-and-grab. We needed to rile up their security forces, get them pointed in the wrong direction, and make our move from an unexpected quarter while they were otherwise occupied. This involved expanding our team to involve an additional decker, rigger, and magician. Shortly before the run, the extra mage and our shaman called up a small army of spirits, and our riggers and technomancer pulled off a drone heist to gather up some expendable attack drones. It took them a few days to scrub the drones clean and make the necessary modifcations, but the mage took almost a week to bind all of those spirits (much of that time was spent recovering from the drain from each attempt). Our technomancer also registered their own army of fault sprites during this time. By the time we were ready to go in, our employer had started to show some signs of impatience. We’d put over two months of planning into this run, but if it took us more than fve minutes to accomplish, we’d be dead. We told them to sit tight a little bit longer and get our credsticks ready.**

**It’s worth mentioning that our team’s rigger had an Ares Venture—a vectored-thrust aircraft— with a few special modifcations that improved its speed and helped to hide its signature. If you get the opportunity to acquire one of these, I highly recommend it. I doubt we could have pulled off this run if we had to rely on ground vehicles. Even so, most of the team carefully maneuvered to just outside a kilometer from the site, going in on foot in full camoufage. We took our time, with updates on when and where to move from our rigger and technomancer, who were our eyes in the sky. Our shaman was with us on foot, but didn’t have any magic running in order to avoid the attention of the patrol spirits. The hired rigger had a feet of aerial combat drones (mostly MCT roto-drones) in a rented van several kilometers farther away, well outside of MCT’s security perimeter. The combined forces of the shaman’s and mage’s spirits were on standby, ready to move in. Our technomancer was in regular communication with the decker, who’d be very busy providing Matrix cov-**

Even with all of our planning, it was a close thing. MCT doesn’t send all of their security forces out to respond to threats, and we weren’t able to avoid all of their reserves. It was a hard run and cost us more than we’d hoped, but we went up against some of the toughest security MCT could muster and came out on top. We couldn’t have snuck past the security through any other means— they were just too vigilant and had every angle covered. So we relied on misdirection instead. **We started the show when one of the guard patrols was almost opposite our position on their route. The MCT drones sent out to chase down our sniper drone came under assault from the Matrix thanks to our technomancer’s crew of fault sprites, which drew the attention of the security spiders on site. Soon our expendable attack drones were engaged with what remained of the MCT roto-drone feet—but even with the Matrix support, it was only a matter of time before they’d be wiped out. Once the astral forces were fully engaged, our shaman’s kindred spirit made some appearances near the guard shacks, using its illusion spell to make it seem that there were people skulking around there. Because the astral security forces already had their hands full, they weren’t able to verify what was happening, and each of the high-threat-response teams felt they were pinned down as they expended an insane amount of ammunition into the surrounding terrain to no effect.**
