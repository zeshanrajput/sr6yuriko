## Genetic Tailoring

Nature is a barrier built from old thinking. The true nature of humanity is change. There was a time when cracking the human genome was considered a blasphemy, yet now church funding of genetics exceeds that of any independent governmental entity. Genetic engineering or gene tailoring is often seen as a subset of biotechnology. Where this may have been true in the past, in modern parlance, biotech is more often associated with the creation of new organs or organisms, while gene tailoring is responsible for the blueprint of what is to be created. In the coming years, genetics will no longer be gated by technological insufficiency. Advances in Quantative Trait Locus Analysis enable us to not simply explain the genetic basis of variation in complex traits but guide it through trials toward a desired outcome. What remains is the rigorous testing process to show that we are repeatedly and systematically making the specific desired genetic changes we desire through these gene modification procedures and ensure those changes do not have unintended side effects.

As I said, this is a map of where we are going, and the roads we must travel in order to get there. We began this journey centuries ago with the selective breeding of animals. The transgenic nature of HMHVV accelerated our understanding of how to modify DNA to our needs. Finally, metahumanity—and specifically, Sudden Unexplained Recessive Genetic Expression (SURGE)—opened our minds to the possibility of different modes of being. As we have grown to accept these new modes of being, many have begun to ask the question: What does it mean to be human?

The partial answer to that question is: You are born that way. Embryo modification remains a social stigma, whose future is darkened by the historical cloud of eugenics. However, choice post-birth is widely accepted, especially with regard to correcting genetic errors. Post-birth human customization is now standard practice. This is not limited to the selection of eye and hair color, gender affirmation, or even the adoption of modifications previously limited to metahumanity. We are not just moving toward selective meta-variance. We are creating radically enhanced human beings. Those genetic changes will be passed on to their children and fundamentally challenge what it means to be human.

We still don’t know everything about how gene interaction works through long-term human reproduction. We might be able to pinpoint specific traits and add others, but how are the interactions between all the genes changed by the one we pulled out or put in? For all we truly know, genes could interact like a set of dominoes on a table. One edit could create terrible side effects. On the other hand, we’ve been modifying genes in plant and animal life for decades, and the domino effect has not been observed even once. The benefits far outweigh the risks of continuing, and for that reason, in the next twenty years, you will begin to see commercial applications in three areas of radical genetic enhancement. A fourth area continues to grow, though far less publicly than the others.

Athletic performance is gaining ground as the most commercially requested form of gene therapy. At present, the technology to perform advanced genetic modification of this sort is limited to gene labs; there are, however, specific therapies relating to muscle strength and bone density moving toward over-the-counter accessibility. Beyond access, the reach of these therapies is increasing exponentially. The dermal toughness and physical power associated with trolls will be able to be assigned to other metavariants in the next twenty years.

Physical trait management may be the crowd pleaser, but the work to boost human intelligence is fundamental to our advancement as a species. We sit on the precipice of the ability to create super-intelligent beings far beyond the abilities of our present thinkers. That process is notably dangerous. We still know so very little about the brain, yet it presents the final significant barrier to body transience. Until we have the ability to transfer human consciousness to a new brain or a digital counterpart, human lifespans will be tied to our ability to maintain that nervous tissue. Therefore, the work continues to enhance our intelligence and create the individuals who will help our species take the next step forward in evolution.
