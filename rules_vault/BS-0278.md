## History of Nanotech

Nanotechnology is the science of building structures and devices on a microscopic or even molecular scale. The basic ideals behind the tech have been around for a while, starting with a physicist named Richard Feynman. He made a presentation in 1959 titled “There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom” that introduced the idea of making machines that could build smaller machines, then create still smaller machines, and so on until they were being constructed on the atomic scale. It took another twenty-five years for the scanning, tunneling electron microscope to provide a tool for working at the atomic scale and for Eric Drexler to refine the idea of nanoscale assemblers. Before long, the first commercial applications of nanotechnology appeared with field-effect transistor circuits and engineered materials like carbon nanotubes.

Eventually the promise of nanofabrication envisioned by Drexler began to bear fruit; smaller and smaller drone-like machines were created, eventually becoming small enough to work inside the living body and carry out medical operations on a

scale previously unmanageable. The neuro-optical interface used to connect every modern cybernetic device is a prime example of this. Nanoscale surgical tools made the twenty-first century’s cybernetic implants possible. As the tech advanced and nanomachines became smaller and more sophisticated, nanotechnology became an essential part of every type of manufacturing, leading to breakthroughs in biotechnology, computing, cybernetics, and even heavy industries such as mining and aeronautics. By the 2050s, nanotechnology was a subtle and pervasive part of the world.

That changed in the 2060s as breakthrough after breakthrough moved the technology through decades of advances in a few short years. Nanoware augmentations using semi-autonomous injectable nanites became the industry standard. Of course, the true source of the rapid advancement is a dark secret kept hidden by the megacorporations. In 2059, the AI Deus locked down the Seattle Renraku Arcology and started experimenting on the 100,000 metahumans trapped there. In 2061, when the UCAS military liberated the arcology, it was discovered that Deus had advanced several technologies far beyond anything seen before. Some of it was immediately apparent such as its bizarrely effective drone designs, but the AI’s greatest achievements were not so readily visible. Deus had radically advanced the science of nanotechnology to carry out its ultimate plan, escaping the arcology’s host in the minds of the survivors. Blind to any moral consideration, every megacorporation raced to obtain samples of the technology, reverse-engineer it, and integrate Deus’s advances into their own products.

Perhaps it was karma that this rapid advancement of nanotech led directly to its destruction. In December 2072, a few shadowrunners broke into a NeoNET facility outside of Albuquerque and unintentionally carried something back out. The facility was a prison for AIs and e-ghosts, and these captive Matrix entities had adapted the highly advanced nanites in the facility to carry copies of their code and rewrite it into the minds of metahumans, hijacking their bodies. Eerily similar to the original purpose Deus had for creating the technology, perhaps they found some hidden fragments of code in the nanites ‘operating systems, or it could have been a case of convergent evolution in digital life. Regardless of the source of the CFD exploit, by 2075 it had spread worldwide, and the world noticed. Anyone using nanoware who carried active nanite colonies was especially vulnerable to the mystery disease, but the risk also extended to anyone with cybernetics created and installed with nanotech. Any device that used nanotechnology in its construction or operation was susceptible to sudden failure, as its nanites were taken over by the entities. The result? All the cutting-edge technology that we had taken for granted was now tainted. Nanotechnology had infiltrated every aspect of modern society and then it went rogue. It could have been worse, like a full-scale gray goo nano-apocalypse worse, but it was still bad. The only option left was to mitigate the damage. That meant redefining what nanotech could and couldn’t do. Nanotechnology went backward several generations. Nanites were simplified, limited to specific hard-coded single functions. The end results were similar in most cases, and some new tricks were developed through studying the monads, but tasks that once were handled by one versatile multipurpose type of nanite are now needed dozens of different simplified nanites whose actions are centrally controlled by a new generation of nanohives.

Monads, on the other hand, moved in a completely different direction. The original nanites hijacked in 2075 evolved at a geometric pace and today have progressed numerous generations past the original technology. Each nanite created in a monad’s system is now a truly autonomous auxon, an independent synthetic living organism that does not need any central control. It is not only capable of carrying out complex operations across a vast array of tasks, but it can exactly replicate itself from an assortment of easily obtained base materials.

The monads themselves have also evolved. The fear generated by CFD abated somewhat after therapeutics were developed to reverse the condition. Many of the most militant “head cases” have either been killed or imprisoned, and many other monads have left the solar system on an interstellar colony ship dubbed Deep Space Exploration and Colonization I (DSECI). Those who remain have accepted the protection of a megacorporation SIN or have withdrawn into their own isolated communities. An unspoken agreement seems to have been reached between monads and metahumanity to leave each other alone. The only new monads being created these days are the rare person who seeks them out to join them—or perhaps the rumors are true about monads using cloned blanks for new bodies.
