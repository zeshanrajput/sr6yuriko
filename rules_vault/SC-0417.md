## Interceptor

The title of interceptor covers a few different but related aspects of rigging. Sometimes it’s about tracking down another racing rigger—a stoppo or a courier, for instance—and preventing them from continuing to move whatever it is they’re carrying. Or just straight taking it from them. That connects

to the second way an interceptor can function— as a pirate. Pirates have the goal of meeting an in-transit vehicle and taking things from them, up to an including the full vehicle. Some law-enforcement officials play interceptor roles, catching up to speeding vehicles, smugglers, or other lawbreakers in order to bring them to justice—or collect a nice bribe from them.

While the stoppo and the courier are kind of playing a large game of dodge ball, avoiding any obstacles that might slow them down, the interceptor is playing chess. They want to think ahead about where their target is going, how they will get there, and most importantly, where they can beat them to the punch. The interceptor is a master of geography, routes, and seizing slight advantages. The stop you avoid here, the ten seconds you save, can be the margin that puts you in front of your target. It may take several such ten-second increments to make up the gap between the interceptor and target, but if that’s what it takes, that’s what the interceptor will do. They have the confidence to build a strategy and the patience to carry it out.

The interceptor who is stealing other people’s stolen goods, or the pirate out to find a haul for themselves, have a short path to the shadows. The law enforcement interceptor’s path is longer. Maybe they got on the wrong side of some other powerful officers and found themselves drummed out of the force. Maybe the regimented grind of corp life got to them, and they decided to use their skills to be their own boss. Or maybe they realized the people they intercepted were dong far less damage to the world than the people they were working for, so it was time for them to switch sides.
