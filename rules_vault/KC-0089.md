## Hawalas

The hawalas are primarily known as money lenders, but in truth that image is just a pleasant cover for their real position in Lagos: power brokers. They deal not only in naira but also in information and goods (ill-gotten or otherwise). They may not hold the sway of the Lagos Council (except Kiniun), but they control the flow of money and information on the streets and in the shadows of Lagos.

How they do this is the important part of knowing about them. The hawalas maintain their power through trust, fear, and money. They keep the public trust by making sure individuals in Lagos honor the value of the naira they place their symbol on and by making sure others do not violate that trust by forging those naira. They maintain part of this trust through fear. Forging naira or breaking a deal based on a hawala’s naira is always punished. For some, it is certain death, often in a very public andgruesome manner, but for most it is a very obvious disfigurement or something similar. Each of the hawalas has their own particular flavor of disfigurement in order to make sure others know who this person wronged, and more importantly, that violation of the hawalas’ trust does not go unpunished.

They also maintain that trust through money. Money is power, and that power does not just come from its value—it’s also tied to the symbolic power of its existence. The fact that some non-valuable object holds an artificial value based on the belief in the power behind it is a strong force. The hawalas power the money, and the money powers the hawala through the people’s reliance and trust in that currency.

Here are the basics of the hawala system: A person can make a deposit (certified electronic funds, hard currency, precious metals or gems, or occasionally other valuable commodities) with a hawala in their area. For a percentage, generally ten to twenty percent, the hawala gives the client the proper chits for their remaining value. The system depends completely on the honor and honesty of the hawalas and the trust their clients place in them. As such, these people are held in high esteem within their societies and often act as trustworthy and reputable fixers.

Hawala tokens in Lagos are usually small items, such as tiny shells, fragments of bone, or small rocks with intricate carvings indicating the originating hawala and value. Forging a hawala token is a serious cultural taboo, and most Lagosians believe doing so will call down a curse on the thief. Eerie stories of carved leopards springing to life to slaughter a thief, a plague killing a transgressor’s entire family, or spirits driving people mad are whispered throughout the sprawl. The truth is that violating the trust of a hawala in this way is a surefire to get you killed and possibly worse.

Hawala tokens always have two sides, with symbols on both. The top shows the value, the bottom the hawala’s personal symbol. In Lagos, the network of Lagosian hawalas has agreed upon the common symbols and values, as identified in the sidebar.
