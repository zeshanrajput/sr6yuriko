## **> Legal Eagle**

**> Although sometimes those using it don’t understand how it works. I once had a field training officer who thought you always had to start with verbal commands, then work your way up. He got shot in the face when instead of going for his gun, he went for his baton.**

**> X-Star**

**First off, most cops will have an impact weapon of some kind. These days it’s usually an extendable baton as opposed to an old-fashioned nightstick. But some still carry full-sized models, especially in places where having something big and threateninglooking is also a deterrent.**

**Next up is OC (or oleoresin capsicum) spray. There’s plenty of brand names out there, but the**



**most popular is known as Pepper Punch. This stuff can incapacitate a drugged-out razorhead in short order. Getting dosed with it is absolutely miserable, and most cops see it as justifcation for lethal force should it be used against them because of its potential to incapacitate an offcer and take their sidearms. Keep that in mind when choosing your less-than-lethal option.**

**Next is a taser or shock weapon, also sometimes called a “pain compliance device.” The most common model is the Defance Super Shock. It’s reliable and has the best electrical bang for your buck. I swear, Defance stays in business through LEP cartridge purchases.**

**Last, but certainly not least, is the cop’s sidearm or “service weapon.” A cop’s gun is heavily tied into their PAN and their bodycam’s imagelink system. So, when it (or a taser) is drawn, the recordings begin. Sidearms are issued to cops when they’re hired and are the offcer’s responsibility to maintain. This means that not all guns are always kept up to date or in pristine condition. Some oldtimers retain older weapons, and as long as the smartgun connection works, they can keep using them. I carry an older Thunderbolt, even though the higher-ups are pushing newer models. I’ve known some old-timers who still have their old Ares Predator or Colt Manhunter with an external smartlink. I guess when you use something for that long, you get really good with it.**

**In some places, beggars can’t be choosers. Some precincts are still waiting on their upgrades. These are always held up by some kind of logistical delay, corporate-sounding reason, or even a political pissing contest. Best I can say is that’s above my pay grade.**

**In some jurisdictions, the sidearms and duty guns used vary wildly. Long guns or assault rifes tend to be more common in places such as Aztlan, Amazonia, Russia, and Southeast Asia, and seeing cops casually holding an M-23, AK-97, or even an Ares Alpha isn’t beyond the pale. In Europe, H&K and FN weapons are popular for law enforcement use. And of course, if it’s Knight Errant, expect nothing but Ares Arms hardware.**

**Speaking of, one thing I tell other cops not to carry is less-than-lethal ammo. The justifcation being, if you have to draw your gun, the time for less-than-lethal has passed. The chance for batons, OC spray, taser, or even de-escalation is gone. This isn’t to say some idiot with a badge won’t just draw down immediately when they shouldn’t. Just be aware the training exists to respond reasonably to the threat being perceived.**

- **This has been a controversial topic for decades and has only become more complicated since the advent and prevalence of firearms with ammo-selection capabilities. Why employ lethal force when less-than-lethal options are available? Depending on**

**the political climate of an area, this could be problematic for the LEP and their officers.**

**> Legal Eagle**

- **Look, I don’t have any particular love for cops, but I do know gunfights. Ammo selection is a great option and can provide additional choices when on a run. But sometimes a less-thanlethal option isn’t enough to stop the threat that’s about to take my fragging head off. And no matter what any corporate sales pitch promises, ammo changing takes precious seconds I may not have. So, in that context, I get and agree with what the good detective is saying.**

**> Cayman**

**Beyond all of this, it’s also common for a cop to carry a backup gun (because things happen) and a reliable knife (because they are also great tools), even though it isn’t part of their standard loadout.**
