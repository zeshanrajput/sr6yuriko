## racing rigger

## playing Advice

The primary skills the racing rigger needs and how to use them should be clear. A boost to the character can come from building a secondary skills set, and some of those choices depend on which background the player wants to use. The smuggler/coyote should have some Stealth, which could come in handy in a number of non-driving situations. The stoppo needs high Reaction, which is a step toward having a high initiative—having a good combat skill could let them get a quick lick in during battle. The interceptor’s high Logic could put them on the road to being a part-time hacker. Finding a secondary skillset can help the racing rigger feel useful in more situations.

The racing rigger has many specialized uses, but they can also be effective utility players. Their attributes and physical skills overlap with combat-focused roles and scenarios outside of their vehicles. They should be able to hold their own in physical activities and combat. Their “soft” skills of Stealth, Perception, and Etiquette, along with various Knowledge Skills, make them useful before combat or to prevent combat. The racing rigger who can be dropped in any sprawl and find the local street racer hangout in ten minutes is already one step ahead in building the sort of connections the team’s face can exploit. Their spatial and psychological awareness of how people act and react may extend beyond the vehicle. Someone who can navigate and pilot has

planning, logistical, and strategy skills that could make them a team leader. Knowing what deckers can and can’t do makes them useful in coordinating active operations. They could even _be_ the decker. That function isn’t just a manner of manipulating GridGuide or remote vehicles. It’s also knowing what information to collect from one’s environment and what disinformation to feed to opponents. Stoppos and stealth couriers who use and make “sleeper” vehicles are working on the same principles as someone who dons a ruthenium sneak suit or a full body disguise. The racing rigger with a thieving background is also going to have some sick infiltration and electronics skills to bypass security.

There are racing riggers who are adepts, magicians, technomancers, and miscellaneous because the need for speed doesn’t drive just mundanes (or metahumans). Naturally, utility and split characters aren’t going to be as good as dedicated riggers on paper. However, racing riggers, like the other characters in this book, are not just stats and gear. They are people with intelligence, motivations, experiences, and biases. The elements that make strong racing riggers aren’t just how to shave nanoseconds off the route from A to B. The racing rigger gets from A to B in a world with pedestrians, cops, and Mother Nature. The racing rigger can be anyone: from the lithe elven street racer or the stereotypical dwarven tinkerer to the troll getaway driver.

The heart of a racing rigger is piloting vehicles, so they should do that will both skill and strategy. Going fast is the easy part—just hit the accelerator and let the machine go. The real trick is knowing how to use that speed. If you were racing on some salt flats, pure speed would be enough, but that usually isn’t the case. There are turns, corners, other vehicles, weather effects, and more. Knowing how to anticipate these elements and what others will do in them is critical.

It’s also important to know what vehicles can and cannot do. Know if you have more speed, better acceleration, or better handling than the opposition, and then be prepared to exploit your advantage. Be prepared to mess with others’ expectations—go slower than your max speed for a while, only to kick it higher when the time is right. Watch the habits of the people chasing you (or that you’re chasing). Do they gun it into corners or pause to get a clear view of what is ahead of them? Do they avoid alleys? How much regard do they show for others’ lives? Knowing all this will help you guess where other vehicles are going, and you can then try to get there first.

Information is important. A rigger tapped into GridGuide cameras will beat one driving blind every time. A t-bird pilot who can get a quick aerial look at the canyon they’re going to fly through is better served than one who is just reacting. Knowas much about what is going on around you as you can, and use that knowledge well.

The most important piece of advice for racing riggers is the one they already know: Ignore fear. Push your vehicle to its limits, rocket ahead, and let any nerves you feel amp up your energy instead of slow you down.

The best part about playing racing riggers is that they tend to be idiosyncratic. The racing rigger is not going to be a hyper-minimalist, antisocial loner. They are going to have totems, rituals and customs, personal styles, and social presence. The racing rigger spend a lot of time in their vehicle, and that space had better be as physically and mentally comfortable or familiar as possible given the amount of time they spend and the work they do in it. The life of a person whose speciality comes from repetition and multiple iterations of the same tasks in the same spaces is going come with certain behavioral quirks and mannerisms. This is why, like athletes, the racing rigger is prone to totems and rituals and superstitions. Play this up, because it can be light or can be the inspiration for a run with deadly consequences where things go sideways because the ritual was broken.
