## The Corp Operative

If there are any groups fully aware of the power of augmentations, it’s the people who make them. If a corp makes cyberware and bioware—and even if they don’t—they are sure to have some cyber ninjas on staff. There is no quicker path to your first set of synaptic boosters than signing up with corp security. Of course, they don’t give that prize to just anyone. You have to show considerable physical skill even without the bioware, and you have to show stability and loyalty to your corporate benefactor. The last thing they want is to give a few hundred grand worth of augmentations to someone only to have to face that someone in the shadows.

And yet that keeps happening. The corps are pretty good at building a soldier, but they’re poor at maintaining loyalty—mainly because they think it should only be received, not given. So they keep falling into their bad habit of hanging their people out to dry, or double-crossing them when it suits their needs, and they keep being surprised when the people they screwed end up in the shadows.

Putting all that corp ’ware to work in the shadows is great, because seeing that much high-grade gear in a runner is rare. You’ll stand out, in a good way, but you need to be careful. If too many people start talking about you and your gear, word might get back the the corp you abandoned, and they might decide that a little repossession is in order.

It gets even better if you make a special-ops team. These soldiers get the good stuff, and their modus operandi fits in well with how cyber ninja do business. You become what opposing forces fear, especially the decentralized ones—the shot in the dark, the blade in the night. You can get in anywhere and find anyone.

But eventually, enlistments end. The ’ware you received is tough to remove, plus you earned some medals and commendations in your years of service, so they let you take the augmentations with you. So you hit the streets with the skills and the ’ware to do some damage. All you need to do is figure out in what direction you should be pointed.

More than a few former spec ops soldiers find their way into private security and mercenary forces, but a few of them feel another call. Sometimes it’s because they’re tired of taking orders and working in a structure, and they just want to do their own thing. Other times, though, it’s because to them, it seems to be the best way to carry on the ideals they picked up in the military. Military life gets tied up in the murk of international politics and chain of command weirdness, but some soldiers manage to carry the ideal of defending the defenseless all through their military career. The shadows, to them, seems to be the best place to carry out this work. Who else is going to look out for the people in the barrens? The cops? The corps? Not likely. So you decide it should be you.
