## Magical Nudges

I have it on good authority that someone’s posting some of the latest and greatest state of the magical arts to help in your run, but wielding mana is more than just slinging spells and reading auras. Sure, once you’re onsite and especially when the bullets start flying, it’s important. There are, however, many things a mage can do that prevent a hostile situation altogether. It’s always better to do your research than to worry about catching lead.

Magical security can be a real bear of a problem, with wards, spirits, watchers, and various spells. Worse yet is most of your team can’t see any of it. If none of your chummers are Awakened, you’re just setting yourself up for failure. And your mage or shaman needs to be one of the folks doing some recon on the facility to have a good handle on things for later. Knowing where the spirits are camping is going to make it that much easier to avoid them. Same with alchemical preps on door handles. That one got us once—and seriously burned my hand.

So, yes, the first part is just getting in to see if there’s a ward on a door, or if the watcher sits above the reception desk or in the loading dock. But beyond that, if your mage is good at reading auras, they’ll be able to pick up the astral signatures of the caster or summoner. That piece of information can be useful if you want to keep the mage busy while the team is active on the site.

Another nice trick I have is to strike a ward in a spot I have no interest in as a diversion. We spot all the mana barriers in the place, determine the ones we’ll need to deal with, and then when we go in, we send a spirit after the door that’s farthest from what we want to hit. That only works if your mage can pass unnoticed through the other wards, because if you set off two alarms, you’re still going to deal with guards coming your way.

The other use for your Awakened teammate is to add a little magical assistance while trying to influence and win over others before the run. Having information from employees, gangers, or anybody else in your way is going to be useful, but sometimes they’re tight-lipped about it. Given enough time, a face can build really strong connections with those people, but time is often not a luxury we have. So what if there was a little magic to help get and keep their attention? Maybe it makes them feel a bit lonely so they’re receptive when I show up at the bar with a drink in hand—maybe then they’re willing to talk. And that’s just one tool in your mana-powered tool belt to make planning just a bit easier.

You can prep for it, however. The first thing to know is where the big dogs are operating out of, and which way they’re coming from. The second is knowing what will push the locals to call for HTR or their local gangs for help. There’s value in going in light when you know it will delay the moment that call is made.

To the first point, if you discover the heavies are offsite and always arrive through the helipad, you can set up a little surprise for them, or render that pad inoperable early in the run. If they like to come in via the big doors on the gang-controlled warehouse, then welding them shut ahead of time will have the same effect. It gives you a reprieve, and that’s going to be a blessing when all hell breaks loose.

The second one is just preventive maintenance. The bigger the boom you’re carrying, the more likely you’ll trigger an overwhelming response. Better to bring just the right amount of force required and run away when you can—delay when you can’t. As soon as you go loud, the job’s effectively over and all that matters is getting the heck out of dodge. The way I’ve typically dealt with this is by having a small insertion team, but a couple people for extraction in case you expect the drek to hit the fan. This can delay calls to those nasty threat response units until the end of the run, and hopefully you can get far enough away before it really becomes a problem.
