## **Resources**

**Most megacorp CEOs are staggeringly wealthy, possessing the kind of money that you or I can only dream of. Bishop’s different. Oh, he’s rich—you don’t stay in the executive game for two decades without getting rich—but we’re talking millions, not billions. The kind of money that gets you a nice car, a big house, and in his case a massive wardrobe, but not superyachts or private islands. His previous position gets him some stock options, and being CEO will give him far more, but as it stands, he owns less than one percent of one percent of the corporation’s stocks, a far cry from the heady days of Knight being able to do whatever he wanted due to having an effective majority. This means that, at the end of the day, Bishop is an employee of the Ares corporation, not the owner. He’s accountable to the board of directors, and he can’t just use the corp’s resources for whatever he wants. He has to produce shareholder value, and while they’re likely to give him some wiggle room for a year or two, to get the system established, he’s on the clock—they want their investments to pay off, and he gets the blame if they don’t. This means that he has access to Ares assets, like private jets, a security detail, bleeding-edge medical care, and he can call on top operatives to get things done,**

**but any or all of it can be taken away by the board if they’re misused.**

**In terms of his own private resources, details are less well known. Until his (edited) backstory was released, the man was a cipher who held his cards close at all times. He doesn’t have a family legacy to draw from, no network of contacts from an Ivy League education, no magical talent, no elite military training, no decking skills. He’s not even particularly brilliant. Bishop isn’t a chess master, ten moves ahead and operating a dozen plans at once in case one fails. So what does he have? Moxie.**

**Bishop has an ability to speak with confdence and the willpower to get things done when he puts his mind to it. There’s a phrase that you might have heard bandied about in the past that goes something like, “If I can walk, and I can talk, who’s to say that I can’t deliver?” Bishop has a knack for understanding what you want, offering it to you, then giving it to you at the agreed-upon price without post-negotiation. Did it cost him more than he gained? Maybe, but he doesn’t pass that cost on to you; you had an agreement, and a deal’s a deal. This builds up reputation, and people start to know that he’s someone you can count on. He negotiates frm but fair, and he sticks to it. It’s a core code of honor that stands out in a world full of people who’ll stab you in the back just to get one rung higher on the ladder. People respect that, and he uses those people to build out a network of contacts and operatives he can rely on in return. He doesn’t** _**need**_ **to be an ace sniper, programmer, shaman, billiards ace, and apiarist—he knows people in those felds who are, and who he can count on to share those abilities when he calls them up.**

- **That, my friends, is a damn good face. When you learn how to network, you learn the true definition of force multiplication. Why spend months learning how to kayak, decorate cakes, and do dental surgery when you can have other people learn it and loan you those skills when you need them? You only have twenty-four hours in a day. Get ten people in your network and you have a collective 240. Get a hundred? Over two thousand hours in a day. One decker prodigy might give you as much use as ten, but a hundred? A thousand? The numbers add up.**

**> Haze**

- **The diversity is key. Any thug on the street can break somebody’s knees or wave a gun around, but what if I need someone who’s an expert scuba diver? Someone who worked in the meat packing industry and can drop industry terminology smoothly for an infiltration? An expert in mechanical locks? Faces, fixers, and Mr. Johnsons all sink or swim based on who they know and what those people know. Your job is to assemble a team with the skills to get it done, and you have to trust them to do that job without watching over or sitting on their shoulder every step of the way.**
