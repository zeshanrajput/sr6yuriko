## Backgrounds

**>** Magic begins with a dream, and it dies when a runner wakes up to the reality of it all. The folks in the Fifth World used to say you had to sell your soul to the devil if you wanted to master the arcane arts, and even if they didn’t know a damn thing about what it takes to reach across the astral plane or make the laws of physics into a set of loose options, they were onto something with that little gem. But it ain’t just one devil you’re gonna have to pay—it’s a legion. But it all starts with a dream of fireballs and fairy dust. A few years later you’re nothing but a fried husk of flesh and bag of expired grey matter. If you’re lucky.

Doesn’t matter what little black path you decide to dance on down, magic takes sacrifice. You might want to be the master of your own destiny, but the truth is you’re gonna end up a slave before all is said and done. Maybe you find a totem that trades power for impossible obligations. Perhaps someone with a cheap suit and an expensive smile offers you a corporate sponsorship and you become a wage mage slinging spells for

the greater good of the corp. Sometimes it’s a master-andapprentice relationship where service in the flesh gets traded for the will to power. Doesn’t matter.

The real truth of it is that no one put a Predator to your head and forced you to learn this stuff. Magic and all the power that comes from it is an addiction worse than anything you can find on the street or the sweetest BTL. And it’s never enough, and it always comes at a price. There’s no way out, except the chrome. Rip away enough of your humanity, enough of what makes you an actual living thing, and that itch in the back of your brain, those dreams of flying across a penumbra more endless than reality itself, and those strange whispers that never quite escape your ears—it falls silent just long enough for you to scrape together a few more years before your mind finally collapses in on itself. Keep cramming in chrome, chummer. Maybe science can outrun sorcery all the way to the grave and back again. **>** Scorch

The burned-out mage is cynical, even for a shadowrunner. Whether they started as a corporate-sponsored wage mage, a shamanic true believer, a streetBACKGROUNDSwise sorcerer, or any other of the millions of arcane idealists with an excess of hope and a bounced check full of talent, it all came crashing down somehow, somewhere. The cost of magic was just too much for the burned-out mage. Maybe it was a blackened psyche, a broken body, endless nightmares, or just seeing one too many nightmares in the astral plane. Whatever the case, the burned-out mage lost some of the power they had worked so hard to build, then gave up what was left for something better. They turned to something easy. Something cold, hard, and far more reliable: chrome.

All that study, all those rituals, all that exhaustion took its toll. And when the burned-out mage saw Johnny Chrome moving faster than the blink of an eye while dancing past bullets like they were standing still to weave a dance of death clean enough to cut paychecks, they realized they were being played. Magic, at least at its lower levels, is for chumps. For many burned-out mages, by the time they figure this out, they’re already in too deep and the rush of magic as it courses through the bloodstream and the soul has become an addiction. Others throw it off without a second thought, shoving in cyberware as fast as their credstick will allow.

As they surrender flesh and blood for steel and circuitry, those years of magical training and natural talent start to slip away but are tough to fully shake. They often become something that’s not quite as fast as a street samurai or as skilled as a mage. They’re a wraith walking the razor’s edge of technology and thaumaturgy that epitomizes the Sixth World. They’re everything and nothing all at once, and they’ve got the scars to prove it.

Because they have no true specialty and they often appear to be past their prime, it’s easy for most of the rest of the SINless of the Sixth World to dismiss the burned-out mage as a has-been best put out to pasture or given the kind of mercy only found in the instant after the bullet leaves the chamber. But the truth is that while the burned-out mage is no specialist, they combine machine and magic in ways few others can. This, combined with the cynical awareness of someone who has been running the shadows for a long time, makes them capable of something more dangerous than traditional runners: the unexpected.

The burned-out mage has been around the block a time or two and knows a bum deal when they see it. They’re wise to the ways of magic beyond the traditional mantra of “Geek the mage!” that is often the extent of what most mundane runners call an arcane education. They’re loaded full of enough chrome to pull off a miracle or two inside those broken bodies. This combination of real-world experience and earned abilities makes them excellent leaders or advisors to groups of less-experienced runners and allows them to more easily react to the unexpected. If there’s something you need done, the

burned-out mage has a finger in that pie and can probably get it done. Probably.

For all their vast array of abilities, the burnedout mage is weary from years of scorching themselves away in both mind and body. This, coupled with having part of their very essence stripped away as they become more and more machine with each upgrade, can leave them feeling increasingly fragmented and broken. This means they do not have the high level of focused training of other shadowrunners necessary to be true experts in any field.

Ironically, this lack of expertise feeds into a fear found in most burned-out mages: that they’re just not good enough. They couldn’t cut it as spell-slingers, and though the road to arcane power isn’t any easy one, one only needs to walk down the street to see that practitioners across all walks of life found a way to this road that has closed to the burned-out mage. Somewhere along the line, they lost what they had and couldn’t chase it anymore. Like so much of the Sixth World, their path was replaced with something shiny and chrome. As ashamed as they may be of their failure as magicians, many burned-out mages are more ashamed of the alternative they chose.

With their shame sewn into their skin and flowing through their veins, many burned-out mages look for a way out. Addiction is common among their kind. From the bottle to the BTL, more than one has burned away not from the magic in their veins but from the needle in their arm. Still others go around with a chip on their shoulder, desperate to prove to themselves and everyone around them that they’re good enough to make it in the shadows. They risk impossible odds or take jobs no one else will just for one last shot at the glory they’ve been denied all these years. Many die in the process.

There are a rare few burned-out mages who manage to look past their own cynicism and find some shred of hope for the next generation or even the future of the Sixth World. They’ll never admit it, but hooding or supporting the heroics of neo-anarchist movements like Black Star, the Ork Underground, or even the Ghoul Liberation League gives them a sense of purpose. Maybe it’s a lost soul searching for a lost cause to serve or a simple death wish. Perhaps it’s a guilty conscience that comes standard issue with running in the shadows, but burned-out mages who hood are true believers.

Some of them use those years of experience and the tricks they’ve learned from living on both sides of reality to become surprisingly capable leaders of younger, less experienced shadowrunners. They’ve walked countless miles of bad road with nothing to show for it but broken dreams, and they see damned idiot kids who are setting themselves up for the same fate. The majority of burned-out mages are human, with their impatience and desire for quick and easy power without consequences or discipline. Going from magic to machine is like lighting a stick of dynamite at both ends, and humans of the Sixth World (and every era before it) have a natural talent for self-destruction. But they by no means have the market cornered when it comes to regret and over-compensation. Metahumanity in all its variety seems to have a talent for slow, inevitable self-destruction.

Dwarfs who burn out often seem to be taken by surprise. Their focus and determination often don’t allow them to consider the possibility that they could burn out, so when they do, it takes them some time to adapt. Usually, though, when it’s time to commit to a new path, they do it fully. This is not to say that they avoid the cynical downward spiral that is common among burned-out mages. In fact, their tendency to be dour seems to make such bitterness inevitable as their magical struggles push them to new indignities from multiple vectors of the Sixth World.

Elves are, by far, the rarest metahuman species to become burned-out mages. Such base bitterness and unrefined dabbling seems antithetical to their sensibilities. Elvish pride seems to forbid it, but nevertheless it does happen. Elves who become burned-out mages often do so because of some great expectation set before them (or that they set upon themselves) that they were unable to meet. After having proven themselves unworthy of being called a true mage or shaman, the elf escapes into cybernetics as an “easy out.” Unfortunately each so-called upgrade is a permanent mark of shame of how they just weren’t good enough when the time came to prove themselves. While cynicism seems to be the stock and trade of the burned-out mage, elves tend to channel this into bitter anger and often seem like agile, anxious predators gracefully prowling their cage, waiting for any opportunity to strike out with both old spells and new chrome—outward manifestations of scars that run deeper than most other metahumans can possibly imagine.

Orks make up a surprising number of burnedout mages. They understand more than most other metahumans what it means to be tolerated, but never truly accepted. Though orks can become skilled magicians, it is not an easy path for them, and frustration comes easily to the ork. That frustration turns inward, and before long the longing for quick and easy power can easily lead to a quick piece of chrome or two. Before long, the ork begins to feel trapped between worlds. As magic and chrome fight for supremacy, the ork burned-out mage becomes filled with regret and bitterness as once again they never truly belong to either world.



Perhaps it is ironic that troll burned-out mages often seem the least scarred or broken among the many metahumans of this archetype. Most trolls who go down this path to power actively chose it. The path of magic is not something that comes naturally to a lot of trolls, so they focus their efforts and intentions on mastering a few specific spells or incantations. Once a level of expertise has been achieved, they then turn to cybernetic augmentation to enhance their already naturally exceptional physical abilities. The ability to summon the occasional spirit or pop off a manabolt in the moment is all they were ever really looking for to begin with, so relying on chrome and sheer, unsubtle brutality as their primary means of finishing the job at hand is as natural to them as breathing. Sure, they might not have as much metal as a fully augmented samurai or the sorcery of a dedicated shaman, but regardless of where their power comes from it’s still their power and no one can take it away. In short, troll burned-out mages revel in the independence brought to them by this unique path to power. Sure, it might all burn to the ground in the end, but the troll will be the one to strike the match.
