## Loyalty and Favor Points

When the scene involves an interaction between the player characters and their contacts, knowing the contact’s ambitions and desires will guide how they approach the situation. In most dealings, the NPC isn’t going to have an opinion about the outcome of any given work or job the PCs are conducting, it’s simply a transaction of favors or nuyen—or the contact is showing kindness and/or loyalty. However, if the contact has a vested interest in the outcome, they might skew the accuracy of the information they give to the runner team by varying degrees, or it could be completely misleading. That degree comes down to how loyal the contact is and any outstanding favors they owe.

A contact’s loyalty to a character depends on several factors. Let’s say a character has worked with a particular bartender repeatedly for the last few months, that character will likely have a certain understanding of whether the contact will sell them out or not for some quick nuyen. This is measured by the contact’s Loyalty rating, unless you’re using the rules under For Gamemaster Eyes Only: Contacts and Corruption. If the contact has a high Loyalty ( _Sixth World Companion_ , p. 174), then any information they give the player will likely be more accurate. For gamemasters, deciding whether a contact is going to be dependable or fully truthful will vary based on several factors. But, in most cases, any contact with a Loyalty rating higher than 8 is going to be completely dependable unless they’ve been compromised.

Loyalty isn’t the only way to determine how a contact will deal with a player character. A gamemaster should also consider any Favor Points the contact has. While a given contact may have a low Loyalty rating, if they owe you a bunch of

favors, they may opt to make good on those to balance the sheets. The contact may share more information than they normally would (p. 174, _Sixth World Companion, Runners Spending Favor Points_ ) when the character spends those favors. In addition, the contact could indicate they have information that’s worth a lot to the player character, and by cashing in an owed favor, they’ll share the information. For example, a player character’s contact has a heated rivalry with another black-market weapons dealer and would love to see the runners take a bite out of the rival’s business. Depending on how much this hurts the rival, they may grant Favor Points. The flip side is also true—if the contact is an ally of whoever the player character is investigating, it would require the character to spend Favor Points for the contact to divulge information about their ally.

In situations where giving out the information helps them, contacts are more likely to give teams the information they need without spending any Favor Points. Depending on how effective the team is at helping the contact, the team may earn additional Favor Points.

There are also some contacts who will never have a high Loyalty rating with members of the team. Maybe they want to keep that relationship at a professional level, or they have some inability to trust people working in the shadows. A great way to determine how loyal a contact can be is to flesh out more of their personality. We’ve included the Questionnaire for NPC contacts to assist you in adding more context to your contacts—and from there, you can set a maximum Loyalty rating based on the Loyalty Rating Table ( _Sixth World Companion_ , p. 174).
