## Research Forever

The NYU Medical School host is sleek and modern, with lots of polished metal and inlaid lighting. It prioritizes making people and places, especially classrooms, labs, and professors, easy to find. It presents the illusion that everything is on the surface and plain to see.

Brinkvale clues the PCs to a secret. Word is there is a way to access a secret lab from the faculty lounge in the host. Further rumors say the e-ghosts of some professors inhabit that lab. That is the place to look.

The faculty Matrix lounge is an attractive, modernist space with large windows that regularly shift their views—one hour it’s the Manhattan skyline, then a mountain stream, then a white-sand beach, and so on. At any time of day, about half the seats in the lounge are occupied, and about a quarter of those occupants are actually defender sprites. Which means the PCs will have to tread carefully.

The faculty lounge is actually a separate host from the rest of the Medical School. The secret lab is a third host, connected only to the lounge host.

The first trick is noticing the hidden host, which requires an Electronics + Intuition vs. Firewall + Sleaze test (the entrance to the host is disguised as a filing cabinet against one wall). Then the PCs will need to hack it to gain User access to enter the lab.

The virtual lab is not your classic sterile beakerand-Bunsen-burner-filled room. Instead, it’s quite like the faculty lounge, but with more desks and AR displays. It also has a different vibe, as people walk back and forth, looking at results and talking to one another, rather than just relaxing. They initially won’t pay much attention to the PCs, as they figure anyone who enters is supposed to be there.

The PCs should quickly notice someone who matches the description Brinkvale gave them of Dr. Sonya Peters (straight black hair cut short, 1.2 meters tall, narrow brown eyes). The job after recognizing her is convincing her to talk to them, as she is far more interested in her work than in talking to strangers. Here are some approaches the PCs might try:

- **Discuss neuroscience with her.** Dr. Peters is a top-flight mind, so PCs will not just bluff their way through the conversation, but PCs with any sort of neurology-related Knowledge skill can make an Influence (Etiquette) + Charisma (4) test to start a conversation that interests Dr. Peters. Once they start talking to her, they should be able to steer the conversation to their topic of interest.

- **Threaten her.** Threatening an e-ghost with harm is difficult, as they no longer have physical bodies that can receive pain. They can threaten de-rezzing, but any such threat will lead Dr. Peters to immediately call security. Which is to say, threats are a bad idea.

- **Con her into giving information.** Whether they present themselves as someone in authority or as an old friend, the PCs could try to persuade Dr. Peters they’re someone she should talk to. This requires a Con + Charisma vs. Intuition + Willpower test, and the PCs need two net hits to fully persuade Dr. Peters to talk.

- **Hack her files for information.** This is a challenge, as Dr. Peters doesn’t have the information the PCs want just sitting out in a file. Still, if they can gain Admin access in the lab host, they can attempt a Hash Check action (Electronics + Logic [4]) to find thirty-two documents that might have useful information. Decrypting them later will provide the information described below.

The PCs can come up with other ways to convince Dr. Peters as long as the gamemaster determines it’s reasonable. In all of this, remember to keep track of the PCs’ Overwatch Score. The PCs can’t just sit in the hidden lab all day. The main



goal is to show Dr. Peters the files from the Octagon and see if she recognizes anything about them.

The PCs are in luck—Dr. Peters recognizes the work being done. She was approached by some people who wanted to do similar work a year ago (just before her death, as it turns out). They referred to their operation as Project Anima, a name the PCs might recognize. She says they were from the UCAS government—if she didn’t miss her guess, they were CIA. After reviewing some of their proposals, she turned them down, believing the speed of their research did not leave sufficient room for ethics. She remembers, though, that they described the effort as a “public-private partnership.” They did not want to disclose anything about their private investors, but she had a commcode she was supposed to call if it turned out she was interested. She gives it to the runners.
