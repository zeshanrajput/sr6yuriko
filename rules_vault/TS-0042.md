## **Corp v. Corp**

**Let me recount to you an event, one that I was personally privy to while I was mucking about in the Seattle Metroplex. I’ll need to be vague with details because I don’t want a knock on my door in the dead of night. But the story is worth telling.**

But regardless they’re hopped up and ready to roll. The gangers get it in their heads that they’re going to hit a major street during rush hour. As far as they see it, they’ve got a captive group of prey, stuck in traffc and ripe for the picking. Not only that, but the stalled traffc should theoretically block the badges from responding and allow for a**

Unfortunately, these geniuses failed to recognize the four Lone Star patrol cruisers caught up in the traffc as well. A shootout ensues. **It was a frst-class goat rodeo.**

**Soon after the shooting had started, it was clear that the Stars were losing the battle. Severely outgunned and down at least half their original numbers, they were in a bad way. To make matters even worse, these offcers seemed to take their job seriously. There were numerous reports of them prioritizing the avoidance of collateral damage by attempting to evacuate civvies and not spraying bullets down range. A welcome rarity, as heartening as it was tragic. Through sheer numbers, the gangers were able to overpower the ambushed offcers, killing all of them, and began reaving their way through the traffc, killing indiscriminately, either out of bloodlust or to simply expedite matters.**

**Backup soon arrived, every one of them champing at the bit to get revenge for their fallen comrades. Another shootout breaks out, even bigger than the frst. A lot more people die, cops, gangers,** _**and**_ **civilians. Stars on scene are screaming through the radio waves now, asking for any and all assistance to come to their aid, what we refer to in the business as “mutual aid.” To make a long story short, the gun battle rages on for an extended period of time and moves from the streets into a McHugh’s that was adjacent to the carnage. The frefght only ended when the Seattle Metroplex Guard rolled up in force and put the rest of the gang into the ground.**

**A sad story, right? A bleak tale of how the depredations of a few can claim so many lives, and in just the blink of an eye. But you didn’t read this for a sob story, you see those all the time. So what’s the point of this, you ask? The answer to that lies within a detail I omitted, purposefully, from this little yarn. Less than a hundred yards away from the epicenter of this catastrophe, there was a satellite research facility owned by Aztechnology. It was staffed with a cadre of Leopard Guard protectors, who stood by and watched the battle unfold.**

**The extremely skilled group of Aztechnology’s corporate killers could have put a swift end to the brutality that the street cops were struggling to contain, but it wasn’t in the interest of their**

**corporation. And so, they didn’t act. There are rumors that the Leopards killed a Lone Star offcer who attempted to enter their facility—without authorization—to enlist their aid. But this can’t be confrmed, and my lips are sealed on the matter.**

**Never forget, corps only care about what they’re paid to care about. Megacorp forces will never cross the jurisdictional lines drawn up for their contracts, and they’ll never waste ammunition on helping anyone they aren’t paid to. Corporate enforcers are more consumed with enforcing their boundaries, and they’d throw their own grandmothers into a woodchipper if it meant that the corporation’s borders remained secure. Take note: If you can escape from one corporation’s boundaries into another’s, you’re home free.**

**There was a time in history, in the old world, where law enforcement was a universal concept. If a criminal did evil and fed, it didn’t matter where said reprobate fed to, they’d be pursued. Police departments, sherif’s ofces, constabularies, state police, federal police: it didn’t matter which badge you wore in the old world; you were united in purpose. This was an intimidating proposition to many prospective lawbreakers; the surety of pursuit and punishment is an incredibly efective deterrent. But this is the old world’s reality, not our reality.**

**The climate of the Sixth World’s security and law enforcement industries is diferent. Corporations are paid to oversee a specifc area of a metroplex, nothing more and nothing less. These corporations, like all corporate entities, exist solely to generate proft for their shareholders. This, of course, extends to their public security divisions as well. There’s zero proft in assisting competitors in neighboring sections of the metroplex, regardless of how much carnage is being wrought.**

**As an example, Knight Errant forces have zero reason to reinforce a Lone Star position under fre. Not only would this be a potential loss of Knight Errant operators, who represent a substantial investment in training and equipment, it would be an unproftable endeavor and therefore an unconscionable one. In many respects it would actually be benefcial for these hypothetical Knights to stand by, watch Lone Star operatives die, and for the violence to spread. As long as it doesn’t creep into KE’s borders or threaten any of their contract holders, it could be a boon to the security ofcers’ managers. It would make Knight Errant’s sectors seem safer, which could lead to more contracts down the road. The elimination of competition, in this case literally, is always worthwhile.**

**Most corporations and LEPs go so far as to have incredibly strict regulations against assisting anyone who does not reside within the confnes of the contracted areas of protection. Breaking these regulations could lead to termination and other, harsher, penalties for security staf. The corporate powers have disincentivized altruism. Quite an amazing feat if you ask me.**
