## Controlling the Chrome

## Posted by: Black Knight

I was asked by the triumvirate to talk a little bit about how law enforcement deals with heavily augmented criminals. Not to be glib, but a lot of time the office is just going to put the perp down. Sure there is a bunch of paperwork, but when

you’ve got a troll sporting the hottest in muscle replacements and titanium bone lacing, most restraint setups are going to be hard pressed to keep them in cuffs.

The longer answer is more complex. Dealing with a cyberfreak isn’t that different from dealing with some adepts. In fact, with chrome lovers, you have a lot more tools at your disposal. We can use restraints on many inmates because they’re not sporting chrome specific to strength and instead using a wide variety of ’ware. The second control aspect we use is to take ownership of the augmentation and just turn it off. This method, however, doesn’t work on a lot of folks who got their augs through the black market, since their ownership structures have usually been hacked and the public safety features have been removed. We can also use a decker to go in and shut things down, but the constant oversight on these criminals can hinder your work and cut into your profitability. Luckily, someone invented cyber-jammers, but some internal routing can bypass these controls. This leaves two other techniques, both of which are more drastic.

The first of those is the installation of kill switches or kink bombs. Usually, we rig it up through the activate circuit. The kill switch just automatically turns off the augmentation immediately on activation. It’s pretty simple, and the nice bit is that once they’ve paid their debt, we can remove it without too much of a fuss. Kink bombs are usually reserved for the most dangerous inmates. It’s typically rigged as a six-second countdown, and if they don’t turn it off themselves, then they’re just meat for the recyclers. This usually keeps things quiet and safe for the guards. But for the inmates, I’ve seen a bunch of folks who are willing to take that bomb-timer gamble to take out a rival.

If it’s safe and cost-effective to do so, we’ll just go in and remove the cyberware. If the cybered-up criminal isn’t going to get out of prison, why do they need that gear? It’s no coincidence that the for-profit corrections industry establishes extraterritoriality for its prisons—the usual laws preserving property and bodily autonomy rights can be legally exempted as the corp sees fit. Besides, it’s common practice to resell or repurpose the removed components. Where did you think the cyber that goes into street cops comes from?

There are times when cyberware must be disabled, but outright removal isn’t feasible for economic or legal reasons. Cybernetic restraining collars are commonly employed by bounty hunters and used in pre-trial confinement. Such restraints are reasonably easy to remove, but brightly colored so that their absence is immediately apparent to their custodians. Then again, it can be hard for a detainee in the county lockup to get their hands on the necessary tools to remove such an inhibitor. Another common circumstance where cyberware is inhibited is in the commercial aerospace travel industry. Just




as a civilian can’t carry a firearm into a cabin, a razorboi can’t be allowed to push cyberspurs through the aircraft’s hull. But unlike a firearm, cyberware can’t just be placed into checked baggage. Instead, when dangerous cyberware is detected by preflight security scanners, in order to board the flight the user must accept a bit of code into their cyberware that disables the offending augmentations, until security personnel can remove it upon disembarkation. Of course, what sorts of augmentations are requested to be locked out can vary between corporate and national jurisdictions.
