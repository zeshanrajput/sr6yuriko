## Business 

The primary reason the Corporate Court exists is that chaos is bad for business. Omaha’s in chaos, so a lot of business left. And while you won’t see any logos of the Big Ten plastered on any buildings, that doesn’t mean they’ve entirely fled. Aztechnology still exists in town through Stuffer Shack and Productos Cultivados, Evo’s Pensodyne has an office in the city, and Shiawase has a newer subsidiary called ProsperGro with a growing office. On the next level down, AG Chemie and Zeta-ImpChem have a lot of workers in town, and Maersk brings a lot of things in and out. On the local level, the venerable Berkshire Hathaway continues to manage its considerable investment portfolio, and relative newcomer Stabler NanoGro has a fertilizer line that is helping rejuvenate some of the exhausted farmland in the area. 

The business climate in the sprawl had been pretty collegial, as the corps located here viewed their completion more as other sprawls rather than each other. As the structure of the sprawl changes, though, the corps have started looking out for themselves more. Stabler NanoGro kicked things off, acquiring a farm equipment manufacturer and seed distributor in fairly aggressive fashion in recent months. ProsperGro already sees them as a rival, and their executives are looking at Stabler’s acquisitions as a gauntlet being thrown down.

One of the monkey wrenches in this whole setup are the independent farmers. Some of the larger agricorps sold off their land recently, looking at getting out of the area before things declined more. Independent farmers bought up the land, and they are known for their contrary streaks. Both Stabler and ProsperGro have strict controls over how their materials can be used, and the independent farmers have set about violating those as much as they can. They’re sharing seeds in ways that break their contracts and using tech that turns off the kill switches in Stabler’s nanofertilizer so that it keeps replicating instead of dying off and needing re-application. So far the corps haven’t done anything about this, but if the pace of contract-breaking continues, expect them to take firm action.
