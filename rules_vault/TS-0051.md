## **Same Drek, Different Day**

**For the most part though, being a beat cop is a job. A steady bi-weekly paycheck with okay benefts. The hours suck, and the bosses can**

**be grade-A jerks. But it’s a job where you don’t have to sit plugged into the Matrix all day or pretend you’re working at some kind of desk. If you’re lucky, you may get to have a rank like “Sergeant” or “Lieutenant.” But really, it’s pretty interchangeable with “Assistant Manager” and “Manager.” It’s more of a holdover and a formality because of the history attached to the job. Because law enforcement is still technically a civil service job, there is still a perception that once someone gets hired on, they’re set, as long as they can make it to retirement.**

**> He’s not kidding when he says for some of these jerks it can be a power trip. We had a guy who used to run with us, all chromed out and slicker than hot drek on ice. The problem was, he couldn’t work with the plan, and had to do things his way. He went in too early and tripped a silent Matrix alarm. Almost got our decker fried, and I ended up catching three bullets in my side from an all-too-ready HTR team. We kicked him from the team, and lo and behold, two months later he was in a Lone Star uniform guarding a district courthouse in the background while some political talking head blathered on about something on trideo. Couldn’t cut it in the shadows, so decided to go work for the law.**

**> Ironworker**

**As with any job, how things are done depends on the employer. In the LEP business, it also depends on where you’re at. One corporation will have similar rules and regulations wherever it conducts business. After all, it’s still a multinational corporation. But the contracts with each city are going to largely defne the day-to-day work of the employees.**

**Take the recent shakeup in Seattle. For many years Lone Star held the security contract with the sprawl. Then in the early ’70s, Lone Star fraggedup with how they handled the tempo crisis and ended up losing the general law enforcement contract. They still administered the penitentiaries and some other minor services, but the bulk of day-to-day work went to the new contract holder, Knight Errant. The news made a big deal about it at the time, about how the new corporation was going to come in and change policies. And until they got a grip on things, anarchy would reign in the streets while the cops tried to fnd their own hoop with two hands and a map.**

**The fact was, most of those who worked the beat at the time obtained a new uniform and badge, watched some workers change some signs, and showed up early to their shifts to do the same fragging job.**

**Sure, KE used it as a political move to come in and clean house of some problematic individuals, but overall, it was business as usual. No one could spend the time to bring in and train an entirely new law enforcement contingent in the time it took to switch the callsign from LS to KE. So aside from perhaps bringing in a few higher-**
