## **> Haze**

**Even though psychometry is inadmissible in court (and for good reason), the information it reveals can help a case start in the right direction. Imagine you’re sent to investigate a wife brutally murdered during what looks like a textbook “home invasion gone wrong.” The husband was out of town on business, so (for once) you are not liking him for it. You’re lucky enough to have a colleague nearby who mastered psychometry, and they unveil to you that, despite appearances, the “killer had a strong, intense connection to the victim, it feels … very personal.”**

**Okay, not going to stop deep-diving into recent strings of burglaries and home invasions, but maybe it’s time to also triple-check the hubby’s alibi, search for jilted lovers, and maybe look into an estranged brother who happened to reconnect with her a few weeks ago. Yeah, it may be some extra work, but that’s fne as long as you get the right suspect.**

**> This method flirts dangerously with a person’s right to avoid self-incrimination (i.e., mainly remaining silent, or even lying). It does not, however, prevent investigators from obtaining or deducing perceptible elements. This means that psychometrics is an untainted source, which therefore allows the evidence it found to be used subsequently in court.**

**> Kay St. Irregular**

**Now, I’m not going to rant about what exactly psychometry can do. Apparently there’s an upload called** _**Street Wyrd**_ **that does all that for me. I will instead remind you that, more importantly, it’s not an exact science. Reads can be diffcult, misleading, and worst of all, wrong. Let’s take the previous example. Your Awakened partner felt a “strong personal connection,” right? But wouldn’t a stalker have an intense and emotional albeit oneway connection to the victim? Or worse, she may ft the profle of a serial killer ... or your coworker may simply have misread (or failed to describe correctly) this astral breadcrumb.**

**And here’s the kicker to prove my point. In this specifc case, my buddy from Dips really screwed up and completely misinterpreted the situation. What he thought was a strong connection was, in fact, traces of blood magic. The murder was solved by pure chance a few days later by the gang unit. They arrested a few street urchins behind a home invasion in the neighborhood, and the DNA left on the scene linked them to this murder. One of them was an adept, who recently became addicted to blood magic.**

- **Needless to say, organized crime and other felons who planned ahead would sell their own mothers half-price to have an Awakened detective on their payroll. As demonstrated by this example, they can really derail an investigation if they decided to “misread” the scene. And even if honest guys from the Dips come afterward and report something entirely different … well, who’s to trust? That’s why most security corps have to be very careful about using those assets and psychometry. They also assign cases to them as randomly as possible. I’ve even heard rumors that Lone Star’s Internal Affairs Division has a dedicated, secret unit specialized in detecting crooked Awakened officers within the DIPs.**
