## Sha Tin Districts: Racing, Scholars, and Stuffy People

Sha Tin of today is a place where Hong Kong’s more elite classes often go when they want to hobnob with one another and look down on the common people. Numerous gaming and entertainment houses are found along the Shing Mun River, including the Penfold Park and Pak Shek Kok resorts looking out over Tolo Harbour.

**Penfold Park** , on the Shing Mun River, offers racing fans all the thrills and has done so since before the Awakening. Gambling is legal in the HKFEZ, and there is no small amount of it here at Penfold. Box seats are stratospheric in price but come with all the bells and whistles one could imagine. Across the track from the family-friendly rides in the middle are the venerable Hong Kong Jockey Club and the Sha Tin Clubhouse.

- There are several stories about the Kanabo facility as well, with many residents claiming to have seen a large cat of some kind stalking the district’s streets and alleys at night.

The Wong Tai Sin Temple is a fascinating place, filled with heritage and cultural enrichment. Now over a hundred years old and part of a larger complex of gardens, shrines, and Taoist retreats, the area boasts large number of visitors every year, with nearly half a million visits from the citizens of Hong Kong alone.

- The main gate to the temple and its grounds is also a place where a confluence of energies ebb and flow throughout the day. It’s not a true ley line, but it reflects the beliefs and philosophies of the Tao. There is some truth that people who pass through the gate with a troubled mind or strong sense of self-conflict feel a release. More than a few people pass through the gate after their work shifts are over, which gives the area an almost constant metahuman presence.

- Just to let you know, there is a gentleman’s spa and sauna house here for members and their admitted guests. A lot of business activity is often discussed between men who like to languish in the illusion of corporate and cultural authority.

- Ma’Fan

- Don’t let the glitz confuse you—the district has a lot of hidden little gems to be found, and the area is crisscrossed with half a dozen different corporate security zones because of the various resorts and guests who use them. Yes, there are many shadowy things that take place, with extractions being the e-ticket operation of choice.

Where the Shing Mun River’s waters merge with those of Tolo Harbor, there are a number of small marinas servicing tourist yachts and a stretch of water that has come to be used for unofficial small watercraft racing, such as wetbikes and jet skis. The entire district is where fully onefifth of all Hong Kong residents come on commercial holidays.

Middle Kingdom has two stations dedicated to protecting the thoroughfares and rail service that shuttle persons back and forth.

There is an unspoken pact that firearms are not used when doing so, because stray shots would cause considerable collateral damage in the large crowds and are a sure-fire-way of attracting the Middle Kingdom or KE’s immediate attention.

Now for some updates on known locales. The **Kowloon Bay Typhoon Shelter** suffered a few years ago when a roach nest was found to be operating in a particularly dense cluster of older ships. Many people who once retreated here deSMALLparted, and those who stayed keep an uneasy watch on their remaining neighbors.

The **City Gate** on Lantau Island remains alive and as well as can be expected. Some innovative— and likely bored—residents decided one night to paint the entire community a variety of colors and themes in order to break the dulling white retro-deco. They were partially successful, but a lot of space above a second or third floor or below the top one or two rooftop layers were left unpainted. The end result is a strange new image of white blocks floating between layers of sun-cookies.

**Chop-Chop Shop** in Kowloon City remains a more affordable reminder that healthcare, like cyberware, doesn’t always come with everything somebody needs. The Black Chrysanthemums remain the main power behind this locale, and they continue to cut anyone who seeks to do anything about it.

**Dante’s Inferno** in the central part of Downtown is one of three in the world, and it is just as active as ever. If you can get to the subterranean levels, then you have access to a wide variety of entertainments, with dancing being the most basic but far from the only option. Or the most physically involved one.

**Noodleboy** in the Yau Tsim Mong district is as delicious as ever, with their options expanding over the years. The owner—no longer a boy, of course—has risen to the status of Hung Kwan and has taken to training multiple Cho Hai for his own association and others in order to create a stable network of go-betweens that are seen as honorable to all Triads, ensuring their travels are more secure.

**Kai Tak Night Market** is still in Kowloon City, and it remains one of the primary methods of smuggling goods or buying gear in Hong Kong, especially if you don’t mind secondhand brands or knock-off items. Trading is not difficult and still comes with some percentage of what it is you are buying, taking a tax at the time of your purchase or transaction. Trusted Kai Tak visitors can get in and out for free, and it’s always good to know one if you plan on doing something a

bit shadier and can’t afford working through the bigger boys.
