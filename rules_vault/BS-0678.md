## Nanotechnology

I hesitate to include this as its own branch. It remains my belief that nanotechnology is best described as a tool for the various branches of scientific advancement rather than a branch in and of itself. However, the emergence of monads and the impact that resilient techno-organisms have on our understanding of nanotech requires me to define it as a branch here. When CFD announced the arrival of monads in 2076, it set back nanite development by several decades. Due to security concerns, single-function nanites are now the basis of the nanotech industry. The differentiation between hard and soft nanites will expand as we continue to redesign the technology in accordance with updated regulations. The limited lifespan and external control systems inherent in modern nanotechnology, however, curtail the use of that breathtaking tech. Just as the world would’ve been different had steamtech not been shuttered in favor of a new, limiting direction, so will the world be revised when multi-function nanites are reintroduced to society. The question of security remains. The nature of self-replicating multi-functional nanites fundamentally exposes them to hacking on an atomic level. The question

is: How do you produce auxons that cannot be hacked?

The focus of emerging nanotechnology is the hive. That centralized control mechanism may also be the solution to the hacking problem. Current-generation nanohives are built to run colony templates, thus having control of single-function nanites. The goal of pre-CFD nanotech was autonomous auxons with the capability to self-replicate from base materials. Now, as with eugenics in the wake of the Second World War, this type of scientific thinking is considered abhorrent. The risk to the world is presumed to be far too high. But is it?

An understandable lack of consumer support has slowed the progress of nanotechnology and continues to do so. Fears exist that old-era technology is still in use and can be exploited. Those fears are valid. Given the possibilities inherent in CFDera nanotechnology, it would be foolish to assume that corporations disposed of it all without saving some for study. Furthermore, as long as monads exist and walk among us, so does the technology that led to their creation. This is not to say that commercial nanotech is vulnerable to attack. It is not. But the choices made to limit nanotechnology have also limited the advancement of this particular field of science.

The template model of nanotechnology is close-ended. The basic strategies it employs, such as neurochemical amplification and muscular reinforcement, are patches. They make what we already have stronger through nanite supplementation. Applying multiple templates and loading a subject with multiple hives has proven costly and cumbersome given the current state of nanotechnology. As such, I believe a return to the multipurpose, self-replicating nanites based on monad technology is the best way forward. These are far more efficient than the current generation of nanites, which are controlled by a hive using a centralized command structure.

- **>** Orbital DK

While the progress of nanotechnology has been slowed, it has not been halted. One area of nanotech that is leaping forward is the study of drug permanence. This theoretical technology is of particular interest to the military sector, where subjects are expected to maintain peak combat effectiveness for the duration of a sustained conflict, often in situations where supply-chain breakdown is inevitable. The science behind drug permanence is fairly simple. When an enhancement compound, such as kamikaze, is secreted into the system, it triggers a chemical reaction in the brain. Nanotech has the capability to simulate that chemical reaction by mimicking the neural signals resulting from the chemical interaction. This in turn continues the impact of the drug activity in your system, allowing you to ingest a particular cocktail and recycle the impact of that recipe indefinitely. This is merely a glimpse into what is possible when we allow ourselves to fully consider the possibilities of the technology we create.
