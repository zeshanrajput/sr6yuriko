## Terrestrial Qualities

The Hive appears to be a patchwork reality on several conceptual levels. The expected elements are the presence of Hive material, varying in degrees of evident age by virtue of observed entropic elements. In the absence of reliable radiocarbon and chemical dating techniques, less precise procedures have to be undertaken, from visual and even pressure-based comparative measures. Material is most often layered in strata, either of the same base material and chemical composition recognizable in certain insect strains, or changing to another insect type. Though of limited archaeological or strategic value at initial observation considering the constantly shifting borders and the time it takes to gather samples, deeper levels can show how long a single species or possibly even in-species family have occupied a region, or whether it has been a contested area (indicated by alternating Hive materials).

**Gravitational standards:** These prove to be an oxymoron, as gravity itself seems to shift from region to region, or even chamber to chamber. Some locations appear to have reduced or even no gravity, others an increased load. Direction is also inconsistent. Probes and scout teams have reported sudden shifts between regions, spirals in tunnels, even sudden spikes. They appear to be relatively consistent from one encounter to another, making short-term charting possible, but as time itself appears to be unreliable here, long-term charting has proven vaguely reliable at best.

**Chronological shifts:** Within the confines of the Colony, there is a fairly stable flow of time consistent with the manasphere established, but regional time-displacement has been documented both in drones and expedition teams, experiential perspective, and synchronized timekeeping devices. These occur in broad strokes from one location to the next, rather than micro-instances, though these have been noted in very rare circumstances. The fluctuations never seem to be more than a few hours’ worth, up to days in the most extreme observed examples. Expeditions, as a result, have a fairly relaxed scheduling timetable, and plans are formed around the assumption that some degree of time displacement may take place. While this has overall been the occasional boon to resource allocation when a team returns ahead of time from our perspective, it is more often a disruption to extended operations, as well as a drain on station morale; it’s easy to second-guess whether to send a rescue squad for a missing team when you don’t know if they’re in trouble or simply displaced, while also knowing the rescue squad suffers the same risk of being lost.

**Hive pathogens:** Less exotic in concept but still novel in scope is the problem of local disease. In full encounter suits, this is less of a problem, but every team is required to undergo full sterilization and observation for at least three days (more often a week or more) to ensure foreign pathogens are not introduced into the Colony under any but the most contained circumstances. We have already cataloged entire libraries of these alien diseases, and more reveal themselves to us regularly. This is to be expected from exploration of any alien environment, and to our benefit they seem largely unable to adapt themselves to the standard metahuman form, rendering us immune insofar as current studies show, but occasionally forms are shown to have deleterious effects ranging in severity from the common cold to ebola. Current theories abound regarding the native flora or fauna that might play host to these organisms, up to and including the insects themselves. One interesting note is that a population threatened with death by infection may be incentivized to colonize Earth to escape death, though healthy populations clearly still make the trans-dimensional migration regardless of threat beyond overpopulation.
