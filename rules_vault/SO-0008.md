## **>** High Road

- It’s a huge problem when someone frags it up, too. Our face was impersonating an MCT engineer during a lab hit. Turns out the receptionist was the engineer’s ex-husband. We didn’t know, and it almost blew the run. Luckily, our face was able to adapt and worked out a deal to screw over the ex by letting us in. Don’t count on being that lucky, though.

**>** Cayman



Diva also preferred portraying fictional characters—she said it was easier to create things from a blank slate than to learn the nuances and history of a real person. The trick with this, and any impersonation, really, is consistency. You have to make sure that all the details track at all times, because people pick up on inconsistencies really quickly. She’d also point out that your supporting cast (read: the rest of the team) is just as important. If your hopped-up gun bunny needs to pretend to be a bodyguard or love interest, make sure they are dressed for the part and know how to play their role.

- This is why I spent time learning about disguises from experts. The first time I ever went out to impersonate someone, I needed to play exactly this type of relationship. The problem was the face was a frag-head and I kept giving him a piece of my mind— this killed the illusion. If you feel like you don’t need to learn social skills because you can shoot straight and hide well, you’re doing a disservice to your team.

- Hard Exit

- Pretending to be a fictionalized persona isn’t always about playing it with gusto. You don’t want to be the person aiming for a best actor award. You need believability or even subtlety. Sometimes, you don’t **want** to be memorable. My preferred method is to go in quietly, but when a disguise is necessary, I go for one that will be overlooked or ignored. Whether serving food or cleaning floors, look like you belong. Once that disguise has served its purpose, you always have the option to ditch it.

- **>** Balladeer
