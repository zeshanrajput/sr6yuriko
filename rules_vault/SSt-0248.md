## **You Could Get Better Care**

**The employees of the major medical corporations are competent, which is nice, but they also operate under some fairly ruthless business models. When choosing between making a patient well or well enough, those models always opt for the latter. The time they spend with any patient is actively tracked, and if they go far beyond the median time expected for the patient’s documented condition, they will be sanctioned. They will also be limited in what tests and procedures they can recommend. The corporate bottom line will always play a role in the decisions they make.**

**With a street doc, though, things get a lot more variable. On the bad side, some docs run a patchand-dash operation, passing patients through at a speed that would make DocWagon proud but giving much worse care. On the good side, some street docs are caring and committed and give their patients the time they feel they need. They don’t have state-of-the-art equipment the big corps have, but tech is everywhere in our world, and it’s amazing what you can kludge together if you’re determined.**

- **For example, body scanners are pretty common security equipment, and you can get a used one for only a few thousand nuyen. Is it as detailed as cutting-edge hospital gear? No. But it can give you info that, combined with data from the health apps on all of our commlinks, can give a skilled doctor a start on a diagnosis. I’ll put it this way: Even a relatively low-tech street doc has access to better medical diagnostic gear than anyone ever had before, say, 2060 or so.**

- **Butch**

**While a corporate doc is all about maintaining the bottom line, a street doc has a reputation to uphold. That’s what you should look at: Based**

**on their reputation, what are they prioritizing? A doctor who is valued for protecting the privacy of their patients is a good thing, but don’t count on them delivering quality care, because it’s not their priority. Similarly, if you hear about a doctor where you can be in and out in minutes, you know that a long consultation or anything is not going to be part of your visit. Once you hear a number of patients who say things like, “My doctor really cares,” or “They really listen to me,” then you’re closer to identifying the right person.**

**But wait, you say. How good can any street doc be, really? If they were really good at their job, wouldn’t they be making big bucks at some hospital or insurance-connected private practice?**

**That’s a good point, but couldn’t you say the same thing about all of us? Couldn’t the shooters and faces and hackers and spellcasters among us make better money by working for a corp? Very possibly. But that’s not the life we want, so we chose something else. That being the case, we can assume some street doctors made a similar choice and are good at their jobs, regardless of their current trappings.**

**That, however, is not the only possibility. There’s a healthy percentage of street docs out there who got on the job because they ran out of other options. They were drummed out of the medical establishment through incompetence, malfeasance, intoxication, some combination of all of those three, or some other problem only their creative minds could invent. Some of them are still very much enmeshed in their problems—who among us doesn’t have a tale of a street doc who treated us while deep in their cups? Others, though, are seeking redemption, doing the only profession they know how to do and trying to show they can do it well. They could be one of the best doctors you’ll ever meet, and they forever will be unable to charge corp doc prices. Find them and treasure them.**
