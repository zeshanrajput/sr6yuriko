## Nano-Critter Augmentations

Augmenting mice, small birds, or even spiders and insects has become possible with nanotechnology. This is most often used for surveillance and reconnaissance, and using an array of them to create a redundant sensor network—well, you’ve heard the saying about wishing you could be a fly on the wall when something interesting happens. A more sinister application uses modified mosquitoes to deliver toxins or enhances the venom of a spider—bringing a whole new meaning to the term “security spider.”

These augmentations can only be implanted in critters with a Body attribute of 1 or less. The implantation procedure is minimal—a liquid solution containing the nanites is placed into direct contact with the critter, at which time the nanites become active and take care of the rest of the process, which takes one minute. After the augmentation is installed, the nanites usually become inactive. If the critter is in a liquid environment, however, the nanites may come into contact with other eligible targets and implant them as well. The nanites are not capable of reproducing but generally have enough resources and power to implant as many as a dozen critters—hence the Matrix relay plankton effect mentioned below. Some aquacologies produce Matrix relay nanites and regularly release them into the surrounding ocean, creating a stable and clear Matrix connection that extends for several kilometers into the otherwise signal-impenetrable salt water. So far, none of the megacorporations have conducted studies on how this affects the marine life that consumes these modified plankton—or if they have, they’re not sharing the results with the public.
