## Getting the “Read”

One of the best sets of tools available to you is reading your opponent’s body language and facial expressions. This is one of the classic ways of understanding and finding out more about them. Ask yourself: Do these cues indicate that they’re lying? Do they think you’re a joke? Are they keeping something back? Could they be low-balling you? Do they think the product or deal is dodgy?

The easiest place to start your read is with the larger facial movements. These facial gestures, such as eyebrow movements, lip biting, or nose wrinkling, can convey a person’s emotional state. Raised eyebrows might signify surprise or disbelief, while lip biting might indicate anxiety or apprehension. These are also the easiest ones for the person to manipulate, and you’ll need to determine if these are manufactured movements or something more natural.

The deeper read comes from micro expressions, those fleeting facial expressions or tics that last for a fraction of a second. These are more difficult to control and can be just as hard to detect because of how quickly they vanish. You’ll want to look for quick changes in facial muscles, such as a brief frown or a slight raise of an eyebrow. These can betray the target’s true feelings. Interpreting micro expressions is always done in the context of the wider conversation and in concert with other non-verbal cues, because they often reveal nothing when viewed in isolation.

What a person does with their eyes is also a very good indication of their emotional state. Sustained eye contact could indicate confidence or assertiveness, while avoiding it could suggest discomfort or a lack of confidence. But be aware that this doesn’t always hold true. These habits can vary based on cultural differences or neurodiversity.

Facial expressions, location choice, and communication are only part of the equation. A person’s body language can also help reinforce or betray the speaker. For instance, crossed arms might indicate defensiveness or disinterest. An open posture could signal receptiveness, interest, or a willingness to deal. Posture isn’t all about the arms either. Leaning forward can show interest as well, for example.

Hand movements and leg positioning are also indicators of someone’s comfort level in a conversation. If a person holds still, it may indicate tension and discomfort. Conversely, expressive talking with their hands can indicate their agitation. Rapid tapping of fingers can indicate impatience, while big open hand movements can show being open to ideas. Where they position their legs could simply indicate that they want to create some distance or maybe even a barrier, but tapping legs could show a nervousness they wish to hide.

Note that people don’t give single cues; it’s often several at the same time. So, keep your eyes open.

Something else to be aware of is when the other person is mimicking your movements. I’ve seen this in situations where they are trying to put you at ease by using your own facial and body movements. That’s not to say that those who use it do it intentionally—this could be part of the natural attunement of ideas that can occur. Or they’re doing it to get a read on you, seeing how you react. Remember, this is a game, so expect moves and counter-moves.

Another clue or cue to consider is where they position themselves in the room. You’ll often come across people who are afraid to sit with their backs to the door. They’ll want to sit with their back to the wall but have an easy egress plan. Meets at restaurants with booths are a big problem for people who are nervous. It also tends to be a problem for trolls, too. If your counterpart has bodyguards, watch their positioning as well. They aren’t likely to be nearly as well trained at hiding their emotions and intentions.

But always remember, reading people isn’t an exact science. People are complex, and even when fully truthful, their emotions might not align with their expressions. A well-crafted persona could be internally consistent and still hide away any deception. Stay observant, trust your instincts, and be ready to use any advantage you can to prevent yourself from being taken advantage of.

- A pretty good summary of a fairly deep topic. The real depth here comes from how people have trained themselves to avoid these tells. The best tells are the ones that you see over time. Instantaneous reads on a person are nearly impossible. I can spend a bunch of time staring in their eyes or watching their hands and feet, when really, I won’t know their tics until after they’ve screwed me over. The best strategy is watching this over time and staying paranoid enough to live that long.
