## shadow stock 

A blue-white jet of fire—a stream of plasma comparable to an oxyacetylene torch that dazzled eyes unprotected by flare compensation—flashed and flared and tore an expensive suit’s tightly-woven threads to nothingness, reduced a human being to component atoms, and filled the room with oily-slick smoke that carried smell of burning pork fat. 

Schneider was unharmed, save for his dignity, his suit soiled. 

The legs of the building’s vice executive, which was all that remained of his corpse, fell to the floor and smoldered. The human resources manager fainted, and two others joined Mr. Schneider in soiling themselves. A third vomited. 

“Mr. Clarke betrayed you, and in doing so, Saeder-Krupp Heavy Industries,” the Prime agent said around a smile and wisps of smoke, not showing any sign of exertion from performing the execution. “He accessed company medical records. He shared that data with a freelance espionage agent, and he allowed—actually, encouraged—their team to use Mr. Schneider’s iris patterns to infiltrate smoothly, bypassing the lion’s share of our security measures to retrieve proprietary information to be turned over to our enemies. He did so for personal profit _and_ to remove Mr. Schneider from his path to promotion.” 

His golden-framed glasses swept the room. 

“Mr. Clarke’s time with us has come to an end. His family will be taken care of. Mr. Schneider will be the recipient of Advanced Frontier’s top-end cyberoptic suite, these freelancers will be dealt with in short order, and your Matrix protocols and personnel will continue to be updated as my associate, Abbas, deems necessary to prevent further incursions.” 

The building’s in-house security decker swallowed nervously. 

The Prime agent’s teeth flashed pearly white, and then he simply turned and left. His decker, Abbas, hurriedly followed, a handkerchief over his mouth and nose. They shut the door behind them. 

Neither one cared enough to wonder how long the building’s employees stood there, breathing in that awful smoke, until they felt like they were allowed to leave.
