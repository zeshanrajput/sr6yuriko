## **The Prisoner’s Confession**

**This works best as a two-person job when both the prisoner and interrogator are secretly working together. The way it works is this: A person is nabbed by the authorities for some sort of crime—theft from the chambers of a high-ranking authority is a good one. This person—henceforth known as the prisoner—becomes desperate, telling authorities he only stole because he has a family to feed or some such. He pleads for his freedom, saying his family will be lost without him. An interrogator comes in and plays hardball, saying no sob story is going to get the prisoner out. He committed a crime, and he’s going to do some time—unless, of course, the prisoner has some sort of bargaining chip. The prisoner has to be a little reluctant here, not offering anything at frst, pretending they don’t know anything about anything. The interrogator pokes and prods and fnds some connection the prisoner has that might yield interesting information. The prisoner clams up, insisting they can’t talk about** _**that.**_ **This goes back and forth until fnally the interrogator drags some intelligence out of the prisoner. They pass it up the line, convinced that this is some good intelligence the prisoner only gave up because their situation was so dire. The prisoner typically is freed, as the information is seen as being more valuable than a relatively minor prosecution, and a message has effectively been passed without it seeming like a message at all.**
