## **Wants and Needs**

**==> picture [233 x 4] intentionally omitted \<==**

**There’s a bit of dance that you need to do when you’re working with informants, confdants, or runners. We’ve talked about building trust and making sure that you don’t ask them to do too much. The reverse is defnitely true as well. One of the things that I hate dealing with is the fact that your contact likely doesn’t appreciate the number of risks that you have to take for some of those favors. But I suppose the opposite is just as true. I’ve lost a few contacts because they weren’t careful enough, and it does eat me up a little bit.**

**First and foremost, they’ll want protection. Sometimes that is protection from persecution. This might be as simple as looking the other way while they do something that you know you won’t be happy with. Or maybe you’re gonna need to call in a few favors from peers.**

- **Bringing in someone to talk with is not as good as if they’re coming to you. As soon as you’re the one doing the asking, then you owe them. But if they’re coming to you, then they need you, and any trades you make are going to favor you.**

- **Juice Box**

**Sometimes that protection needs to be more tangible. The underbelly of the city doesn’t like snitches, and if they slip up, they’re headed straight to your door to get round-the-clock protection or a safehouse. This is always a touchy situation. If the informant’s no good, then you’ve got no reason to help them out. But if they’ve been valuable, then shipping them out of the city hurts the carefully selected network you’ve built.**

**Looking the other way doesn’t always protect them from the trouble they do. They’re going to call you to bail their hoop out of jail or maybe just a reduced sentence. Same as before: It’s all about making a deal. If you’re going to go to bat for them, they need to deliver the goods. They probably don’t realize how big a pain it is to get the district attorney to agree to anything. They didn’t get into that position by doing favors for some lowly detective or confdential informant who can’t keep their nose clean.**

**The favor you’ll see asked for the most are little extra incentives. Nuyen makes the world work and they’ll always want a bit more to put themselves at risk. My department has a fund for informants, but that isn’t inexhaustible. But sometimes I can work a deal out for some non-cash benefts, from a quick place to hide out to maybe some extra supplies. Most folks never really check on all the confscated equipment that’s sitting down in some storage locker in the basement.**

- **I remember a few years back calling up one of my contacts on the force and asking for two main favors. I knew that he had a guitar down in the cold case room and also needed to keep the beat cops away from one of the residential neighborhoods for a couple hours. The detective came through and I owed him big.**

- **Lanyap**

- **I assume the guitar’s special, but what’s with keeping the neighborhood cop free? Doing a run?**

- **Juice Box**

- **Nah. A friend of mine finally got out of prison for something unrelated to the cold case. But his one-of-a-kind guitar had been tied up for years. The neighborhood was for the party we threw with live music and a killer parade. Neighbors complained so much.**

- **Lanyap**

**There’re a few other weird ones that I’ve had to feld over the years. One gal wanted me to get her a year bus pass for her granny. Another guy had fallen for the front desk clerk and wanted me to introduce them. I didn’t, by the way. Him dating someone at the precinct was just going to put a target on his back.**

**One group of runners asked me to haul in a rival team so they had a leg up to get a job from Mr. Johnson. They were nice enough to supply evidence against the other team as well as a full itinerary of where they’d be over the next eight hours. I was defnitely a little hesitant, since the last thing you want is a team of angry runners taking pot shots at your fellow offcers, or worse yet, the public. We helped the team out, but not without doing a lot more due diligence. It puts a fne point on just how much some of these asks cost you.**

- **Sometimes your contact just doesn’t want to hear no. I had a solid informant who I used on occasion. They were part of a runner team, but I mostly leaned on their connections within the yakuza. For the most part I didn’t reach out to them unless there was something big going on. Because of that, we really didn’t have that much trust. When they came in asking for some confiscated drugs, there was no way for me to help them out. They didn’t know and wouldn’t hear that the department had done a crackdown on exactly what they were looking for.**

- **> LSPD**

**I’ve found that being straight with my informants has been the best policy. They need to know that you’re doing your best to help them out. The other is just being up front when telling them no. Don’t string them along for an ask that you know you can’t help them with. You’re not taking orders from them, but it doesn’t hurt to help them out if they’ve been helping you out.**
