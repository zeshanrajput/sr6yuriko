## **Street Cred**

**In the shadows, your reputation is everything. Establishing a reputation in a business largely based on illegal clandestine operations in which discretion is nearly always critical is diffcult and takes time. Bragging about your success is a surefre way to bring about the swift end of your career— and also your life. A single slip-up—sometimes even just a rumor—can damage that reputation. That’s why it’s so important to be professional and trustworthy—especially when performing underhanded and dishonest work. Someone seeking to hire people to do their dirty work for them often expects to be done dirty. They are far more likely to mistrust the shadowrunners they hire simply because they know themselves to be untrustworthy. As I said, discretion is critical. Mr. Johnsons expect you to keep their secrets, and some of them are willing to kill you to make sure that happens. They live by the saying that two people can keep a secret if one of them is dead. But screwing over a team of capable criminals is incredibly risky—so risky that most are willing to accept the lesser risk that those criminals will keep their secrets if they are paid well. Runners who become known for messily botching jobs or betraying that discretion will quickly accumulate a very negative reputation, and they are much more likely to be dealt with as dangerous loose ends. Keeping secrets and maintaining discretion are the best and strongest defenses**

**be—and developed a tenuous trust with the runners who worked with me on a regular basis. I did my best to share as much pertinent information as possible with them, and I had a few teams that could be relied upon to make an example of anyone who crossed me. Those targets would include both Mr. Johnsons and runner teams who betrayed my trust. It took many years—and many close calls— to build this reputation, but establishing trust is one of the most diffcult and time-consuming tasks imaginable in a business rife with double-crosses and clandestine operations.**

**My success as a fxer over the years can be attributed largely to my ability to put together groups of individuals to form teams with all the necessary skills and abilities to pull off a job and a knack for sussing out which people are likely to work well with others. This takes a fair bit of trial and error. When possible, I would seek out smaller, easier missions to use as trial runs to see which teams worked well together. You never quite know how reliable someone will be until they are tested under fre when things get messy, and so I would often save the more diffcult jobs for known quantities—teams that have pulled off a number of runs. I would do as much research as possible on the reputations of shadowrunners as well as the Mr. Johnsons seeking to employ them. I tried to avoid working with employers who were likely to treat shadowrunners as disposable assets, or ones so desperate to keep things quiet that they would try to snuff out the runner teams to keep their secrets. But no matter how careful I was, I could still be surprised. Over time I acquired a reputation as a fxer who could be relied upon to fnd the right team to get the job done—whatever that job might**

**Trust between runners, fxers, and Mr. Johnsons is important, but even more critical is trust among the members of a runner team. Runners need to learn to rely on others in order to be successful, and they need to trust one another to hold up their end. I’m not terribly likely to hire a team that botches a job more than once, but I will never put someone on a runner team if they are known to have betrayed their own chummers. I have been known to post bounties on such individuals, either to prevent future problems by removing a dangerous element, or to make an example for other runners about the consequences of screwing over their own team. Some runners think that they can take a payout and make out better by betraying their own team, but I fnd it best to make sure that course of action is too costly to consider. In order to survive in this business, you must both rely on your fellow runners and be relied upon by them. It**

**only takes one double-cross to get a reputation as an untrustworthy teammate, and such people have very short lifespans. Even if they seem to get away with it once, word gets around, suspicion builds, and someone puts them down. No matter how clever you think you are, never think you are clever enough to get away with turning on the people you work with. That goes for Mr. Johnsons as well, of course, but especially for your fellow runners.**

**On the other side, runners who establish themselves as reliable often form close bonds with their team and can rely on them not just for holding up their end on a run but to help them in other ways. Such bonds are deeper than friendship, sometimes stronger than family. This is the sort of runner team that I would always attempt to cultivate as a fxer. The teams that got the most lucrative jobs, the ones I relied upon the most, weren’t always the most skilled or best equipped—they were the ones who formed the closest bonds with each other.**
