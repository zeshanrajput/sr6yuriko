## **Corporate Police**

**As for the rest, the majority of law enforcement in the Sixth World today is subcontracted to forproft corporations. Many of these, such as Knight Errant, started as subsidiaries of a larger corporate entity, usually doing corporate security. Others, like Lone Star, started as an independent company but have since merged with other corporations to form a larger entity, in this case OmniStar.**

**There are also former municipal police departments such as NYPD Inc. that separated from the municipal government and incorporated but are still patrolling the same territory. The Pueblo Department of Public Safety (DPS) is also a corporate outlier, structurally operating as a municipal-style national police force, but because the PCC (Pueblo Corporate Council) is itself a corporation, the police force is corporate by default. Some corporate operations also work in partnership with an existing municipal police force, contracting to patrol a specifc area or handle a specifc task such as riot control.**

**Whatever the extent of a contract, all of these corporate LEPs are obligated to enforce the law, or at least as much of the law as the contract requires. At the local level they usually duplicate the structure of the existing municipal police department. Above the local enforcement level these organizations have a very different structure, and this gives corporate LEPs a separate priority from municipal departments. The bottom line is proft—the more the better.**

**Typically, a contract will be for the corporate law enforcement provider to take over the duties, responsibilities, and obligations of the municipal police department. A contract will be written for a set time and for a fxed amount, usually lower than what the previous police force was costing the city. This means that for most corporate departments the LEP contract is a loss leader, and they need to offer other services to make up the difference. This opens the door for services such as private security contracts with the LEP’s sister corporations, subscription services like PanicButton™, and branded products like home security systems and frearms. Many corporations also branch out into**

**other felds, such as prison system administration and even media programming.**

- **Remember that it’s impossible to write a perfect contract covering every scenario. So most of the time, those already quite complex documents simply define a framework for both the city and their contractor, the LEP, to operate within. How much this contract is followed in spirit or to the letter very much depends on the relationship between city hall and the security corp. If the relationship gets strained, maybe because of politics or city hall getting behind on its payment plan, the more you can expect a laissez-faire attitude from the LEP on just how they interpret the terms.**

**> Sim-Eon**

- **Of course, that’s not necessarily the attitude from the officers in the field. If you pass a patrol car while doing something obviously criminal, don’t expect a pass just because Corinne Potter is three months behind on Seattle’s payments. The issues are more likely to show up at the organizational, support, and logistical levels. For instance, that patrol car now chasing you may never see the SWAT backup they just called in and will be quite unhappy to find out their cruiser doesn’t contain half the ammo and weapons it should.**

**> LSPD**

**In the end it doesn’t really matter to the people who live in the areas served by a corporate contract. Lone Star, Knight Errant, Eagle Security, and other names are all just synonyms for “police” to them. For most citizens, there is little difference between municipal, corporate, or even military police. The wealthier, more infuential neighborhoods always get the best police protection, and those who live in the poorest areas receive little to no police service. It doesn’t really matter if this is from the limited resources of a municipal department or the fact that serving poor areas is simply not very proftable.**
