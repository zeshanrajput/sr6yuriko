## Combat Support

## Posted by: Rainbow

Just because you aren’t the street samurai on your crew doesn’t mean you get to sit out and watch when the lead starts flying. When your job or the lives of your crew are at stake, you do what you can. Unless you’re the combat monster, no one expects you to lead the charge—but you’d best help from the sidelines. The same thing applies to social situations, though the other way around. The street samurai is out of their element, and it’s the face’s job to lead the charge. First, I know most street sammies and other similarly antisocial critters think the best thing they can do during a negotiation is to keep their mouth shut and let the face do all the heavy lifting—and that may be the best plan for some. But in my experience, everything goes smoother when it’s not just a one-man show. Now, of course there are

going to be some delicate circumstances when you do need to shut up and stay out of the face’s way, but that shouldn’t be your default. Which brings me to my second point: Follow the face’s lead.

Knowing when to speak up or shut up so you don’t ruin things is probably the biggest reason most non-social types don’t step up. Well, that’s your own fragging fault. If you’ve focused so much on your other talents that you’ve neglected learning a bit about how to talk to people, now is the time to remedy that. I can hear you grumbling now, “Oh, I just couldn’t, I’m just so bad at that stuff, it’s scary!” Well, stop being such a damned coward! You think the face on your team likes having to pull out their gun when a firefight breaks out? No. But you still expect them to carry their weight. So, they need to be prepared with the right gear and skills to throw down. Same goes for you. You need to bring the skills and gear to back up your face when it’s their turn. I know, learning a new skill that you’re not very well-suited for can be frustrating and scary. You’re used to doing things you have a natural talent for and have spent years developing. Well, the good news is that you don’t have to get good at the talking stuff. You just need to learn enough to not be a detriment to your crew. Assuming you have a face on your team, you have the perfect resource to guide you.



Which brings me to my third point: Talk to your face, develop a better rapport with them, and ask them how you can help them do what they do best. If they tell you to frag off and stay out of their way, either you messed up big in the past or they probably shouldn’t be trusted. Maybe that’s a bit unfair; they might not be untrustworthy. They just might not trust you. If that’s the case, you can always hit up some of your contacts for help. And if you don’t have any friends willing to help you out (or not the right friends), there’s a ton of published drek out there you can learn from. And keep in mind that you don’t need to worry about actually getting good at talking. You just need to know enough to help out from time to time. You won’t be expected to lead the negotiations with Mr. Johnson, but you can put in a word or two that relates to your expertise to give the person leading the negotiation some ammo.

When it comes to supporting your face, make sure to let them take the lead. Leave most—if not all—of the talking to them. Don’t introduce new topics or information unless the face asks you ahead of time to do so. If you’re the muscle on your team, you don’t want the squishy ones charging ahead of you; it’s the same basic principle. You probably don’t know enough about the finer points of negotiation to avoid messing something up. Now I can just picture you saying, “Didn’t you just tell me not to sit out during the talking bits?” If you think I’m giving you mixed messages, think a little harder. I didn’t tell you not to help out, I just told you not to get in the way. It all comes down to letting your best expert take the lead on the thing they’re good at. That doesn’t mean sit on your thumbs. You help how you can, but don’t forget that you’re the support and not the lead. Knowing when not to talk is one of the first things you’ll need to learn. Ideally once you develop that rapport with the face I was mentioning earlier, you’ll work out some signals that let you know when it’s time to step up and when to step down. This is most difficult when your face is pulling a con of some kind. It’s easy to lose track of the threads they weave, and you might think the plan is falling apart when your buddy actually has them right where they want them. It would be awfully awkward if you mistook what was happening and threw off their game.

Which brings me to my fourth point: work with a face you can trust and then trust them.

This part is probably the trickiest, because no one’s better at misrepresenting themselves than a face. They con people for a living—it’s what they do best. Knowing who to trust to fill that role isn’t easy. A face acting in bad faith is worse than having no face at all, and they may leave you with no nuyen or dead in a gutter. That’s where street cred comes into play. Anyone who has screwed over their team is going to get a bad rep, and even if they managed to spin it so they look blameless, there will be rumors. Check in with some of your contacts, ask around about their past, and keep an ear out for gossip. If the teams that worked with your face came to a bad end more than once or twice without the face sharing the teams’ fate, the face probably had something to do with it. If not, you probably have someone you can build trust with. Even so, you should play it cool for the first few jobs and be careful not to give them enough rope to hang you with. Don’t let them find out where you sleep, don’t introduce them to your friends and family (or your best contacts, if you can help it), and try not to let them go solo for any part of the job. Honestly, that goes for faces you can trust as well, because you never know when they’ll need backup. But the main reason for this is to avoid giving them the chance to play their own separate game. If you have a trustworthy and reliable hacker on your team, you should also have them monitor a new face’s messages during a run. This should be easily accomplished, as no commlink is going to stand up to a solid hacker. All of this may sound like the opposite of trusting your face, but trust is something that needs to be earned. You should never trust someone on faith alone. Part of how you build trust is by being competent and showing integrity, and the other part is knowing what the person you’re building trust with is all about (and that includes whether they’re planning to frag over your team). What I don’t recommend doing is setting up a test or trap. A face acting in good faith will be upset by this when it’s over and they’ll likely kick you to the curb, and if they have bad intentions, they’ll smell your trap a kilometer away and avoid setting it off. Play your cards close to your chest with new crewmates and keep your eyes and ears—both literal and electronic—open. And if something seems off, don’t dismiss it. That’s about all there is to it.

One good option for all of you socially awkward deckers, wizards, and combat monsters is to use nonverbal communication to back up your face. Those physical cues are a good way to add something to the conversation without interrupting. You don’t actually have to talk to say something—your body language, facial expressions, demeanor, and gestures can convey a lot. Sure, they won’t get the job done on their own, but they’re a great way to give whoever is taking the lead a bit of a teamwork boost. A little bit can go a long way, especially where a little too much might gum up the works.
