## **Horizon and**

## **Aztechnology**

**I already mentioned these two as rivals, but to explain it more fully we have to delve into the type of game they’re playing and why these two corps are their chief rivals in it.**

**Power in the Sixth World comes in lots of forms. Magic can be power, bullets can be power, money defnitely is power, political offce is power. Those things are more clear and measurable. When Lofwyr wields power, it’s typically by throwing money and corporate resources around. Whether he’s pledging to deliver infrastructure to a damaged sprawl or pushing to take the lead in a technology race, he is capable of moving tons of cash and personnel around to deliver what’s needed and make sure he’s the one who wins the prize he’s after. It’s not the most subtle use of power, but it’s effective, and Lofwyr demonstrates particular skill at identifying opportunities where - the investment he makes can generate a signif cant return.**

**While Aztechnology rivals S-K in size, Horizon cannot. They do not have the cash or material resources of the other Big Ten, including newcomer Spinrad Global. If they fought on those felds and in those terms, they would lose every time.**



**So Horizon (and Aztechnology, even though they have more resources) prefers to fght on the more nebulous feld of desire. Not that Lofwyr doesn’t, but he’s usually focused on the desire for money or material things, rather than other wants. But Horizon and Aztechnology focus on the question that has been on the minds of marketers since the job was invented: How do you change what people want?**

**This is the realm where power becomes less concrete. It has a similar core to Lofwyr’s power, in that it rests in people’s confdence in your ability to give them something they want. It’s just that you are trying to give them self-esteem, or sex appeal, or fame, or a not-clearly-defned connection to another person. Sometimes you’re not directly selling the thing—you’re selling the promise of the thing. That doesn’t sound like much, but the people who are really good at it make it seem incredibly valuable. They do this in broad marketing campaigns, but it can also be person-to-person effort to push decision-makers to make choices that go your way. An illustration might help show how this works.**

**A national government is preparing rules for contracting decisions. If the rules include your corporation, you could have a line on hundreds of millions of nuyen of revenue. Even better, if the rules exclude your rivals, you have a clearer line on accessing that revenue. Of course, the offcials making the rules are not going to be persuaded by an argument simply saying that rules should be made to beneft you. What do they care about benefting you? Instead, you have to give them something they want.**

**And it can’t be money, at least not directly. While low-level government operatives can often be bribed like crazy, upper-level ones know they can’t be obvious about it. So you need to identify what they want and show you can do something about it. If they’re an elected offcial, you know there’s at least one thing they usually want, and that’s being re-elected. They also might want leverage to use in any negotiations with colleagues. If you can tell them how to parlay the rules you want into a message that will resonate with voters. If you can fnd another legislator who will beneft and highlight that to your frst target, you can start setting up some connections.**

**None of this requires moving money or resources, but it does require a lot of talking. You need to be able to get people to listen to you, and the promises you make need to seem credible. There is no concrete path to gain this credibility, as it’s art, not science. Once you start getting a few people to listen to you, though, others think you might be worth their time. So you open doors and hope to make the most of the time you spend charging through them. Rather than throwing a pile of money around, you use your credibility as currency. Which means if someone wants to attack your resources, they’ll focus on breaking down your credibility.**

**With Horizon and Aztechnology both seeking to bring Convergence down a peg, their different approaches will be on display, and we’ll see how the overall defense of Convergence can rise to the occasion. The different approaches can be summarized as follows: Horizon tries to convince others that you’re wrong, Aztechnology tries to convince others that you’re an idiot. Or put another way, Horizon attacks arguments, and Aztechnology attacks you.**

**This is not to say that Horizon is entirely logical or fact-based. They want to convince others you are wrong—they don’t really care if you actually are wrong or not. They will falsify evidence, appeal to people’s vanity and insecurity, and use every logical fallacy in the book to counter what you are doing. They know how few debates are won with actual logic, and they’re in it to win it. So they attack with the tools that work. If you want to keep people believing in you, you’re going to have to come back with more than spreadsheets and data. You need to persuade as well as they do.**

- **> Cover**

- **That’s exactly the point. In the long run, for most people, nothing trumps their vanity and insecurities.**

**I don’t want to say that Aztechnology can’t conduct that same sort of campaign. Of course they can. They’re the best PR company in the world. But they tend to enjoy serving up their arguments with extra viciousness. They don’t just want you to lose an argument, they want the people they convince to never trust you again. They will destroy your reputation with everything they have, and that’s a lot. You’ll see trideo footage of you doing things you’ve never done, read documents by you that you never wrote, and hear things about yourself that you never imagined. If you want to survive, you will have to perform every type of damage control humanity knows.**

**This is what Convergence is going to face, but as the wags would say, they probably knew that already. The question is, can they stand up to it? That’s what we’ll fnd out.**
