## **Investigation**

**While patrol and tactical are geared to respond to crimes in progress, the investigation departments usually move in after the fact. In most local police departments, the investigation departments make up the second-largest block of offcers, just behind patrol.**

**Like every other aspect of modern policing, whether a crime is investigated or not tends to depend on where it occurred and who the victim was. Crimes in wealthy neighborhoods are always investigated, while poor neighborhoods will be lucky to even have a report taken. Of course, if a crime victim is a well-connected or affuent individual, an investigation will happen regardless of where the actual crime occurred.**

**Criminal Investigations is the catch-all department handling the cases that don’t go directly to a dedicated investigation unit like robbery or homicide. This department deals with misdemeanors, petty theft, assault and battery, trespass, vandalism, harassment, animal abuse, vagrancy, minor weapon charges, and similar relatively minor offenses. Forces that lack a specifc dedicated investigation division for vice or drug enforcement will add those types of crimes into criminal investigations as well. This department is usually seen as the lowest rung of the investigation squads, and new detectives all end up spending time here until they have the seniority to transfer out.**

- **And the ones who can’t transfer are basically in limbo, making them perfect disgruntled employees, ripe to become a contact. Also, because they don’t handle anything major, Internal Affairs will not be monitoring any of them very closely.**

**Robbery is the department set up to investigate burglary, shoplifting, muggings, vehicle theft, and other forms of robbery, as well as the recovery of stolen merchandise. Petty theft under a thousand nuyen usually goes to criminal investigations, while cases over a half-million nuyen will transfer to the major case squad; everything in between is handled by robbery. Typically, this type of investigation involves a lot of taking statements, checking pawn shops, cataloging seizures, running Matrix searches, and looking for serial numbers, but sometimes a robbery investigation will uncover the clue that leads to a breakthrough in another case. Organized crime syndicates, smuggling rings, and other types of racketeering organizations have all been taken down by what started with a robbery investigation.**

**Special Victims investigations involve sexual assaults, crimes against children, elder abuse, and domestic violence cases. Different forces may have some or all of these broken out into their own specialty squads, but in general the skills needed to investigate this type of crime are similar. These cases all require the detectives to have a lighter touch and good social skills. A background in psychology can often be more useful than a high frearms score. Some forces, Lone Star specifcally, do not utilize special victims units, and instead group sexual assaults and domestic violence together with prostitution in their Department of Vice and Morality. As you may guess, this greatly reduces their success rate for solving these types of crimes.**

**Homicide offcers carry out murder investigations and will be on scene for obvious homicides as well as any other suspicious deaths that may become a homicide investigation at some point. Homicide detectives work closely with other divisions to generate information for their cases. Forensic technicians work murder scenes to collect and catalog trace evidence. It’s also common to work closely with the magical operations division since a number of spells, rituals, spirit abilities, and**

**specialized units focused on investigating a single organized crime syndicate. This may be the Mafa, Yakuza, Triads, Seoulpa rings, drug cartels, or national gangs like the Ancients; whichever is the biggest problem at the time. Despite the assistance these units can provide to other departments for solving certain types of crimes, some see these squads as wasted resources. A single organized crime case can take years to put together, and when they do manage to take down one cartel, family or kumi, there’s always another syndicate ready to take its place.**

**Major Case units are staffed by the best and brightest detectives on a force; they handle the high-profle crimes that make the news. Bank robberies, political assassinations, arson, theft of expensive artwork or jewelry, blackmail cases, and kidnapping where the victim is famous (or at least wealthy) all end up in the hands of Major Case. These cases normally have the highest priority, and the squad has their pick of offcers from a force’s specialty divisions. Some major case squads even have full-time magical, Matrix, and drone assets permanently assigned to them. I also want to point out there’s one other crime typically handled by the major case squad that usually doesn’t make the news: offcer homicide. Anyone who kills a cop in the commission of a crime can usually expect their case to become an extremely high priority.**

**Vice is a department whose existence depends a lot on the local government and by extension the views of the local population. In areas where prostitution is legal, or not considered a serious crime, there may be no vice squad at all, or they simply enforce the proper licensing. Other jurisdictions may take vice crimes more seriously, but even then, it’s often a case of keeping it out of the public’s view. In areas where such services are illegal, prostitution is often run by organized crime outfts along with gambling houses, BTL chips, and street drugs. In these locations the authority of a vice department may be expanded to cover illegal gambling and occasionally drug and chip enforcement. In any case, information collected by vice investigations ends up with a major case squad, an organized crime unit, or even a highprofle task force.**

**Task Forces are sometimes organized by command for very high-profle cases that remain unsolved and stay in the news for a length of time. Task force offcers are typically drawn from the major case squad with the addition of dedicated specialists from the support services or other divisions. Task forces tend to stay together and work as a unit until the case is either solved or it stays cold long enough for the media to lose interest.**

**Drug and Chip Enforcement departments have an issue similar to vice, as what’s illegal in one area may only be regulated or be completely legal in a neighboring municipality. Many departments also see this type of enforcement as a bottomless hole that can consume endless resources without having a noticeable effect on chip and drug abuse. As a result, enforcement of these laws is spotty at best, with units solely dedicated to combating street drugs and BTL chips being uncommon. Often the task is relegated to CI units or it’s rolled in with Vice.**

**Departments that do take drug enforcement seriously often do so because the local trade is tied directly to an organized crime syndicate. These units also tend to go all in, using drone surveillance to trace a suspect’s movements and associates, blanket Matrix surveillance, and even inserting undercover cops in the drug distribution operation. Once they get a case together, drug charges are easier to make stick than ones like racketeering. And they can be used as leverage, leading to breakthroughs for the organized crime task force.**
