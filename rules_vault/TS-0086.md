## **Let’s Get Physical**

**Most trace evidence will be the physical kind: sweat, blood, other fuids, hair, dead skin, fngerprints, or even body prints of some kind. In short, literal bits and pieces of the suspect that trace back to them.**

- **Speaking of hair, I have a good one for you. I was hired to track down a murderer, so I started by obtaining a copy of the police report. It mentioned that the shower was still wet at the time of inspection, and it was pretty easy to deduce that the victim hadn’t taken one just before being killed. We just had to unscrew the drain to recover a whole bunch of hairs and the analysis gave us a good lead. The perp won’t harm anyone again, and it’s all because of his sense of hygiene.**

- **Jimmy Kincaid**

- **For the cops to have missed that, they had to be either extremely incompetent or bought off. Or, come to think of it, maybe both.**

- **Sim-Eon**

**Once discovered, any trace evidence will need to be documented, inventoried, secured, and of course transported to the appropriate facilities, all while**

**maintaining the chain of custody. At the lab, technicians will further document and analyze the items. In most items such as clothing, or biological samples, the goal is to identify any DNA or traces thereof. These samples will then be compared with the company DNA and ID database, both directly and indirectly.**

- **Wait, how do you indirectly do anything with DNA?**

- **Treadle**

- **Indirectly basically means that they search for relatives. Basically, they can deduce that it’s not a person who directly possesses the DNA found, but a family member who shares part of their DNA. Based on subtle differences, it’s possible to deduce whether it’s a sister, a son, etc. This makes the search much easier and increases the initial result sets significantly. Oh, and it means that if your not-so-smart half-brother gets caught and his DNA is in their database, you can thank him by joining him in prison after leaving your own, theoretically untraceable, DNA while doing a job.**

- **Hard Exit**

**As already mentioned, key pieces of physical evidence will be the clothing and other items the victim or even suspect wore, which could contain trace or large amounts of biological material for analysis. Additionally, trace evidence can also indicate where the subject may have been, such as dirt, paint, chemicals, etc. Such trace evidence can indicate where the suspect regularly goes or has recently been.**

- **I love exotic trace evidence. During a case, I was surprised to learn that some tiny mushrooms are exclusively indigenous to the Ork Underground, and that fibers and drek (literally) from Glow City have their own unique radioactive signature.**

- **Gumshoe**

**Other things to look for at a crime scene include good old-fashioned footprints or even hand and fngerprints. Shoeprints reveal not only the brand, but also the foot size. From there, things such as weight, height, and in some cases metatype can be postulated, while hand and fngerprints can be used for a more exact ID or to link the suspect to other crime scenes.**

**We can also spot and pick up seemingly innocuous traces that can turn into other leads. Remember the old trideo trope of the matchbox that tells the detective the murderer is a regular customer of a specifc joint? Well, we do the same, but it’s more often greasy Stuffer Shack bags. Garbage cans and trash dumps around a scene can be great sources of clues. Suspects typically want to get rid of a murder weapon, and they usually do so quickly and in the flthiest places. Have you ever rummaged through a chiphead’s toilet while his guts are spilling out next to you? My lab guys have.**

**The fuids mentioned above may also be of interest in understanding the scene itself. Specialists**

**can reconstruct events only using bloodstains. For example, it’s easy enough to deduce how old they are, since they tend to coagulate and dry normally in the open air, and fuid mechanics do the rest. Once a tech showed me that blood drops on the foor can be used to deduce a good deal of movement at the time. Well-rounded drops, or even a stain, indicate that the person was standing still. By contrast, movement and direction can be deduced if the shape of the drop stretches. The naked eye can even deduce the approximate speed as a function of stretching. Very useful for tracking a suspect you’ve just shot.**

**Finally, and perhaps my favorite part, are witnesses. All the people who heard and saw nothing, as you’d expect, and with whom you’ll feel you’re wasting your time. But you’re not. Concentrate on the small details and leave no stone unturned. A witness tells you that there was no noise that night, whereas the alleyway is usually a loud place; the suspect could have used a Silence spell. If so, the suspect being Awakened is a major bit of info. Or maybe they scared or slotted off the locals, who are now more than willing to give a description.**

**Write down the names, qualities, professions, and habits of the witnesses and make them feel useful and important. Most of the time, people have an afterwit; details that come back clearer to them later, and that’s when you need them to be thinking of you.**

**It’s also because people tend to have biases and infuences that, just like the crime scene itself, you need to be able to “freeze” them and ask them questions very quickly. The mind tends to want to fll in the blanks and add explanations when you don’t have any. Worse than a hostile witness is one who is convinced that they have seen/heard something that they themselves, without even knowing it, invented. Check your witnesses, verify their testimony, get a grasp of their biases.**

**> Canvassing is an art often lost to runners. Done well, it can change an investigation from a wild goose chase or dead end to closing it. One needs to be good at befriending people, however, smelling their preconceptions about things or people and assembling a picture of moving pieces, as each bit of information is being added. Few of us in the shadows think about using it, often believing that, without cop’s credentials, no one will reply to our questions. In fact, most people will cooperate more willingly with us than the police! Of course, many may charge us for the information, but that’s just biz. > Sim-Eon**
