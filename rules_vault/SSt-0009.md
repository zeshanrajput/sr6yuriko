## **What You Don’t Know**

**Having a team with a wide range of skills and equipment with the street cred to match isn’t just about trust. As I said before, every shadowrun is different—even similar types of jobs are going to have wildly different circumstances. Unexpected turns of events are going to happen. You can’t expect everything, though you need to be ready to adapt to changing circumstances and unanticipated revelations.**

**Almost every Mr. Johnson I hired a team for attempted to conceal vital information. Some of them do this out of shame or a need to maximize discretion, while others use it as a test to make sure the runner team is professional enough. If I could change one thing about this business, it would be this aspect, but unfortunately it is a ubiquitous practice. Even those Mr. Johnsons who attempt to be as forthcoming as possible are likely to be unaware of important aspects of the circumstances, or they overlook something they didn’t believe was critical.**

**As a fxer, I would do my best to fnd out as much as I could and pass this information on to the runners. I would also stress to the Johnsons the importance of not withholding information, to the point of offering more favorable terms to incentivize greater transparency. Most fxers do not see things this way, as they only look out for themselves. The ones with longevity, though, understand how important it is to gather and share information as thoroughly as possible—both for their own reputations and to protect the reputations of the runners they rely on. Reliable shadowrunners are the fxer’s lifeblood, and they are an incredibly rare and fragile resource. You can’t afford to treat them as expendable assets—unless they prove that they must be dealt with as a threat.**

**Even under the best circumstances regarding information gathering and transparency, it is impossible to know everything needed to make an operation go smoothly. Situations are always fuid, and the best-laid plans are rarely suffcient to cover every possibility. Those that are tend to be so complex that they are diffcult to keep track of, and ultimately one error or oversight will undermine them.**

**This isn’t to say that planning is not a critical aspect of a shadowrun—not at all. It is incredibly important to formulate a plan, including contingencies, as best as you can. What I am trying to say is that you must also be able to improvise. The frst step toward this is to accept that your plan is likely to fail in some key ways, and you won’t be able to know how it will fail until it happens. You need to be prepared to be surprised so that you can overcome the shock of that failure and adapt. Plan as best you can, but also plan for failure. Do your best to anticipate that failure, but don’t become set in your expectations. You are much more likely to miss something if you focus too much on those expectations. Keep your mind open, and be ready to react to changing circumstances. If things go wrong, and they will, you must be ready to come up with new plans on the fy.**

**That is why the best plans are often simple ones that don’t rely on too many details. Complex plans are dangerous—they are often required for delicate operations, but look for ways to eliminate as much of that complexity as you can. The simplest plans are the ones closest to being foolproof. When things start to fall apart, and your plans begin to fail, you won’t have time to develop complex new plans. Rely on your instincts, and the instincts and intuition of your chummers, and come up with a new simple plan. And be ready for** _**that**_ **plan to fail, so you can react quickly and make another new plan if necessary. You can’t be ready for everything, but a skilled team of shadowrunners with a wide range of skillsets and abilities that trusts one another should be able to adapt to anything.**

**The last thing I need to mention is that failure is an option. If a run goes very badly, sometimes the best thing you can do is minimize the damage—both to your own reputation and to your employer’s goals. There’s failing a job, and then there’s botching a job. Trying to cover your tracks by screwing over your Mr. Johnson, your fxer, or your teammates will only make things worse. Being open about these failures and providing the necessary information to allow your fxer or Mr. Johnson to prepare for damage control won’t leave your reputation unstained, but it might prevent it from being shattered. I always tried to keep a few safehouses available for when this sort of thing happened, and this has come in handy more often than I would have liked. But a team of runners that can be relied upon is an incredibly valuable asset that I could never afford to waste.**
