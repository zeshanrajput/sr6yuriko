## **Hunker Down When Necessary**

**In most sprawls, we do a lot of our work at night, because the less visible we are, the better. In extreme environments, you have to take some other factors into consideration. When you’re in a hot desert, don’t go out in midday. Just don’t. Even if you argue to yourself that you’ll have an advantage because no one will expect you to be moving when the sun is at its peak, don’t forget that there’s a** _**reason**_ **no one expects that. Because it’s stupid. Deserts cool a lot quicker than more humid climates, so why risk death during the hottest part of the day when you might be able to drop twenty degrees in the not-too-distant future?**

**Similarly, while moving around at night is nice, the fve or ten degrees it might drop in polar regions when it gets dark could cause some serious problems. The sun might not give off much heat, but it’s a lot more than the frozen moon will provide.**

- **Don’t forget all the ways there are to not be seen. The sunlight on an arctic day can be blinding with all the snow and ice. Dress in white, keep low, and learn how to move smoothly, and you’ll be amazed at how you can sneak up on people in some of the brightest light you’ll see.**

- **Cayman**

**And these are just fairly normal conditions. When the weather gets wild, only the foolish stay out in it. A desert sandstorm has no visibility, making it all too easy to become buried, lost, or both. Same with a blizzard. I don’t care how geared up you were to carry out your plans that day** _**—**_ **if a blizzard hits, you delay. You may be the biggest, meanest metahuman on the planet, but step outside for a moment during an arctic blizzard and listen to the unearthly howl of that unending wind, and you’ll realize that there are things much bigger and more powerful than you are. Then go back the hell inside.**

- **I can hear some of you out there thinking, “silly Traveler Jones and his mundane perspective—he doesn’t understand how magic can keep you safe from some of those things when a simple suit can’t protect you.” Don’t make the mistake of thinking that magic will deal with a hostile environment all by itself. Yes, a good spell can provide a measure of protection and leave you feeling like you’re walking through a comfortable environment, but the whole time it’s in operation it will be under siege. The beating a spell would take during one of these storms puts a high strain on the mind of the caster trying to sustain it. You may gain a little extra time, but once that spell collapses, you’re left out there just like anyone else, with the elements descending on you.**
