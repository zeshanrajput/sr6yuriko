## **Bunyip**

**The bunyip (** _**Crocodylus wergaia**_ **) is a legendary creature that was originally considered part of the folklore of Indigenous Australians. During the Awakening, we discovered that many of these mythical creatures were actually real. The bunyip is a relatively late discovery due to its native environment and its elusive nature. This terrifying creature inhabits the swamps, rivers, and billabongs of Australia. It is often described as an ambush predator, using its excellent camoufage to take down unwary prey as they go for a drink of water.**

- **I have a contact in Bioenergetica Ukraine, who’s been working on training vodyanoy. She says that while a tasty dragonfly works well, deep-fried chebureki from the local convenience store has proved to be much more effective. She reports that her daily message from the creatures no longer is of insects and exclusively shows these fried treats.**

- **Red Anya**

**When traveling in Australasia, knowing more about the habits of this furtive animal can be vital to your survival. The last thing you want is to be staring at this sizable creature as it leaps from its hiding spot because you happened into its hunting ground. It is often better to use this to your advantage by leading your opposition straight into a trap. No one will really bat an eye when someone gets snatched and eaten near a watering hole. There is also the question of how these animals survive in the magically hostile Australian environment.**

**While scientists have classifed the creature as crocodilian, it has some attributes of other critters. The adult bunyip is roughly four meters long from tail to snout. The creature stands one meter to the shoulder but often lies submerged in the water, with only its eyes visible. The animal has powerful and large back legs similar to those of a kangaroo. The body is covered in fur that looks like algae and seaweed, making it able to blend into its preferred hunting locations. The head of the bunyip is broad with a shorter snout, sometimes looking bull-like, other times resembling a platypus. Their coloration ranges from reddish brown to a brilliant green and matches the local environment. They can adjust their coloration in less than two minutes.**

**The bunyip is a solitary hunter with a voracious appetite, only traveling between waterways at night to search for new hunting locations. The creature feeds on medium- to large-sized animals but will also eat small animals such as wallabies and possums, as well as fsh and reptiles that live in the waterways where it resides. It’s a fairly indiscriminate eater and uses its ability to mimic sounds to draw its prey closer to its hiding spots before lunging out with its sharp claws and teeth. The bunyip is even known to eat many venomous creatures and is seemingly unaffected.**

**Like many of the reptile and crocodile family, the bunyip is an egg-laying animal. Of a normal brood of one to two dozen eggs, there will be only two or three survivors that will live to adulthood. Juvenile bunyip grow from six centimeters in length to their adult size in about fve years. And while we don’t know how long they live—the oldest known specimen is thirty-two years old— it’s theorized they could live up to seventy or eighty years, depending on the health of the specimen.**
