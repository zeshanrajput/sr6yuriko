## Well, shit.

She really needed to get out of sight. She had no idea what was on the datachip, but it had better turn out to be worth this hassle. She was of half a mind to stand in front of a camera and very obviously throw it away so that people would leave her alone.

But there had to be something juicy on it. The way she had received it …

“Ms. Desai, this is Mr. Coburn from HR, Ms. Ducey from engineering, Ms. Furniz from general services, Mr. Cho, also from general. Everyone, this is Ms. Desai. She likely will be doing some contract work for us in the near future.” There were handshakes, quick greetings, and all the basics of an initial business contact. The large conference room they were in was on the south end of Central Park, and sunshine and light, fluffy clouds made the windows look like blown-up postcards someone had decided to hang on the wall. Jugaad would have been happy to ignore the talk and stare out the windows, but that’s not what she was there for. She had to be friendly and sociable, make an impression, so that the assembled people could believe she could perform work that was nothing like what she was doing here (not that these people all knew what she would be doing—this was legwork to get to know the waters in which she would be swimming, as it were). She smiled, she nodded, she answered questions clearly and directly, and she projected confidence.

After more conversation, her guide, Ms. Branch, led her out of the conference room into the hallway. They were ostensibly going to meet a junior vice president of something or other, but a door about eight meters away opened, and things got weird.

The first thing to come out of the door was a voice, female and clearly working hard to stay calm.

“Get out of my office, Borden,” she said. “You’re clearly very drunk.”

Then a person staggered out of the office. He was middle aged and wore a grey suit that looked slept in. There was what looked like a wet spot on his upper thigh that Jugaad did not want to think about.

“Drunk?” the man said, in a full-on drunk yell. “I haven’t drank enough yet! Not nearly enough!”

The woman who had spoken emerged from the office. The wide shoulders of her jacket and severe lines of her skirt made her look like two elegant, vertically stacked triangles. “I’m sure security will be happy to listen to the details of what you have consumed. Or you can just talk to yourself on your way out of the building.”

The disheveled man flapped one hand like a wounded bird. “That, there. You hear that? That’s the con … condensation I’m talking about. Always looking down on people like me. Always so … _smug.”_

“Yes,” the woman said. “I can’t imagine why anyone would ever act superior to you.”

The disheveled man pulled himself up, suddenly gathering two-tenths of a meter in height. “You … _dare!”_ He took two steps toward the woman.

Workers emerged, seemingly from nowhere, and grabbed the man’s arms. He immediately stepped back and raised his arms. The woman made a dismissive wave.

“Let him go,” she said. “He’s harmless.”

The disheveled man glared through angry but unsteady eyes, then took another step forward.

Workers moved to grab him again.

“This is the way out, dammit!” he said. “I’m going out. Get your hands off of me.”

The workers looked at the woman, she nodded, and the man was left to totter forward. He took five steps, then tripped and stumbled forward—right toward Jugaad. Threatening to crush her. She jumped back two steps so that he fell at her feet, on all fours.

The elegant woman smirked. Several others laughed aloud. The man kept his head down, looking for a moment like he might vomit. Then he shook his head and started to stand. He tried to lunge to his feet, and his hand landed on Jugaad’s shoulder. The motion of his feet pushed her backward, but he was moving faster, so he collided into her. His face briefly rested against her face.

She did not smell a trace of alcohol on his breath. “Starting now,” he whispered. His hand briefly touched her hip. Then he staggered to his feet and tottered away.

She felt the datachip in her pocket, but she didn’t reach for it while all these office people were around. She finished her meet and greet, got outside, pulled it out, connected it to her commlink, and saw there was a single document on the chip.

Before she got a chance to look at it, though, two orks stormed outside and came after her.
