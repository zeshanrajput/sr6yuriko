## **Defense Attorneys**

**Most runners who work for lawyers do it as a way to pay off their legal fees. The smart way to do this is after your trial, suggest that you can help**

**if there’s anything else they need done. Stay purposefully vague about this, because even criminal lawyers often make an effort to follow their professional code of ethics (if only to make sure they can keep their job). If they turn you down, make the nuyen for their bill from your usual crimes, instead of crimes you do on their behalf.**

**If your lawyer is open to repayment in kind, they’ll usually ask you to interfere with the prosecution’s case somehow. In jurisdictions that have the presumption of innocence, it’s the prosecutor’s job to prove the defendant’s guilt “beyond a reasonable doubt.” That doesn’t mean conclusively proving that someone else committed the crime, or even conclusively proving that the defendant didn’t do it—it means making it impossible to be sure that the defendant did it. That might mean destroying evidence, “persuading” witnesses not to testify, and other means of obstructing justice. The work involved isn’t much different from any other run, but it’s worth being extra careful and making sure you don’t get caught. The courts frown on anything interfering with the judicial process; in some jurisdictions, the sentence for witness tampering can be as harsh as it is for murder.**

- **If these jobs sound like the kinds of things a Mafia consigliere would hire you to do, it’s because they are. There’s a reason most of those guys are lawyers.**
