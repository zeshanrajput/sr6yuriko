## Optional Rule: Heist Points are Edge!

If you want to provide additional options for the players, you can offer to let the team use Heist Points as a team Edge pool with an exchange rate equal to three points of Edge per Heist Point. Once the pool is expended, it’s back to thinking on your feet and hoping for some natural good luck.

infinite. The following topics are listed to help both the gamemaster prep their game and give players an idea of the types of investigations they can do before they crash through the door.

These activities have also been tagged to the most appropriate Heist Point designation. None of these are part of the Oh Drek designation. This specific type of planning tends to go further afield into the realms of possible runner activities but could fit within the ideas below. For instance, hiring some gangers to hit the northern gates of a facility would potentially fit within the Distractions activity whereas calling in an airstrike on any pursuit wouldn’t necessarily fit into any of the below suggestions.

As a note, this is not an exhaustive list of legwork activities.

**Acting** : (Egress or Ingress) Doing the proper research or getting some instruction from another person can help you sell the role the team is trying to play. If the team needs the hacker to pretend to be their accountant, then making sure they know something about finances can give them an edge in the situation.

**Astral Detection** : (Astral) Some facilities and locations will have various methods of detecting the presence of Awakened individuals. This typically includes paracritters trained for this purpose or using Awakened plant life, such as glomoss. Alternatively, the team could look into using something similar to prevent some spirit or mage from hitting them unexpectedly.

**Auras and Moods** : (Astral, Egress, Ingress, or Physical) Learning more about the people you’ll encounter isn’t limited to just legwork about their social life or medication. A mage or spirit can read the target’s aura to determine their mood or disposition. It’s helpful to know if they’re naturally skittish, happy, bored, or anything else in between to know whom to avoid.

**Blueprints** : (Egress or Ingress) The place probably doesn’t have any secret hallways, but one never knows unless they’re digging into the layout. This provides an excellent view into choke points, avenues, and blind corners that a team will want to know about prior to the run.

**Cameras** : (Egress, Ingress, Matrix, or Physical) This includes knowing the physical location of the cameras, knowing which ones are in service, and

how the data is stored. This also covers whether the stored footage is protected with encryption or via a data bomb.

**Credentials** : (Ingress, Matrix, or Physical) One solid plan that teams use is getting some form of credentials in advance. This could be setting up badge access to the building through theft or via hack, or maybe they’re just setting up a Probe for Backdoor Entry later on.

**Distractions** : (Astral, Ingress, Egress, Matrix, Physical) The bad thing about distractions is they cause everyone to switch from passively perceiving to actively searching for threats. Hopefully the runners’ plans create enough distance between them and the distraction. On the flip side, there could be a honeypot that entices the team into to a trap room by something they perceive as valuable to them.

**Drone Net** : (Ingress, Egress, or Physical) Getting a good operational overwatch is extremely helpful, and making sure the runners’ set of drones are positioned to give a wide view of the area is a great way to achieve that. Alternatively, knowing what the opposition is using for their own drone security sweeps will provide the team with opportunities to sneak in.

**Guards** : (Physical) Knowing the patrol patterns, barracks locations, and basic sets of ranks can help a team plan for how to handle situations in which guards are going to be an issue. Getting the shift change schedule so they know when they can move freely makes the run less dangerous, unless a team accidentally doubles the number of active guards because of bad timing.

**Host Topologies** : (Matrix or Ingress) Not all systems are simple with a single host controlling all the devices within the facility. Finding out what you’re up against in terms of nested hosts and connected devices can help your hacker prepare.

**Host Visitors** : (Matrix) Knowing how many personas are logged into the hosts provides the team with an understanding of when more spiders might be staffed to handle the traffic.

**Locks and Sensors** : (Physical) The equipment the team brings on a run is often determined by the physical security they need to overcome, as well as any devices the facility uses to detect intruders. For instance, if the job requires wetwork but all the entrances all have olfactory sensors, the team may want to use knives or similar methods to complete the job. With sensors coming in a wide variety of types, knowing what could be encountered means a team can select the most appropriate tools or weapons. For locks, having the right lockpicking tools could mean not being stranded in the middle of a run.

**Quick Response Units** : (Physical) Nothing strikes fear into the hearts of a team more than a bunch of well-supplied, heavily armed fighters bearing down on their location. Not just because they’re tough, but because the opposition can probably just keep sending more and more until they eventually wearyou down. Knowing where they’re coming from and what their typical load is will help you prepare if you ever trigger their attention.

**Roads** : (Egress or Ingress) A smooth way to a target and the fastest, safest way to egress will increase chances for success. This also includes understanding the routes the target could possibly take, which provides the best locations of where to hit the target and minimize the response from security or other defenders.

**Spirits** : (Astral) Spirits are more intelligent than watcher spirits and can present a real problem to runner teams. Opposing forces like to use them because they’re alerted as soon as a spirit is removed—not to mention they’re tough and can report details about the team. Knowing how many spirits cover the area along with the astral signatures on them will give the team a better ability to deal with them even if it’s just to geek the mage who’s commanding them.

**Surrounding Region** : (Egress or Ingress) You’ll want to start by learning the security rating for the surrounding area, as this can give you an idea about any law enforcement personnel and response times. From there, learning about the people who live and work there can help create cover for any infiltration or escape plans. And if it all goes to hell, they may be the ones to aid your swift departure.

**Traps** : (Egress or Ingress) Setting up traps in advance can give your team an edge, but the same can be said for the traps that a team failed to identify. Nothing says hello quite like a tripwire hooked up to an explosive. And if a team is placing the explosives, they’ll need to know where, and how to place them. And whoever is on the receiving end, knowing exactly where those traps are will increase their chances of survival so they can complete the job.

**Watcher Spirits** : (Astral) The specific patterns, quantity, and astral signatures of watchers can provide the team with valuable information if they were planning on using any magical shenanigans during the run.

**Who’s Who** : (Egress, Ingress, or Physical) This line of investigation has the team digging into what corporations, gangs, criminal organizations, and individuals who are involved. Most of this information will likely be legwork that’s required as part of the run, but exceptional results could reveal a critical weakness—such as the fact that the receptionist always takes a smoke break out the back door and props it open with a brick.
