## The Risk of Hyper-Intuitive Automated Trading

One of the dreams that financial sharks, hackers, and idealists share is the notion that algorithms or expert systems can be developed to execute complex market trades in real time. Highspeed, high-volume trading has been conducted on the major exchanges for nearly a century based on relatively simple rulesets designed to reap earnings from fluctuations and small discrepancies in the everyday trading that occurs in stock, commodities, and currency markets. Of course, sometimes these have gone awry because of the relative simplicity and the hubris of these systems’ creators, and they crashed these same markets not a few times over the first fifty years.

One of the concerns about these algorithms is what variables they include. Sometimes the algorithms are simply about timing, perhaps buying or selling faster than a human could, or spacing orders to optimize the average price. At other times they look at prices across markets, what other traders are doing, or how far an asset is from its mean price.

Note what those elements have in common— they are meta-trading, which is trading based on other trading. They are not looking at business practices, revenue, profits, or anything else. They affect the market by analyzing the market.

This, of course, has a risk of becoming recursive, which is exactly what algorithm-based trading has often been accused of being. Find a significant stock market crash in the past century, and you’ll find someone placing significant blame on accelerating algorithms. Laws have been put in place to install circuit breakers, which are algorithms to stop trading automatically should the other algorithms cause prices to spiral out of control. Whether that is a wolf guarding the hen house or just more chickens watching the chickens is not entirely clear.

Ever since the Crash of ’29 nearly wiped out the equity markets and the computer and financial systems behind them, followed by the birth of the Matrix and ASIST-based decking at the speed of thought, regulators have been quite conservative on how these systems are allowed to be used by “legitimate” investors and on markets owned and overseen by the megacorporations. This happens to coincide with the megacorporations’ general conservatism when it comes to financialization of economies—especially since each of the then-Big Seven’s and now-Big Ten’s internal economies are by far the largest collections of interconnected economic activities, markets, and assets in the world.

The leaders of the Big Ten have been in the business world for a long time, and so quite naturally they see the world as businesspeople would. They see profit and loss, market share, year-overFINANCIALyear growth, price-to-earnings ratio, and so on. They disdain blind groupthink and pack mentalities (unless, of course, those things are working in their favor). They view trend-seeking investing as worse than gambling, because it can often cost them millions of nuyen without them even being dealt a hand.

This is a simple fact of the Sixth World—if you want something to change, do something that costs powerful people a fortune.

Their caution in these matters is rooted in their memory, because some of them have very clear recollections of how these trading mechanisms did more than cost them a fortune. At one point, they altered the world.
