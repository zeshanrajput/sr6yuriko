## Corporate Court

## Securities and

## Exchange Commission

The prime directive of the CCSEC is simple: Make people feel comfortable investing in corporate securities. They can’t guarantee that people will make money, but they’d like people to not be blindsided by a sudden loss of all their invested assets. That tends to reduce market engagement. This means that every time automated trading gets implicated in a steep market crash, the CCSEC gets the vapors. Of course, they can’t take unilateral action. They have to consult with corp execs about how many restrictions they can impose without messing up what the corps want to do. Inevitably, this makes the restrictions they eventually impose extremely watered down.

Perhaps the firmest line they have taken in recent years is against self-spawning algorithms. The basic thinking behind them is simple: If one program can trade faster than a human trader, then ten programs can trade ten times as fast. It’s not always easy to directly insert ten programs where they need to be, so what the self-replicating program does is insert itself and then immediately make some copies. Some variants never stop spawning, making more and more copies until they hit the limit of wherever they set up shop. Since all copies share the same propensities, they have the potential to cascade quickly, and the CCSEC likes that not at all. They’ll only approve algorithms and programs that do their job on their own, without having to make co-workers.
