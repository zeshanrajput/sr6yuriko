## **Support Divisions**

**Support departments operate in the background of most forces, but the fact is that a modern force would be unable to operate day to day without the hard work of these usually underappreciated offcers.**

**Administrative departments make sure the business of running a police force happens. Without departments such as accounting, payroll, personnel, purchasing, and many others, every operation would quickly grind to a halt.**

**Dispatch manages all of the communications within a police force, which includes incoming calls from the public and orders going out to the offcers in the feld. These departments typically use a predetermined set of priorities or algorithms to assign units to a call that includes call location, patrol location, and for commercial forces, subscription services. Dispatch also manages interdepartment calls such as dispatching SWAT as backup as well as sending support departments and specialty division offcers out to investigations.**

- **For obvious reasons, Dispatch generally has some of the best Matrix security, with a spider or two always monitoring their host. If you ever think hacking a dispatch host will help you out, well, trust me on this: come up with another plan!**

- **Clockwork**

**Supply departments are mainly split into the armory and the vehicle pool. Armory offcers maintain and distribute a police force’s weapons**

**and armor as well as general items like comms, restraints, uniforms, and boots. The motor pool maintains all ground, air, and water vehicles, houses them when not in use, and signs them out to offcers as needed. Items issued to and used by specialty divisions, such as drones, Matrix equipment, and magical reagents, are typically managed internally within the different specialty divisions by supply offcers permanently assigned to them.**

**Forensics teams are the heart of many investigations, with technicians showing up at a crime scene to locate and catalog whatever physical evidence is left behind. Most modern forensic departments rely heavily on drone operations, releasing swarms of small fyers equipped with wide-spectrum, high-resolution cameras to document blood spatter, as well as documenting and logging hair and tissue samples. Other LIDARequipped drones will create digital maps of a scene accurate to the millimeter, complete with bullet holes and projected trajectories. Other specialty drones, such as the older Shamus drones, can record DNA evidence.**

**Forensics departments will commonly request offcers from other specialty divisions for a case. Drone operations will also assemble and turn over recordings from the nearby street cameras and any relevant aerial drone recordings to be added to the case fle. Matrix operations may be called out to hack into devices found at a crime scene or to hack nearby cameras that are not part of the police network. Magical operations may also be called to study astral space for residual signatures or use ritual magic on material links collected during an investigation.**

**Once everything is collected from a crime scene, it’s sent to a forensic laboratory. Sometimes the lab is in-house, but smaller forces may be forced to send out material to a national forensics lab or subcontract it to another corporation. Of course, sending evidence out slows down the entire investigation process, but the more specialized a required analysis is, the more likely it is that a local department won’t be able to process it.**

- **Lab time is limited and hotly contested. Priority goes to more profitable cases. Bumped low-priority crimes can languish for months, even years, before being processed. The idea that every crime has a team of a dozen scientists with top-end gear analyzing everything is a trid fantasy. Murder? Sure, but a basic B&E where somebody’s Gamebox got lifted? Call back in five years.**
