## Geography

The city is built around large, shallow lagoons full of brackish, polluted water along the coast of the Gulf of Guinea. It gets its freshwater feed from the Ogun and Osun Rivers. A solid quarter of the sprawl area is actually water, with much of the rest of the city built on a swamp. During the rainy season, streets become waterways and entire neighborhoods are flooded by the vile water. Homes built over the swamps and lagoons balance precariously on thin stilts, with wooden or plastic slats connecting homes like bridges. In the drier areas of the city, homes are often built of cinderblocks, and multi-story apartment complexes are common in the dense slums.

Lagos Island is the high-security heart of Lagos. Named in the singular, the island is actually made up of three islands that became one as the creeks and rivers separating them were filled in to create dry land for development. It is the corporate and commercial core of Lagos, with gleaming steel high-rises and bustling air traffic. The majority of the land and buildings are owned by corporate investors, but the Lagos Island Council provides the main security around and on the entire island. The island relies primarily on a small army of heavily armed metahuman Area Boys to patrol the streets and provide protection, though this force is present as much to keep the peace as it is to keep the status quo and remind the corporations of the power of the Lagos Council.

Inside the buildings, corporate security takes over. The island is isolated from the mainland by the polluted waters of the lagoon, and around the perimeter of the island is a double-layer fence topped with razor wire. Armed and armored guards patrol the fence at all times, ensuring there are no gaps in coverage. There are almost no drones and few spirits patrolling this rim. Two bridges link to the mainland, and both have heavily fortified gates and guards at the entrances to the island. Foreigners have an easier time getting past the guards than Lagosians, but the guards expect a bribe regardless—in nuyen rather than naira (the local currency).
