## **Look for Difference**

**I wouldn’t say that a lot of people look to work with someone just like them, whether intentionally or unintentionally. First of all, most people don’t actually have a ton of self-awareness. They don’t know enough about how they actually are to purposefully look for someone who is really like them. For example, they may think of themselves as observational and witty, when they’re really just casually cruel.**

**What people actually do is look for someone who is a lot like That One Person They Like. Usually this is a close friend, but it can also be a longtime running companion (especially if they recently lost that connection for some reason). They have a dynamic they like, and often what they want from a team is to replicate that dynamic as often as possible.**

**That’s natural, and it isn’t even entirely negative. If you have something in life that works for you, maintaining or recreating it can be a good way to sustain success.**

**But just because you’ve done something one way for a while doesn’t mean that’s the way you should always do it, and the only way to fnd out if there’s a better way to do things is to bring people in who think differently than you do.**

- **And listen to them—at least enough to give them a fair hearing.**

- **> Chainmaker**

**Once you have a diverse range of perspectives on your team, you face the diffcult challenge of making people feel like they can offer opinions while also ensuring they know that when a decision is made, it’s fnal and has to be followed. This often means deliberately seeking clear, specifc feedback. Don’t ask your team members, “How are things going?” or “Have you noticed anything that needs to be changed?” Ask your rigger, “What can we do to get on the road faster?” Ask your mage, “What can we do to make sure we’re well positioned for any spells you need to cast?” Ask the face, “How many people should be present when you meet the subject, and how do you want them dressed?” Questions like that show you’ve thought specifcally about the various jobs on your team and know what kind of input you want. They also are specifc enough to spur thought rather than leaving people casting around to think of something to say.**

**You also have to adopt some of the suggestions you are given. If people see your feedback exercises don’t go anywhere, they’re going to stop participating. If you think a suggestion is outrageous or completely inadequate, you’re free to ignore it. But if it’s a close call at all, err on the side of trying something someone else suggests. That’s how you learn.**

- **And don’t choose between experience and youth. Both have things to offer. Neither one is always right.**

- **> Sunshine**
