## **It’s All About Style**

**Of course, from corporation to corporation, patrol operations are going to be slightly different. The way the Pueblo Corporate Council security forces (or PuebSec) handle things is a much more militaristic approach than a traditional uniformed response. This is largely because of direct governmental control by the PCC. As far as these cops are concerned, they are the law. Since PuebSec has direct military ties, their equipment can be a little scarier on the average offcer. Automatic rifes are often standard carries, in addition to heavier body armor. They like to use their heavily armed look as a little bit of intimidation to keep the peace. This isn’t to say they don’t have more lightly armored offcers as well, only that the tactical look is much more common.**

**As a NAN state, with a lot of land between major population hubs, the PCC tends to deal with far more traffcking through their lands. As such, they use a lot more drone support than other enforcement agencies, and this carries over into the cities as well for general enforcement. While drone use is becoming much more commonplace overall, especially in areas where it is hard to fnd personnel or in places where immediate response is, shall we say, harder to fnd (e.g., Z-Zones and the like), PuebSec has been using drones extensively for offcer support for decades due to their close military backing.**

**Unfortunately, as is said, once the army becomes the police, things can get carried away. PuebSec almost always uses live ammunition instead of gel or other less-than-lethal loads, often leading to deadly incidents that could have been prevented had these alternatives been used. They have them, yes, but unless given a direct order to use them, it’s full metal jacket by default. This adherence to a militaristic doctrine helps with the constant fow of smugglers and traffckers coming in from all angles to traverse or hide in PCC territory, but it’s a different situation for the common jerk who just got pulled over in Phoenix because his taillight was out.**

**Knight Errant, on the other hand, had this reputation coming in as this elite corporate security force dipping its toes into the law enforcement scene. While not quite Firewatch, Knight Errant was once a name runner teams didn’t want to hear. It had an image of sleek**

**professionalism to maintain. This didn’t last in the wake of everything that transpired in the Seattle Metroplex and with Ares in the last ten years. They went from the image of crisp uniforms and shiny chrome, to looking a little too much like they walked off the set of an edgy paramilitary trideo. KE countered by cracking down on some of the more annoying petty crimes the Star let slide, like noise complaints and vagrancy. Arrests for the smallest amounts of controlled substances skyrocketed, and the local jails, which KE didn’t have to manage by the way, flled to the breaking point. While it was always denied, it seemed that any Ares employee who happened to be on the wrong end of one of these petty offense arrests got off with nothing more than a slap on the wrist and a warning. Sure, every once in a while, some poor schlub who worked for Ares was thrown under the bus as an example, but there were always rumors that said those slags were already in hot water with management and this was a way to not only clean out the corporate trash but give KE some nice press. Also, it goes without saying, don’t try to hide from KE on Ares property. You’re just asking for trouble … but more on how jurisdictions with corporations intersect later.**

**As far as law enforcement, KE has done a lot of business in the CAS and has been one of Lone Star’s chief rivals for city contracts for years. Because they are backed by the world’s largest arms corporation. They always seem to have their hands on some cutting-edge technology, at least where it is the most visible. Cops who work the streets in the poorer areas of town get the same scraps they always have.**

**Which brings me back to Lone Star,** _**my**_ **corp. Once its own AA-rated corp, it’s now part of a new umbrella corp known as OmniStar along with DocWagon, Manadyne, and a few other AArated outfts. It’s a bit of rebranding for everyone involved, but it’s another opportunity for the LEP to reinvent itself as a “new, trustworthy, and bright spot when held up against the records of the past.” Yeah, I wanted to puke while writing that. While it is public information that OmniStar is simply a company containing Lone Star as one of its … branches? Hey, I don’t know corp structuring. Anyway, the new name provides a buffer for those who simply wish to put the blinders on.**

**Lone Star has policies that date back forty years regarding their approach to impartiality, police work, and interaction with the local justice system. Since Lone Star was founded as purely a LEP, it was much easier early on for some cities to sign the contract with them, simply because it didn’t appear to be selling out to a big megacorp with other interests besides law enforcement. One of the more unique policies of the Star is their community outreach, wherein they are required to reach out to local neighborhoods, to try to cultivate a more local feel, so the cops aren’t regarded as a monolithic arm of the city that just exists to cost the citizens money.**

**How a LEP conducts business on the street is largely determined by the nature of the contract agreed upon by the city. Most of the time these contracts are massive, chock-full of legalese, restrictions, and expectations. In the end, it’s up to the LEP to follow through in as good faith as they can. Because if they don’t, they’re out of a contract. Ultimately, a LEP lives and dies on its rep among the political circles. Don’t do a good enough job? You’re out. Have a public problem with police brutality? Reform your image or fnd a new city. At least in the public sphere, there are repercussions for dirty cops, unlike corporate security.**

**That’s one of the big differences between a LEP and corporate security: consequences. Corporate security isn’t forced to go through the same training as most LEP offcers. LEPs are forced to have their prospective offcers frst go through POST certifcation (Peace Offcer Standards Training). This is a standard part of the contract for each LEP, and one of the primary reasons a LEP can operate on a similar level to a city’s former police force. It’s a holdover from the old civil service days of the police force. During this training, the prospective employee learns about the laws and criminal justice proceedings of the jurisdiction they intend to operate in, methods of arrest, frearms training, and driving. Believe it or not, each and every beat cop you’ve dealt with has had to sit through and pass a multi-week course on each of these things.**

**Corporate Security, while it often must cooperate with a LEP, has no such required training, and handles all arrests made on their properties in-house. Thanks to the Shiawase Decision and Business Recognition Accords, no governmental body can make them change that fact on their own property.**
