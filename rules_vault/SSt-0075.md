## **Ground Vehicles**

**Unless you’re a combat biker or go-ganger, bikes probably aren’t going to be your jam. However, I’ve found them to be very useful for a limited range of tasks—mostly getting one person or a specifc item in or out quickly. They’re small, fast, and cheap, and most can carry up to two people. If at least one of those people is a solid combatant, you can focus on the driving and let them do the shooting. As a rigger, I would always recommend operating a bike remotely, both because you’re very vulnerable on a bike and because jumping in wirelessly means that you free up ffty percent of the seating capacity. My two best recommendations for bikedrones are the Yamaha Growler and the Suzuki Transit. The Growler is ideal for situations where you need an off-road vehicle, but it handles just as well on pavement and can still outrun most cars. For speedy on-road purposes, the Transit is ideal. It’s cheap, easy to repair, modify, and maintain, and it has more seating than your typical crotch rocket. My honorable mention goes to the Harley-Davidson Scorpion, as it’s not too pricey, has a decent top speed, and doesn’t handle too badly off-road. I just wish it came with a decent muffer.**

**I’m going to split up the larger ground vehicles into two categories: cars and vans/trucks. Cars are great for getting around a city without drawing attention, and most of them are pretty quick—your speed is usually limited more by the environment than the engine. Quick acceleration and deceleration are generally more important than top speed. Another benchmark to consider is speed interval. It’s a fairly recent term that most non-gearheads are a little confused by, but what it generally means is how well that vehicle handles at higher speeds. Bigger numbers are better in this case.**

**One of my favorite cars is the classic Ford Americar, largely because it doesn’t look out of place anywhere—also because it’s cheap and has decent specs. You can haul a team of four people around, along with their gear, and you have a decent chance of getting in and out of trouble quickly with a rigger at the helm. The chassis size is also large enough to accommodate a wide range of modifcations. A nice step up that I like for most of the same reasons as the Americar is the Toyota Daytripper—an SUV made for soccer moms that’s capable of carrying more people or storing more cargo. Neither of these are my recs for a rigger’s main ride, but they’re great options for urban stealth and basic transportation—whether you’re**

**downtown, on the highways, or in the barrens. My favorite stock model is the Ford Dasher—preferably the Interceptor model designed for security patrols. It’s almost as fast as the Eurocar Westwind (and less conspicuous) for less than a third of the price tag, and it maneuvers like a dream both on and off road. It’s also got decent armor for a vehicle its size. If you’re going to look for a car to be your main ride, this is the one to buy. With electrochromic coating and a retractable light bar, you can masquerade as a cop car if you need to. I’ll toss in an honorable mention for the Hyundai Shin-hyung—it’s a cheap sports car with decent speed and handling, and it’s also fairly ubiquitous and doesn’t scream “I’m a rich sucker, so please shoot and rob me” when you drive through the barrens with it.**

**When it comes to a serious transport vehicle and a rigger’s main ride, you’re probably best off with a van or truck. These have plenty of space for passengers and cargo, but most importantly they can conceal a wide range of useful modifcations. There are a lot of different options to pick from, but two of them stand out to me as the most useful: the Nissan Strider and the Range Rover. I know, the Bulldog is probably a lot more common, but have you ever tried driving one off road? That comes up on a run more often than you might think, even in urban environments. The Strider handles better off road than a Bulldog handles on road, and the Strider’s on-road handling is even better than that. It might be a little smaller with a little less armor and cargo space, but its superior handling and speed interval far outweigh that difference in my book. They’re also used very commonly as corp courier vehicles, and that makes them blend in easily on most jobs. For the main all-purpose ground vehicle, the rigger’s personal chariot, and the shadowrun team’s mobile HQ, my best recommendation—if you can afford it—is the Range Rover. Almost all of its specs are superior to every other model in this category: It can handle both on- and off-road driving, has decent acceleration and speed interval, is the fastest vehicle in its category, has plenty of room for passengers, cargo, and modifcations, and the stock electronics—both its pilot and sensor suites—are solid. It might not blend in everywhere, but it doesn’t stick out as much as a Bison or Roadmaster. My honorable mention goes to the Jeep Trailblazer. I know, you’d think an elite rigger like me wouldn’t be caught dead driving one of these, but you know what? These things are cheap, fast, and have superb handling both on and off road. I wouldn’t use one for a main vehicle or waste money on many modifcations, but the price is right for an inexpensive secondary transport vehicle—low enough that I wouldn’t feel too badly about using one as an explosive delivery vehicle if the plan calls for it. Honestly,**

**pay through the nose for even the most basic pile of drek on the market. If you’re doing short trips on the open water or working in a river-heavy or swampy environment, my recommendation is to go with the GMC Patroller hovercraft. This baby can handle traveling over the ground as well as the water and can outrun most vehicles designed for either. It can handle longer trips over the open water if needed, but if something goes wrong with the air cushion or engines, you’re sunk. The GMC Vacationer III is decent, but the increase to the price for the Patroller model is easily worth the improvements. It’s also cheaper than most other watercraft and highly customizable. The only signifcant drawback is the noise—hovercraft just aren’t capable of being anywhere near as quiet as a boat can be, even with stealth upgrades. If I were in the market for an actual boat, my top choice would be the Azzie Nightrunner. It’s fairly inexpensive, easily modifed, handles well, and has decent stock electronics. Set it up with totally enclosed construction and submersible secondary propulsion, and you’ve got a very versatile watercraft capable of handling any seagoing venture. It’s also small and light enough to handle shallow waters and rivers.**
