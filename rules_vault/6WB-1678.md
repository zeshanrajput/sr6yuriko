## Structure and Cityscape

**First impressions:** The first thing that catches the eye when approaching Berlin by plane is the still-recognizable former division between the corporate west and the anarchistic east. While high-rises and mega-buildings dominate the cityscape in the west, the east is practically flat, and except for the dilapidated brutalist “Plattenbauten” from GDR times, limited to the pre-millennium building heights of up to six stories. As one gets closer, more details emerge: In the west, clusters of mega-buildings are discernible, often with an arcology in the middle (of which there are many).

Individual streets are covered with glass roofs or even a top level to protect the historic facades beneath from corrosive rain. In the east, many side streets are completely lost under a tangle of wires, pipes, truck tarps, camouflage nets, and roof connections, and the houses of some neighborhoods seem to have grown together, forming a literal hive nest. Many roofs in the alternative districts are covered with tin huts, greenhouses, solar panels, wind turbines, and generators. To the fringe of the city, the number of derelict buildings and overgrown streets rapidly increases, making it hard to tell where Berlin ends and the surrounding state of Brandenburg begins. Flying at night, the west glows with video screens and skyrakers, while the east is lost in darkness and flickering fires. The barricades between West and East may have disappeared—there are nests of anarchy in the West and sporadic corporate projects growing into the sky in the East—but the decades-long separation will be felt for a long time to come.

**Decentralization:** Today’s Berlin was formed over 150 years ago by the merger of over two dozen formerly independent cities, some of whose identities are reflected in the names and structures of the districts. This decentralization further encouraged Berlin’s disintegration into separate districts and neighborhoods (called “kiezes”), each with its own center, attitude to life, and even laws. The growing transformation of neighborhoods into gated communities, typical of Berlin, is the direct result of the need for self-protection, self-government, and self-sufficiency of communities in water, energy, food, etc. under the Flux State. Gated communities are also much more common in the West than elsewhere in the AGS, in the classic form of operating companies with uniformed gatekeepers and private security forces.

**City of arcologies:** The high number of arcologies—a necessity in a city without statewide supply networks for many decades—as well as the clustering of large buildings in tightly defined areas is a consequence of the earlier unrest in Berlin and the concomitant compulsion for corporations to concentrate their facilities in easy-to-defend areas. Since Berlin has not one single city center but three (the City West at Gedächtniskirche/Bahnhof Zoo, the City East at Fernsehturm/Alexanderplatz, and the Brandenburg Gate as a somewhat symbolic and dead point at the geographical heart of the city), these clusters seem to be randomly distributed, connected by skyraker-lined main streets that cut through the six-story buildings of old Berlin. Especially in the City West, some effort has been made to blend the classicist Old Berlin facades with new buildings, with mixed success: partially overbuilt streets, monumental Nouveau Art Deco or even Neo-Brutalist fronts, and sometimes grotesquely oversized statues give Berlin its special flair, most reminiscent of Neo-Paris or, as some say, “Gotham City on cram.”

**Three kinds of districts:** Administratively, Berlin is divided into corporate districts (called Sectors), alternative districts (called Zones), and “normal” Districts, which were only reintroduced with the Berlin Treaty. The term “normal” refers to a mode of operation that corresponds to districts common to other AGS cities. Corporate and alternative districts have different internal modes of operation— in the case of Sectors as actual extraterritorial areas and in the case of Zones as quasi-extraterritorial areas whose exact legal status is largely unresolved at present, the core idea being that alternatives and corporations can both do as they please in their respective areas of control.
