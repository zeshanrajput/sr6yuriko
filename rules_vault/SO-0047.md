## Wrath (Hate)

Hate, pure and simple, is one hell of a motivator. Wanting justice, however you define it, is one thing. To be consumed by it is another thing entirely. It’s easy to look at groups like Humanis and not see how their point of view is motivated by what they see as a form of justice. They believe it is unfair that metahumans “steal” their jobs, their homes, their opportunities. In fear, they paradoxically believe themselves to simultaneously be both superior and vulnerable, in danger of being pushed aside by those who are stronger, prettier, longer-lived, etc. They have a victim complex that presses them to both a sense of pride in something they had no say in (congrats, your parents fucked,

and that’s the best thing about you?) and the idea that their reliable, traditional way of life is now threatened by the “new races.” And so, in their fear, they Hate.

Hate, as I see it, comes in two basic forms: loud and quiet, defined by how deep the waters run. Loud Hate makes haste where there should be hesitation, wearing its cause on its sleeve and acting immediately for gratification. Quiet Hate is planted deep in the soul, capable of masking what it feels to make “rational” arguments. Often the Quiet creates, inspires, motivates, and leads the Loud. Keeping with the Humanis comparison, Loud is the lynch mob and protestors, while Quiet is the Hand of Five philosopher-terrorist who pens manifestos in between making bombs. Loud will start a bar fight with a “dandelion eater,” but Quiet will make “reasonable arguments” to plant the seeds of prejudice in others in conversation at the school bake sale.

As with so many cons, Hate is based in hunger and fear. Hunger can be for revenge, patriotism/nationalism, superiority realized, or between rivals— or an entire populace, for that matter. Fear drives Hate to seek annihilation of the perceived threat, or at least its power, real or perceived. Where many other sins seek to accumulate, Hate faces outward, a consuming flame that demands fuel. The Hateful will burn themselves in their efforts, poisoning themselves and hoping it seeps out and hurts the ones they despise.

Too poetic? So is Hate. If it feels heavy and syrupy, it’s because Hate is not based in reason, no matter how hard the Hateful will work to rationalize it. And make no mistake, they will justify their feelings to the point of violence. Nothing gets you closer to the Hateful than vindicating their beliefs. Hateful marks think it’s them against the world, whether an enemy or some soft-headed ally getting in the way, unable or unwilling to listen to “reason.”

Hate as a reaction is more of a psychological cue you can manipulate, or a prejudice they’ll act on given the right stimulus. Proactive Hate is a slavering beast, a chained hound waiting to be given the scent and released. Provide a target,

THEand they’ll pursue it. Present a means to hunt, and they’ll invest in it. Loud Hate will likely take (near) immediate action, while Quiet Hate will seek to build power to be used later unless circumstances present a limited opportunity, as with Greed. Regardless, they seek to do harm. Provide the bait and lay your traps accordingly. Your biggest risk is that such wanton psychological motivations have a way of getting out of your control, much like unleashing fire. It may spread and burn in ways you didn’t anticipate. For maximum effect, as with fire, provide specific channels and parameters for it to follow, leading right where you want it. Whether you’re getting the guards out of the warehouse to chase down Sons of Sauron or selling the killer app (laced with Trojans) to take down a decker’s rival, the energy is theirs. Just be very sure you know where it’s going, and where it ends.
