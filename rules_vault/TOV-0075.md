## **(** Spirit of Air **)**

The spirits associated with the orisha of Shango often take the form of a savage-looking warrior, clothed in crackling electricity. These spirits are part of the Anago nanchon and rule over the natural powers of lightning and thunder. When calling upon the orisha of Shango, the spirits can take on male, female, and androgynous forms, and some names that they answer to include Shango and Oya. These manifestations represent different aspects of the lwa family, and who heeds the calling depends on the needs of the summoner.

Voodoo practitioners tend to rely on a combination of potions, gris-gris, and other enchantments as a core part of ceremonies and rituals. The use of herbology to create potions and preparations ( **Alchemy** , p. 149-151, _SR6_ ) work without any restrictions. One of the most popular sale items is a love potion, although the effectiveness of this potion is low.

Gris-gris and enchanting tend to fi ll a very similar role. The use of enchanting is generally reserved for houngan asogwe or mambo asogwe for use in constructing asson. In most cases, priests will rely on the construction of gris-gris and the spirits to assist them. So, you can build and use a focus, but the other Voodoo adherents will be shaking their heads at you.

The primary colors that represent the orisha are white and red, and the most common symbols used are necklaces and beads. Rituals to Shango will include an offering of a specifi c type of stew made from okra and prawn, and any ritual sacrifi ce uses turtles, goats, ducks, or sheep.
