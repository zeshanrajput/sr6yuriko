## Color by Numbers By Jay Erickson

They were being hunted … hunted by an adversary she couldn’t see.

Tight corridors constricted around her like the belly of a snake. Row upon row of pod-like rooms flashed by in a dizzying flurry, swollen polyps in the beast’s gut. Feeling helpless, Prnc3ss simply watched as the coffin motel rooms whizzed past her.

“Grandmother says we’re losing ground, _omae_ .” Prnc3ss looked over Hatari’s shoulder as he carried her and saw eyes like liquid pools of honey staring back at her, intense in the dim hallway light. Tears streamed from those eyes and down the young caramel face of their shaman, Spynner.

“Save it for inside,” Hatari, the team’s muscle, interjected between pants. His breath was hot against the back of Prnc3ss’s neck and reeked of stale soykaf. But it was the blood on her exposed skin, rubbing where her and Hatari’s skin met that

made the current journey unpleasant.

Finally, they reached their destination, a sanitation closet. Spynner slid around the two and ran her coded passkey over the maglock. As the security system ran through all its algorithms, Hatari gave no indication if Prnc3ss’s weight was a burden in his arms—they’d done this dance many times before.

After several agonizing seconds, there was a click and a pressure-controlled hiss. Directly behind them, a wall parted with the merest whisper of pneumatic locks, bringing with it a vesper of cold to greet them, beckoning them inward. The trio quickly entered and Spynner closed the Murphy door behind them with a _whomp_ as the pressure locks reengaged, the illusion once more in place.

Hatari peeled Prnc3ss off him and sat her in her wheelchair, dried and semi-clotted blood stuck to the both of them like rust from an ancient sedan. Anything still wet and weeping felt prickly, like insects pulling at her wounds. Hatari tried to grab a medkit so he could at least clean her wounds, but Prnc3ss waved him off. Eagerness overwhelmed Prnc3ss. Now in her place of comfort, the gnome decker spun around, threw her Microtronica Azteca 300 on her lap, and wheeled herself to what she called her Integrated Control Center at the west side of the room. Meanwhile, Spynner hovered over the trideo display “War Table” in the center of the room, among cartons of old takeout and cans of energy drinks—they littered the area like small skyscrapers at the edge of a calm, black lake. That would soon change, if their paydata was worth anything.

Prnc3ss slotted a fiber-optic from her cyberdeck into the ICC; as she did, she risked a glance into the adjoining room through the heavy plastic dividers that separated the planning room away from the living area of their modest safehouse. The coffin motel, the Ditch Digger, was a front for a Grey Cell safehouse—when not in use, the Ditch Digger was just another coffin motel. Recently though, it had been “closed for renovation” and home to their little “family,” including Lance.

Her heart fluttered as she looked to the nineyear-old sitting on the floor, his hazel eyes lost in their own world as they so often were. Scattered

around him were dozens of puzzle books, each one mired in a multitude of colored markers. The doctors described him as “hyperlexic,” viewing numbers by color, and his markers created a kaleidoscope across his math puzzles.

It was beautiful, really.

Lance gave no indication he’d heard their arrival, but that wasn’t surprising. Besides the thick headset he wore to dampen sound, he rarely indicated he was aware of their presence. The best Prin could hope for were the occasional odd glances he threw her way or a shuffling sort of side hug she could never return, lest it make him quickly uncomfortable.

The ICC came online and she returned to her work, Sentinel-P drones activating along their rails, firing up all the trid displays. She could now see every corner and every floor of their safehouse. Though she wasn’t a rigger, she was able to operate the ICC and its drones via cold-sim, well enough to alert her to trouble. And they were expecting trouble. She just didn’t know what they were going to receive, or when.

Prnc3ss uploaded the paydata they’d filched from an incomplete control station of what their

Images flitted to the forefront of her vision, metahumans isolated in chambers, drained like juice boxes, and then broken down, molecule by molecule. She’d seen children nearly the same age as Lance, and the memory brought fresh tears to her eyes. They needed to make this right.

Please let this be worth it, she thought as she projected the data onto the trideo. Immediately, a gasp came from behind her, indicating it was something important. She turned to the war table.

Hatari, now in clean clothes and sporting his favorite Seattle Screamers hat, joined Spynner and Prin at the table. They stared together at the image projected before them.

Strange concentric lines, the color of sunbaked bone, ran from a large box-shaped construct which she knew to represent the Harvester by the points of latitude and longitude. As they expanded away from the center, they wove themselves into a tapestry, very much like a spider’s web.

“Ley lines,” Spynner breathed. “This is some kind of mapping of a convergence of leys.” She went silent for a moment, clearly hearing something the others did not. She then added, “Grandmother is certain,” referring to the mentor spirit who always seemed to be with her.

Prnc3ss disliked magic, but she couldn’t deny that Grandmother’s confirmation was a godsend. This was what Grey Cell had been trying for months to acquire! She quickly brought up the Transys Neuronet cypher installed in the ICC and overlaid it with the map. Suddenly, the lines became a series of runes, mostly indecipherable, but there were more than a few she could distinguish as letters or numbers. And yet, something seemed familiar. She then realized this wasn’t just a map, but also a kind of operating system. Smiling, Prnc3ss realized this was just the surface, like the title page of a holo on the Trid. Once she ran the cypher on the paydata, they could see it all … but that would take time. No choice, she told herself. Without consulting the team, she began the upload through the cypher.

“Why is that moving so slow?” Hatari asked. Prnc3ss spoke quickly, her eyes dancing over the streams of coding. “The Disian language is complex—nothing has been able to fully crack it yet. I think we were sent to get a piece of the puzzle to help complete the cypher.”

“How long?” the gruff ork asked.

“Twenty-two hours.”

“We don’t have twenty-two fraggin’ minutes, Prin!” Spynner said tersely. “Frag the full intel. Keep the data compressed, grab your son, and let’s bolt.”
