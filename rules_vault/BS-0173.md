Unlike others who’ve posted here, I don’t have a medical degree or an actual doctorate, so I’m going to gloss over some of the more technical details to talk about what matters: how biotech can make you a better you or turn your enemies into something you were not expecting.

Cybernetic prosthetics were not originally developed for knife-throwing blade bunnies but as

functional replacements for accident victims when limb cloning wasn’t an option. The first prosthetic hand was for a Roman general in 77 AD! Biotech was never envisioned to be a replacement for imperfections but a tool to make us more than we could dream. As our society evolves toward ever more strict standards of physical perfection and supermetahuman performance, there’s a rising demand for improvement. And the masses’ insecurity will keep them going back for more.

Neither bioware nor chrome is perfect, but each has its place. Chrome is usually stronger and silicon wires faster, but flesh is more malleable. It’s like a lump of clay that can be formed, not beat into a tool like chrome. I will let Black Knight and the others sell you on chrome. I am here to talk about bioware.

Bioware is by far the subtler and significantly more diverse material to work with. Another advantage is that biomodifications and bioware are harder to detect. Yes, the person walking around with one-meter horns and a tail is going to standout, but if it’s bioware, you’re not going to detect it with a MAD scanner. X-ray scans and other similar devices can’t tell the difference between it and the rest of your body. Yes, some very high-end medical scanners detect even cultured bioware, but these are rare. The thing most people have to be careful of is that biotech changes the way your body processes everyday activities. Most bioware presence can be determined through sophisticated analysis of metabolic fluid such as blood, urine, or fecal matter.

I know what you’re thinking: You don’t need a beginner’s course in bioware because there are more clinics in your area than police stations, but biotech goes far beyond that. I expect everyone here has a gun, and I hope you know how to use it, but why did you choose that piece? Bioware is the same. It may end up inside you, so I would hope you’d want to know more about it than you do your gun.

Biotech is in the food you eat, the cosmetics you wear, and as I previously mentioned, possibly the upgrades in your body. Biotech is used for power generation, scrubbing the air, and a thousand industrial applications from dyes to glues to, well, virtually everything. Before I get too carried away, I suppose it’s time to step off my soap box, take everything that has been posted here and that I found on the Matrix, and give you a whirlwind tour of the biotech of eons past.

I’m going to start with ancient history. There is evidence of dentistry and surgery from as far back as the Ice Age, and we survived said Ice Age by finding the limits of our fleshy bodies and then sticking thick fur from animals over it to shield us against the cold. Fashion, in many ways, was the first bioware. Painting our skin or coloring our hair via plant dyes, adorning ourselves with pretty stones or bones, even adapting local flora and fauna into weapons to make up for our shortfalls, we decided that if we didn’t have it, we’d take from what’s around us or create it ourselves. If you want something that is more obvious and still predates written history, look at cranial deformation, which was practiced on multiple continents. You can see all of these things with a simple Matrix search, but have you ever thought about that before now?

We all know that nuyen makes the world go around. So, we will move forward to the more modern era. The human race, as it was still being called, had established animal breeds, such as the diverse mixture of dogs or cattle, and the codification of plant life. We’d advanced far enough that we could completely change the banana from a small, seed-filled pod to a long, easily peeled, seedless treat that fit into our hands, exactly as we wanted it. We learned how to create delicious apples by grafting one tree into another and used that to create flowers for leisure. Biotech as an industry was truly born in 1635 with the Dutch Tulip Industry, a boom and bust of a legendary size that

revolved around unique color blends of the tulip, the outrageous sums that people were willing to pay for them, and the investment madness that followed. While the actual event only financially ruined about two dozen people, the entire process was recorded by English historians and codified, creating a story that’s since been passed down in most economic circles despite its inaccuracy.

Then, in the twentieth century, right before magic and dragons returned, things picked up due to three developments. The first was the establishment of certain strains of lab mice into official work. While mice had been used for centuries for testing, the creation of a sealed line of mice whose basic responses were so similar as to make recording differences easy was a huge milestone. Transgenic research hit a milestone with “spider goats,” goats whose udders had been modified to create silk protein like a spider, allowing for a massive increase in silk production. Then came Dolly the Sheep, the world’s first clone. She died at a young age, having a genetic vulnerability that had been overlooked, but she blazed the trail that we walk today. There was some pushback in all these cases, claiming that we were playing God or mistreating animals. When humanity became metahumans, the world had other issues, and those who wanted to pursue these fields were left to their own devices.

In 2032, Owen Whiting went to the doctor to take care of a minor injury (he’d broken his toe kicking a coffee table), and his standard blood sampling showed some interesting features. He agreed to have a few tests run to cut down his medical bills, and the anomaly was tracked down: He lacked the epitopes (sugar and protein “shells” on organ membranes) that are normally unique on a cellular level. When an ordinary organ was transplanted into a metahuman, the presence of this shell caused the host body to attack it as it would any foreign entity. Owen’s lack of this shell meant that his organs could be placed into another person’s body without fear of rejection. Shiawase paid him handsomely for copies of his genetic make-up and blood plasma, then they went to work. In short order, they had sustainable cultures of his cells, which they could then use as a base for any organ.

These organs, classified as type-O for patent purposes, were viable in fully ninety-six percent of the metahuman population, allowing Shiawase unparalleled access to medical technology. Even today, any type-O organ purchase slips money into Shiawase’s pocket due to licensing agreements. And Owen Whiting receives royalty payments to this day.

The food shortages of the ’30s and ’40s were eventually overcome with biotechnology, leading to a replacement of most food staples with soy, krill, mycoprotein, algae, and lupinus, though that last one is rarely talked about much.

There are many diverse topics in biotechnology being explored in the modern world, and often this reasearch is conducted outside of the public eye in factories, on farms, and in pharmacology labs. Most of us think of biotech as bioware and bio-sculpting, which are the primary areas I’ll be covering. There’s never been a point in our history where you could simply choose to be beautiful as easily as today, and quite frankly, the only reason to not be flawless is that you’re poor.

I’m not trying to upset anyone with that statement. It’s an easy thing to tell someone’s economic class based not just on what they wear, but how they look. Crooked teeth, blemished skin, obvious malnourishment—these are all problems that the poor suffer, and the wealthy avoid. The only thing standing between ugly ducklings and beautiful swans is a gold credstick.

As a quick reference for everyone, I have an easy-to-follow chart.

Cutting-edge biotech breakthroughs make the news every day, and megacorp R&D budgets remain bloated. Expect continual developments to come from the field of xenotransplantation (the transplantation of non-immunogenic organs from a critter into a metahuman). I believe we are now seeing some of these items come to market with new bioweapons and biosenses. The growth of transgenic organs and bioware within an adult metahuman is also heavily funded.

So, I go back to my original question, have you ever thought about what is in that piece of bioware you are installing, or are you looking at it like it’s just another tool, like a gun?

When it first started, terminology in the biotech world was somewhat muddled. A couple of decades ago, Shiawase dominated the market and started pushing their own modified version of the system onto the rest of the industry. As expected, someone didn’t like that. Dr. Lars Blumenstein, who had worked for several other companies using his contacts, got the rest of the industry to push back. When no one could agree on a new system, things went back to pre-Awakening terms. To me,


it’s a little scary that one of the most cutting-edge industries is using terms from over a hundred years ago. But here we are. I guess doctors, engineers, and scientists like outdated terms.
