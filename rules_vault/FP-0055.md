## **Tactics**

**Trying to pin down the various tactics of this cast is rough, but one thing is for sure: They use shadowrunners almost exclusively for their efforts. Some have a small stable of favorites, but most simply reach out for the right people in the right place at the right time. That being said, the moral limits of the runners they hire often only partially defne the lengths to which they will go to get what they want.**

**As for the rest of their behaviors, you have a spectrum to deal with in almost every aspect of the job. Hiring, intel, access, assistance, payment method, and the all-important hand-off can not only change from one cartomancer to another, but some of these fanatics are so scattered they change up styles in the middle of the job. The contract that started with a very open and giving Mr. Johnson could close out with a hyper-paranoid and super-squirrelly hand-off.**

**Let’s go one step at a time and talk spectrums, starting with hiring. The cartomancers don’t operate with a stable of Mr. Johnsons on call like the corps. Sometimes they use a fxer as a middleman; sometimes they show up to hire you themselves. The important part is to use this as an initial assessment of your newest boss. Those who show up themselves are the most dangerous because they don’t care if you know who they are, meaning they feel they are too powerful to need to hide. This isn’t the rule, just the tendency. Some of the members just don’t see the hiring of people to acquire a card as an illegal act. They hide behind the anonymity of the runners and the fact that blame lands on them frst. Plus, it’s easy to buy your way out of those kinds of charges.**

**If they use an intermediary, whether it’s a Mr. Johnson or your fxer, you know they are at least trying to work in the shadows the way they are intended. That’s a good sign for their professionalism—or for the amount of trid shows they watch. You’re not going to get everything you need to know from how they hire you, but it’s a decent initial measure.**

**Let’s talk intel. Some of these people come in and drop a datafle on you that makes your job cake. Some drop a datafle that has so much conjecture in it you might as well start fresh. How much they tell you can indicate several things. They tell you how much effort they’ve already put in. They tell how much they trust you and often whether you are the only team getting hired or if there are multiple. Poor initial intel often points to a race for the prize. They don’t know anything because it’s new, hot info and they just need action, or they don’t know anything because they’re too lazy to look and fgure spending money up front for a few teams is better than spending money on the initial legwork. I’ll accept that sometimes weak initial intel is a sign of respect, it’s their way of saying they trust your skills. But since most of these wanks don’t know squat about us, you can usually ignore this possibility.**

**Access is all about trust. While some of these slags can easily get you access to anywhere and anything in the world, whether they trust you or not is often defned by how much of their world-weaving power they are willing to show and share. Sure, some like to do it to show their power, but most only grant that access to teams they trust. The other side of allowing it is speed and effcacy. If they want this card and they know time is of the essence, then they’re going to offer every shortcut possible. If you aren’t trusted before you step through that door, you can bet you’re being tested for the next time (or if there will be a next time). The nice thing about their access is its variety. They’re not usually limited like corporate types but instead provide access via money rather than corporate infuence or professional associations.**

**Access is one thing, but assistance is another. Some of these kooks want to go with you, others offer some extra muscle or magic or want to send someone along to verify the card. None of that is the kind of assistance we really want, but we often don’t have much of a choice if we want the work. Since these expeditions are often personal or driven by the desires of a fanatic, this kind of assistance is common, and we need to adjust our planning and plotting accordingly. Just to re-emphasize, be very wary of turning down assistance from this group. They have huge egos and deep psychoses that you could easily trigger, turning what could have been a cakewalk into a nightmare.**

**The Echoes have some of the most interesting payment methods at their disposal, all in an effort to demonstrate their power and worldliness. Many pay with valuables gained from the activation of previous cards or rare objects that demonstrate their reach, especially their criminal reach. These items are usually valued far higher than the agreed-upon payment, but they leave your team to fence something that is often outside their usual purview. This isn’t just about making a display of power to the runners, though. They usually pay with an object desired by another Echo. It’s a power move over the runners and within their society, showing others that they have acquired another card.**

**Finally, we talk about the all-important handoff. Echoes are all about grandstanding and the show. Even when the meet was as back room as back room gets, the drop is usually a show. A brazen display that often involves activating the cards you have brought them. For runners with a moral compass, this can be problematic, as the Echoes don’t usually care about the collateral damage, and activating cards can have some wild and wacky side effects. Even if it’s not about a moral issue, runners often get dragged into these side effects, and nobody wants to get dragged into drek they aren’t getting paid for.**

- **Most expect their efforts to be heavy on the magical support and a little thin on the tech side, but that’s rarely the case. Most of the Echoes aren’t highly skilled mages or shamans—they’re regular people. They’ve grown up in our technological world and generally know more about it than they do the arcane. Make sure you have a decent hacker on the team, or you’re going to get scragged before you even start the gig.**
