## The Nanosecond of Terror

The Nanosecond Buyout in January 2033 was the validation of all fears about algorithm-based trading. Damien Knight’s cluster of expertly programmed computers (programmed by no less than Lucien Cross, who later founded CATCo) connected to the Stockholm Exchange and executed a series of transactions so complicated that few metahumans have been able to approximate what the process entailed. By the end of the minute or so that the transactions took, three corporations ceased to exist. Two multi-millionaires lost their fortunes, three others made theirs (Lucien Cross being one), and Damien Knight acquired 22 percent of Ares Industries.

- Otaku of the Plastikmen tribe were the first to mentally process it and form a way to explain the process naturally (of **course** it would be the communist collective tribe). Their dedication to the Marxian economic theory and scientific analysis saw the whole thing as a means to propagandize on why end-stage capitalism and the corporatism that succeeded it was fragile and illusory. It’s a shame that none of them survived long enough to make

- that argument before Crash 2.0.

There is a twist here, of course. Knight and associates used trading algorithms, but not in the typical sense. Their programs were not monitoring conditions broadly and executing operations based on general trends or shifts in the market. They were (as best we understand them) nested series of specific instructions that perhaps looked at each other for triggers but paid little if any attention to the rest of the market. Rather than the accidental malice evidenced in trend-seeking algorithms, this demonstrated actual malice, weaponizing these tools to do actual harm. The more traditional algorithms have the advantage over

metahuman competition by virtue of the fact that they can look broader and react faster than metahumans can. The Nanosecond Buyout programming certainly relied on that speed, but it also used an in-depth knowledge of corporate holdings, relationships, and financial tools to exploit advantages most people didn’t know existed. That is to say, rather than trying to refine the algorithm by better analysis of trends, Knight and Cross refined it with better inside knowledge.

This pointed toward either a bold new future or an incredibly terrifying one. The real treasure might not be a perfectly designed algorithm, but an artificial intelligence—or group of them—that could use their insider knowledge, access, and speed to accomplish amazing feats.

As often tends to be the case in the Sixth World, when a terrifying option is on the table, that’s what we receive.
