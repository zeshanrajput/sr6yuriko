## **Deception**

**When it comes to dealing with your own team, your contacts, and (usually) your employers, honesty and integrity are key. When it comes to getting paid for doing crime, however, the work often requires duplicity and deception. A convincing lie can sometimes get you past security systems your hacker would have a problem bypassing or guards you can’t afford to fght your way past.**

**The quickest and simplest application of this is fast-talk. A fast-talk is a simple and believable lie told in the moment for a short-term beneft. The classic example is getting someone to look the other way for a moment. It’s not going to get you through the run, but it can buy you a little bit of time—with wired-up street samurai on your side, sometimes that’s all you need. This form of deception is reactive and therefore requires improvisation—it’s something you do in the moment in response to an unexpected setback, such as bumping into a guard patrol that isn’t where you expected them to be, getting a bad result from a scanner that the hacker hasn’t had time to spoof yet, or running into a batch of gangers on the way to a meet. The most effective fast-talks are incredibly simple. You won’t have time to spin a long story or set up expectations to exploit, so you need to rely on what you have to work with in the immediate situation. You should be able to glean something of your target’s expectations from the context, so try to come up with something that matches those expectations—or suddenly and shockingly overturns them. The best use of a fast-talk is to set up a follow-up action that allows you to escape, set up an attack from someone on your team, or give your hacker time to back you up by editing a sensor feed, turning off an alarm, or resetting a device. A successful fast-talk usually isn’t going to get you through a run on its own, but it might be enough to get you out of a jam.**

**you can exploit. Most runs are fairly short-term operations, so all you need to manage is a quick con. Most runs will beneft from using a quick con of one form or another, while some runs might hinge on a con. Unlike a fast-talk, a con is something you plan ahead. The frst key to a quick con is legwork. In order to pull off a con, you must have some understanding of your target’s circumstances, motivations, personality, and expectations. Once you’ve gathered as much information about your target as possible, identify the elements that you have little or no control over—in most cases, these are the target’s personality and motivations. Some circumstances will also ft into this category, while others might be possible to manipulate or contrive. You may also have the opportunity to set up expectations, though exploiting established expectations tends to be more effective. The most effective cons are designed to nudge or steer your target by exploiting their understanding, perceptions, and expectations rather than upsetting or supplanting them with something new. Like with most plans, the fewer moving parts you are working with, the fewer chances there are for things to go awry—simple and subtle works best.**

**On the other hand, sometimes you can get things done by shocking your target out of their comfort zone and keeping them off-balance. The most common type of job this works for is an extraction or a heist. If you can convince your extraction target or the person in charge of security that something is wildly wrong and your team is there to swoop in and make things right, you might be able to get in and out with your objective without having to fre a shot. The key to making this work is shock and urgency—if your target has enough time to think about what’s going on, they will eventually see through your attempt at deception. Your initial approach needs to put them immediately off-balance, ideally into a state of panic. Then you need to keep them panicked until it’s too late. When you are preparing for this type of con, set up as many overwhelming circumstances and distractions as possible, and be ready to trigger them whenever it seems that your target is starting to get their wits back. You need to start strong and keep up the pressure all the way through, or else everything will fall apart. These types of cons are quite risky, and even if you do everything right, they might still fall apart, so be sure to have at least one contingency plan in place.**

- **My favorite con for this is convincing someone that their security has been compromised, and you’ve been sent in to fix things. It’s great, because you can set up your own distractions to prove that they have a breach. The hardest part is keeping a straight face the whole time!**

- **Serenade**

**Long cons are generally outside the scope of most shadowruns. A long con involves gaining the target’s trust over a long period of time, so that they rely upon you or build up impressions and expectations that ft your design. Then when the time is right, you subvert those expectations and betray their trust in order to achieve your objectives. Long cons are useful for gaining access to information that is extremely well-protected, convincing an extraction target to leave willingly, severely damaging or bringing down an organization, setting someone up for a fall, and pulling off an ambitious heist.**

**A long con requires getting your foot in the door, planting a hook, backing up your story with planted evidence and false trails, and allowing time to pass for your mark to become comfortable with and used to the web of lies they’ve become ensnared in. While it’s possible to pull of something like this in a manner of weeks, it works even better if you have months to plan and execute an operation. As I said, very few runs are on a time scale that allows for this sort of thing, so it doesn’t come up as often in our line of work. But if you know that you’ll be dealing with a particular individual or organization over the long term, it might be useful to set up a long con—even if you don’t know what you’re going to use it for down the road. You never know when you might need to have some strings ready to pull.**
