**Every time a sensor beeps in alarm, a hacking attempt is logged, or a suspicious individual is spotted near a secure location, the metahuman security personnel have to choose how to deal with it. If they went straight to a red alert every time something unusual happened, they would annoy the other employees and interfere with effcient operations. So instead of going straight to maximum response, the guards have to evaluate the threat. Is it a false alarm? Someone casing your security to look for a weakness? Someone fragging around where they shouldn’t be just for kicks? Matrix hacking attempts happen so often that security spiders rarely report a single breach in real-time. Unless further ongoing tampering is discovered, they’re much more likely to just add it to their logs and let the IC look into it.**

**When it comes to physical intrusions, anything that wanders into extraterritorial proper-**

**ty without authorization is fair game for almost any level of physical response, but I bet you’d be surprised how often an entitled manager forgets their keycard at home and expects the guards to just let them in without any hassle. Rough up the wrong person, and you’re in deep drek—even if it was their fault for not following procedures. This varies depending on the place, how many people work there, and how isolated it is. In urban locations, security has a lot more to keep track of and tends to have a more gradual response curve. An isolated R&D site, however, is likely to treat passersby as potential security threats. Zero zones are another matter entirely—those guards have it easy. They’re authorized to blow the drek out of anything or anyone who trespasses, and even upper management treads carefully in such spaces. “Shoot frst and don’t bother with questions” is their motto, or so I’ve heard. Everywhere else, the security personnel have to run through their own version of a cost-beneft analysis when considering a response to a potential threat. Policies and procedures often prescribe those responses, but in the real world things rarely fall into one easily recognized category. If you have the chance to read over the security procedures that a target location has in place, do it. If you can fgure out how to do things in such a way that the guards don’t have a prescribed response for your actions, it will give you a solid edge over them.**
