## **Cooling Their Heels**

**The cold ferrocrete walls. The single steel table that’s bolted to the foor with a couple of chairs. A couple of cameras and maybe a panel of one-way glass. The interview room at the station house has been the subject of trids for decades. Most of those conjure up images of intense interrogations by dirty cops, and to be honest, they’re not wrong. It’s just an incomplete view. It turns out that it’s a great place to meet new and interesting people.**

**Personally, I hate that room. It’s stressful for everyone involved and trust is low. From the suspect’s point of view, they don’t know if someone’s behind the glass or if the cameras are recording when the detective tells them it’s off. For the detective, you need information, and the other person doesn’t plan on giving it to you. It just ends up being a dirty game of manipulation.**

**But what if you don’t want to get information right now? That person across the table has connections or skills that you may need in the future. That changes the dynamic of the room from information extraction to one where you’re trying to build a rapport. It really is a shame that the room works against you there.**

**So, why use the interview room at all? It’s really about plausible deniability for both you and the suspect. Taking them out for a whiskey at a local speakeasy is just going to get both of you identifed and put on a list. Better to haul their hoop in for some crime or as part of an active investigation. The second part is that you don’t know if they’re willing to work with you long term.**

**That shifts the strategy you’re going to use.**

**Your frst step is fnding a way to build trust. And that’s no easy task with everything working against you, from your badge to the fact that they might even still be cuffed. I’ve got no magic here that will help you connect. It’s really about connecting at a metahuman level and getting them to see you as more than just some fragging cop. Maybe talk about what you’re after. Maybe talk about where you come from. Find common ground.**

- **Magic definitely plays a part here. One of my favorite spells in these situations is Nudge. A lot of the spells of influence are obvious, but this spell is nice and subtle. Give them a bump around liking you or cautious trust. There’s one municipality that’s hired me for just that purpose. They even**



**have a hole in the wall for me to cast through and not need to be in the room.**

- **Haze**

- **Nick’s different. The rhetoric that one bad egg ruins the reputation of hardworking law enforcement service personnel is complete drek from my experience. With Nick, he’s the rare good egg and actually gives a damn about Chicago and its people. He’s probably the only cop in Chicago that I’m willing to (somewhat) trust.**

- **Old Crow**

- **Fragging Haze. Ignoring the fact that he’s a disgusting piece of drek, using magic to manipulate is the fastest way to permanently destroy any chance at trust.**

- **Pistons**

**If you can fnd common ground, it makes empathy easier. This is where some detectives are better than others, because they honestly want to know more and don’t just see the person as a tool. You need to really want to put yourself in their shoes. Understand their fears and desires.**

**Empathy is easier if you’re good at active listening. You’ve seen that guy who asks you a question and immediately gets that distracted look as if he’s checking his MeFeed or messages in AR. Don’t do that. Be present. I’ve found that asking open-ended questions and letting them speak is a good start. Make sure you ask follow-up questions that touch on the stories they tell you. Remember, it’s not always about getting information out of them in this case; it’s about making them feel heard.**

**The other part of this is to remain patient. One of the beat cops just hauled in a guy that you think could be a real asset. You don’t want to jump straight into there. Let them cool off. Better yet, let them get stuck in their own head. This might seem counter to everything I said above, but jumping straight in there is going to feel like you need them more than they need you. Letting them stew for a bit is just going to help you. And if there’s more than one, like a runner team, make sure to keep them separate.**

- **Fragging cops pull you in for some dumb, trumped-up issue and expect that sitting there in a cold room is going to make you sing. All it does for me is just make me more and more slotted off that they’ve got nothing on me. Joke’s on them if they want to get me to trust them.**

- **Borderline**

- **Getting angry doesn’t really help you. But really, law enforcement is going to use a bunch of tricks to try and get you off balance.**

- **Hard Exit**

- **I definitely can’t control how you’re going to feel. I understand where you’re coming from. And in the end, we’re not bringing**

**you in just because you could be useful long-term. There’s a legitimate reason to bring you in, and even when you’re angry, your knowledge still could help our active case.**

- **Nick Ryder**

**The second part of patience is when you enter the room. Don’t head straight to either the info you want right now or the long-term “will you work for me.” Instead, build that rapport and learn about them. Also, being stuck in a room for hours means they’re going to get hungry or thirsty. Find out what food they want and order it. Bring it in and get yourself something. There’s something primal about breaking bread with someone. Remember that they are going to be pushing to get out of there, so you’ll need to be creative about why they need to be there so long.**

**The other part of having patience is knowing that they won’t be a good informant after the frst time you haul them in. It does mean that you’re going to have to bump into them all the time. The real key will be building more and more trust each time you do. That might be coming in sooner to the room the second time. Maybe have their favorite food or drink ready when you walk in. Show that you listen. The other is based on what they leave with when the interrogation is done.**

- **It’s because the food and drink have a poison or drug to make you more pliable.**

- **Plan 9**

- **Definitely not poison. Doing paperwork for a person of interest in custody is a pain in the hoop. I haven’t really thought about using drugs to manipulate someone. A sandwich is much cheaper.**

- **Juice Box**

**Leaving the room has to come with incentives for them. You still hold the cards, though. The easiest of those is to provide them with a way out of their current trouble. Maybe it’s letting them off with a warning or letting them know that you’re making this go away. But there’s a bunch of other benefts you could offer that make the connection. My personal feeling is to keep it light in the beginning, but that’s mostly because we’re detectives and not trillionaires. Later on, you can ramp it up.**

**The main way that I try to appeal to the person across the table is showing how I’m working toward the greater good. But the only way for this to work is knowing what good they care about. It might be the community, children, or something else. The appeal is the hard part when they’ve got some really serious, immediate problems. It’s hard to care about how the corps or gangs are hurting their turf when they just want a hot meal.**
