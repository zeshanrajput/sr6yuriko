## **The Long Con**

**Now we get into the long con, where you put more time into setup and execution. This could be the whole job, because if you do it right, you should walk away with something special.**

- **Ah, the long con. Clean. Elegant. Professional.**

- **Kat-o’-Nine-Tales**

- **Bulldrek. About a million moving parts and a whole cluster of variables you know about, and the lethal mountain of ones you don’t. Improvisation makes it adaptable, but it’s anything but precise.**

- **Pistons**

- **And some of the moving parts have mouths that, no matter what, won’t stay shut.**

- **Thorn**

- **Three people can keep a secret if two of them are dead. That’s the philosophy that ensures that I always have work to do.**

- **> Rainbowsmite**

- **The dead can be exceptionally eloquent.**

- **> Many-Names**

- **And the cost of stacking the deck gets higher and higher with time, research, and engineering. By the time you know something, there’s a chance it’s changed. Spend a month sweetening the target, he gets reassigned to another facility the day before the op. Infiltrate the janitorial staff, get downsized. Time and cred down the drain, and an increasingly unhappy client.**

- **> Crackshot**

- **But all that work still can be salvaged in some way, right?**

- **> Gillette**

- **Well, you may not be able to execute the contract, but sometimes the information you’ve gained can be sent to the client so they don’t make you the next target of a run.**

- **Crackshot**

- **Just about any data is paydata to someone. If you find yourself on the bad end of a long con, you don’t just chalk everything up to a dry run and toss your gains in the bin. Find out what’s salvageable and monetize it. Sure, that employee who you’ve been building up a whole pile of blackmail on is no longer at the facility you were planning to hit, but he’s somewhere, and that**

**data is valuable to someone. A good fixer can help connect your “junk” data with a buyer and help you recoup the costs of lost time and effort. Does very little to get the current job done (or an angry Johnson off your ass), but it’s still a source of nuyen.**

**> Webwalker**

- **Frag, if you can find anything that may make Mr. Johnson nervous, keep it in your back pocket. If things go well, it’s leverage for later. If things go to hell, you may have a bargaining chip.**

- **Doctor Spin**

- **Kids, let’s remember that sometimes Mr. Johnson wants you to steal *just one thing* because it keeps you under the radar. You go selling extra bits you nicked or data you lifted, you’re contributing to a trail that leads back to you.**

- **Slamm-0!**

- **I can’t tell whether to congratulate you for finally growing up or teasing you for sounding like the same old timers you used to mock on Shadowland.**

- **Netcat**

- **All the same to me, darling.**

- **Slamm-0!**

- **Also, every bit of data you gather while working on a long con touches other data, which can lead to alternate methods for completing your original task. To continue the example, if your mark got transferred to a different facility the day before a run was supposed to go down, dig through the data you’ve collected. Look at who they’re consorting with in the office and why. Chances are good there are five weaknesses in the corporate structure you missed for every one you found during initial intel. Each of those is an opportunity. Make no mistake, your well-planned long con has now become a short con, but if you take a breath and do a solid data sift, it can be a very well-informed short con.**

- **Daisychain**

- **Fair enough, but every alteration introduces new variables, and you’re no longer in control of the situation. You’re playing catchup, and let’s face it, most runs are on a timetable. Unless you’re not so much running as freelancing (i.e., you’re a plain criminal), that’s pressure you don’t need.**

- **Pistons**

- **I think there’s a misunderstanding here. Pulling a Knight Gambit (yeah, probably based on Damien Knight’s infamous Nanosecond Buyout) isn’t something you do in a week, necessarily, or even a month. Long games are all about patience. The definition of the long game is that you can take all the time you need to line up every variable in your favor.**

- **Shogo**

- **No one can line up every variable. Also, a Knight Gambit is when you win no matter what happens.**

- **Bull**

- **I don’t believe in fairytales. I do, however, believe in faeries. Nasty little snots. Avoid them.**

- **Daisychain**

- **Fine, I got the definition wrong. But you’d be amazed what the right acumen and preparation can do to make it look like inevitable success.**

- **Shogo**

- **You’re such an expert, go on and explain.**

- **> Glitch**

- **Information. That’s the key. You’re collecting as thorough a model of data as you can about every place, every person, every scheduled event, every probability based on history. Psychological models of the individual who will or might be involved and how to work them. The pathfinding of patrols. The layout of the sites where the gig happens. If you have a plan for maximum contingencies and a response to at least the most likely variables, then you’ve already determined the fight. It’s chess. It’s thinking twenty steps ahead in every direction. It’s backups for the backups.**

- **Shogo**

- **If you think the level of data gathering is bordering on paranoid, you’re probably on the right track. Information can be useful for the current job or negotiating the specifics for any future work. As well as help improvising those unknown types of situations that seem to pop up more often than not. And if things really smell fishy, you can find use for that to gracefully turn down the job, noting the undisclosed risks as the reason why to Mr. Johnson.**

- **Kia**

- **And information covers both hard data (like maps and security protocols) and soft data (knowing most salarymen won’t use the side alley cause it smells like stale soykaf and feet). And that information is always of use to someone out there.**

- **> Hannibelle**

- **That’s a good philosophy for any kind of run, really.**

- **> Red**

- **Sure. But the big differences here are timetables and budgets. Five thousand nuyen and a weekend to pull the extraction target isn’t a long game, and it cannot be. You can’t buy the information (which is untrustworthy, anyway), and you don’t have time to gather it yourself. Those are short cons. The long con has money. Money for bribes. Money for forgers. Money to assume another identity and become the target’s friend or lover, for the apartment you invite him back to after the dinner and drinks you bought him. For the software to learn his micro expressions. For access to his toiletries to get a bio sample for chemical analysis to make tailored pheromones that will draw him into being a willing accomplice in his own kidnapping. And for the safehouse and cameras and takeout as you stalk the poor fragger.**



**I’m not even getting into the sheer clusterfuck of data for doing the same for everyone else in their orbit. You can use them, too. Alienate him from his lover or family. Emphasize any dissatisfaction he has where he is and drop the right lures for what he wants where you want him. Isolate him from his friends and peers. Compromise the people meant to be watching him, whether for his security or his “lifestyle management.” Change the terrain of his whole existence so that what was a fortress around him becomes a slide into your client’s pocket. He won’t even know he threw everything away until it’s too late.**

- **Shogo**

- **Does it occur to any of you that this is some really creepy shit? Like, gaslighting, manipulative, awful, life-ruining drek?**

- **> Sunshine**

- **It is, in my humble opinion, the creepiest part of running. And my specialty is wetwork! But the good side is that you may not be the gaslighter in the whole scenario. Most of the time, I can’t lie myself out of a Stuffer Shack takeaway bag. But I sure as heck can serve as backup or the distraction. I can pull attention or serve as the beatstick if something goes tits up. I can live with that. And if the mark is a grade-A slotface who has less compassion than a rabid constipated devil rat, I don’t feel guilty about doing a few ballroom waltzes with the fella. (No offense to the rest of da ratties out there. Yer adorable.)**

- **Rainbowsmite**

- **I absolutely agree. I’ve never done it. When I worked for Yakashima, this was the kind of thing I was keeping an eye open for. My job was literally to recognize this exact situation and subvert it.**

- **Shogo**

- **I take it you were the “Lifestyle Manager?”**

- **Kia**

- **Indeed. Externally, you’re an assistant. Internally, you’re monitoring your charge and everyone in their circle for any aberrant or threatening behavior. Ironically, that ameliorates my disgust regarding manipulating them.**

- **Shogo**

- **How so?**

- **Red**

- **The corporation is already doing all of that to them already. If you are worth keeping, they’re playing you on a psychological level every day. They put their agenda into your every breath, meal, friendship, dream, and orgasm. The ethical thing to do is liberate them into actual freedom and deprogram them. But then, this is a rabbit hole that only neo-anarchists like falling into, and I’m enough of a realist to recognize that the biggest employers of shadow assets are as bad as the forces they target. With few exceptions, we are bad people who commit crimes on behalf of bad people. Your personal ethics I will leave to your own conscience.**

- **Shogo**

- **If it helps you sleep at night, it’s probably better to lie to a stranger than shoot them?**

- **Thorn**

- **Eh, bullet wounds that don’t punch your ticket eventually heal. The right lie stings forever.**

- **Daisychain**

- **And sometimes can cause more damage to a person. Once heard someone say ya can kill someone only once, but you can break them over and over and over again. And considering the latter, a quick death could be a mercy.**

- **XXL**

- **The loss of innocence is as sure an injury as the lost limb.**

- **> Many-Names**

- **You’re breaking my heart here, old man.**

- **Rigger X**

- **There are some component plays that serve a role in long games but don’t usually work well on their own as a short con. The Handcuff Escort is a classic. If you need a crew(member) escorted into a secure location, go in as a “prisoner.” Obviously this depends on a few setup factors. You could get one person in with a full disguise and background workup and use that one to get everyone else in. You might have a contact or compromised asset to do it (dangerous, especially as you’ll be very vulnerable). You also need to be sure you can get all the gear you’ll need close enough when the op breaks out of the cover story. But in terms of moving personnel assets, you could do worse.**

- **Doctor Spin**

- **Even better when the crew has that distinctive street-merc look. Little tells like neon mohawks and face tattoos. Having them in cuffs and under guard makes them stand out the right way and disarms observers from thinking they’re a threat. After all, how tough could they be? If they’re caught, the fight is over and they can go about their business, right?**

- **Prime Runner**

- **Folks will let their guards down once a threat has been neutralized. Heck, setting up that mindset may make the con even easier to sell. People freshly feeling safe and secure are less likely to question everything. Less adrenaline, maybe?**

- **> Chainmaker**

- **If you’re going to do the Handcuff Escort, be sure to apply a little Backalley Makeover to your “prisoner.” Make it look like they put up a fight. Walking up to the gate with a street tough who looks like they’d stab their own grandma for ten nuyen all cuffed up yet without a scratch on ’em is a good way to announce to security that you are full of drek. Your volunteer needs to look like they went down fighting. If you really want to sell the illusion, rough up everyone in on the con. Make it look like bringing this target in was costly and painful.
