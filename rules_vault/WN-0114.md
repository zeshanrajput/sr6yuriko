## To The Shadows That Never Sleep

_If the runners are already local, you can skip ahead to Settling In (p. 148). Otherwise, read the following passage when they land:_

Your flight is only forty-five minutes behind schedule when it pulls up to the gate in Newark. That would’ve been fine if not for the crying babies, angry sararimen, and that family of trolls who absolutely did not think they needed to purchase another seat to accommodate their size. It’s another twenty-five minutes before you’re off the plane, luggage in hand, and heading for the exit. Waiting in the crowd is a tall elf with a rainbow-colored afro. He slouches against the wall, seemingly oblivious to the stream of humanity pulsing by him. A quick peek into AR, and you notice your name flashing in a neon graffiti script over his head. All of your names. He notices you noticing, smiles, and pushes himself up off the wall. He says, “I’m Eager. I’m the guy with the keys to your future.”

Eager hands each of the runners a copper-stripped CityPass ID. Entering New York via Newark means passing through the Newark Border Station. He takes them from the airport to the PATH train station, where they will board the train to Terminal station in NY. The station is a fortress manned by two dozen Transit Cops armed with big guns and bad attitudes (use Lone Star Swat Officer, p. 207, _SR6_ ). They treat entry into the station like a border crossing, because for all intents and purposes, it is.

The runners must pass through the security gate, which functions as a MAD scanner 6, olfactory sensor 6, and motion sensor 6. A combat mage stands just beyond the gate, scanning each person for magical devices and asking for paperwork when she detects something (use Lone Star Combat Mage, p. 207, _SR6_ ). The process is quick and brutal. At one point, the scanners blare, and the person who was trying to go through turns back and runs for the exit. Two officers jump on him and proceed to beat him with batons. People sigh and roll their eyes but are otherwise undisturbed by the event. If any runners start to intervene, Eager lays a hand on the runner’s shoulder and shakes his head.

Once through security, the runners are allowed to board the train with the horde of other blue-collar workers trying to reach the city. Play up the idea of normality here. The transfer into the city is what commuters do every day. As strange and difficult as it may seem, it’s just another Tuesday to the people around them.
