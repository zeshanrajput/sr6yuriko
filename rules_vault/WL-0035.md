## **Mongolian Death Worm**

**prey-like mouth. This mouth is flled with razor-sharp teeth. In addition, there are a number of tentacle-like antennas that it uses to locate prey. The young worms are typically red in color with segments along its body length. As they grow larger, they form a hardened chiton around its body along with small, armored appendages. The armor of the two behemoths we found were a blue-green color. It’s unknown if those are the only colors that the armor will exhibit. In addition to the appendages, there are also sensory organs down the side to help sense any movement in the soil around them.**

**Contamination of the soil can cause havoc on our natural ecosystems. In the steppes of Mongolia, this contamination has led to the creation of a whole breed of worms that has become acclimated to the heavy metals, toxic runoff, and other industrial compounds that have leeched into the soil. And they say what doesn’t kill you only makes you stronger. What was discovered was the Mongolian death worm (** _**Megascolides oligoi**_ **), also known as oligoi-khorkhol.**

**The majority of specimens found about a decade ago were scary and large, but nothing compared to some of the more recent fnds. It’s also possible that what we are seeing is a new, larger variation of the death worm. Those original worms were as large as four meters long and about seventy kilograms. The recent ones we have seen are tens-of-meters long and about as big around as a city bus.**

**The Mongolian death worm is a nocturnal hunter due to the nature of its UV-light-sensitive skin. Even the larger specimens with a hardened shell appear to be susceptible to the light. This suits the creature fne, as it is essentially blind, relying heavily on its ability to sense movement within the nearby ground. The worms will typically live in burrows in the ground and are solitary hunters. When a worm senses prey, it will sneak up on it before spraying its corrosive saliva at the creature and then striking their future dinner with those powerful mandibles.**

**Death worms are fairly rare, so when we’re talking about newer specimens, we’re really just referring to two recent fnds. The frst one was eleven meters long, one meter in diameter, and weighed 250 kilograms. The second specimen was twenty-three meters long, two meters in diameter, and weighed 400 kilograms. My guess is that these are very unusual, and you are most likely to see the smaller yet still terrifying variety.**

- **Some reports say that the larger ones may generate an electrical strike or something similar, caused by the friction of moving through the soil. I would love it if someone else is able to get some recorded evidence of this.**

**The lifecycle of the Mongolian death worm is based on the life stage. The adult worms will lay eggs on the edge of the desert around the steppes of Mongolia. Once the worms hatch, they grow and begin migrating toward the steppe grasslands. Once they reach breeding age, they fnd a mate, share some DNA, and then head back to the desert to lay some eggs. The other thing to note is that the eggs are unprotected for the days between laying and hatching. Many of the thousands of eggs don’t hatch and become food for other creatures.**

- **Their eggs are a delicacy, but because of the toxic nature of their environment, they should not be consumed in large amounts. The most common way to serve them is by fermenting them. This doesn’t really get rid of any chemicals but adds a salty flavor.**

**Once the worms hatch, they are approximately ten centimeters in length. They will double their size every month as they feed upon nearly any creature they can sink their mandibles into. The worms generally stay in their vast network of burrows and tunnels and only come up to feed at night. And while the death worm is a solitary hunter, it’s not aggressive to others of its species. So in those dark tunnels, the oligoi-khokhol ignores others of its kind unless during the breeding season.**
