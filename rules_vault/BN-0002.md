January in Siberia is as cold and inhospitable as any place on Earth that metahumanity has settled, yet the pack leaders’ paws crunched down on the icy ground with no discomfort. The killing winds blowing through the leafl ess trees did no more the ruffl e the thick fur coats of the pack.

The pack had been sent out to deal with a Russian patrol that entered their woodland territory just before nightfall. The Russians believe the shifters of Yakut to be primitive beasts. While some may be, many are not. The high-fl oating observation drones spotted the telltale heat signatures of the patrols passing. It was up to the pack to locate and eliminate them.

The pack spread out in a large semi-circle downwind from the Russian patrol. Lyall took up the pack leader’s position in the center. The alpha-female, Rieka, was ten meters to his right, already in position to tear the throat out of the Russian picket huddling behind a tree to shelter from the wind. To Lyall’s left was his rival for alpha-male and oldest friend, Ice.

There was no trace of that rivalry for the moment—no time for pack politics when the enemy is at hand. Now was time to hunt. As a single entity, the pack leaped forward, and the two hapless Russian soldiers picketing the small camp were dead before they could shout. But someone in the Russian camp was still alert.

The fi rst shot rang out, and Ice’s body fl ew back. The rifl e round tore through Ice’s shoulder and out his rib cage on the other side. Ice’s body fell to a curled up heap with a loud yip, steam and blood coming from the gaping wounds. More shots and shouts sounded from the Russian camp, but then it was time for tooth and claw.

Tents were torn open, as the soldiers were beset by wolves working as a team. Disarming, pinning, tripping, harassing, and leaving the soldiers open for the killing blow. Throats were torn, bellies were ripped open, and the screams of the dying mixed with the packs howls and barks.

It was over quickly. The Russian soldiers lay dead or dying, scattered around the remains of their tents and heaters. Lyall trotted over to Ice’s body to see the pale blue eyes of his rival fl icker open with a shuddering breath. The wounds were beginning to close. Lyall’s huge furry head and blood splattered muzzle shook with a snort. Typical Russians, sending soldiers out with cold weather gear, high-powered rifl es, and only standard ammunition to hunt shifters.

With a thought, Lyall brought his commlink back online and signaled the waiting salvage team. Normal bullets may not be good for hunting shifters, but they work just fi ne on the enemy.
