## Backgrounds

Racing riggers consider themselves to be the purest form of driver. In their view, the whole reason vehicles were invented is to get from point A to point B faster than other current available options. So outracing other drivers or captains or pilots is showing that you know how to use a vehicle the way it was intended.

Racing riggers come from many different backgrounds, and often develop different specialties. At heart, the racing rigger has a deep understanding of both vehicles and other drivers. The racing rigger has to develop a strong intuition of how others around them will act and react to their operations, because no race actually occurs in a vacuum (even those in space). The best way to develop intuition in others’ behaviors is to develop a deep techniRACINGcal and practical knowledge of tactics, procedures, and the physical workings and limitations of their vehicles and people.

The racing rigger is an athlete. First, because no racing rigger spends all of their time jumped into any vehicle, no matter how much they love it. Second, because the laws of physics and nature don’t care about ASIST and your sensory overrides. Of course, this also goes back to Rule Zero: Do not stake your life on your control rig. If you can’t operate a vehicle naked, you will eventually lose to someone who can. Racing riggers need to be aware of their physical state, and it helps to be in athletic shape. Competition racers can lose several pounds of water weight in a match because they are running mental and physical marathons inside their cockpits. Their core muscles are tensed and exhausted. They need the muscle and willpower to focus on their vehicle and every other competitor on the track with limited senses. They also have to survive crashes.

This is another imperative: The racing rigger has to know how to crash. No matter how skilled or lucky the racing rigger is, they will crash. This means they have to know how to avoid or mitigate the damage to themselves and their vehicles. This is not simply a matter of physical survival. Standard vehicle safety measures are remarkable in protecting occupants from death and serious injury in all but the most extreme scenarios. The racing rigger probably doesn’t have “standard” safety features. Even if they have standard or better, they don’t matter. The racing rigger has to have the willpower to accept the crash and to keep operating. That may mean the split-second difference from surviving or dying in an ambush, but it may also mean they can fix what’s broken and get back into the race. Some people get traumatized from car collisions for years. The racing rigger doesn’t have the luxury of lingering on the trauma. They need to process it and dismiss it. This is even harder for jumped-in riggers because they _are_ the vehicles. Crashes and combat damage are mentally the same as being in a street brawl. The jumpedin rigger knows how to take a punch and to keep punching back..
