## **The Seelie Court**

**The Seers’ Guild may be the best collection of divination knowledge in this world, but the Seelie Court may have them beat. Remember that the factions within the court are aligned with the major arcana of the Sixth World Tarot, and that tells you a lot about the court’s relationship to divination.**

**To date, members of Court have listened to Convergence’s concerns without making a decision about where they stand on the issue. They seem to want more information, and they’re at least willing to team up with Convergence agents to fnd it.**

**Naturally, a lot of this effort is focused on the Sixth World Tarot, both gathering and interpreting cards. As experts know, the meaning of each card is very much open to interpretation, and this only increases when you start to build connections between cards. On top of that, some of the cards have been known to change, adding more uncertainty to the overall picture.**

**Let’s talk for a moment about one sequence that has gotten a lot of attention from both the Seelie Court and Convergence: the cards with the ammo box. This box, notable for its fve stars, features on the Knight of Batons, the Two of Cups, the Six of Coins, the Nine of Coins. While tarot card meanings are subject to interpretation and dependent on context, in a general sense, the three numerical cards all have an aspect of help and looking out for others. The Knight of Batons, by contrast, is usually seen to be about ambition and risk-taking. One of the questions has always been where the Knight fts into the sequence told by the other cards. The generally agreed-upon sequence is the street urchins of the Six of Coins have the case to start the sequence; they then sell it or have it taken by the wealthy man from the Nine of Coins. The wealthy man loses the case to runners in the Two of Cups. But the Knight of Batons shows a man who is not part of the sequence losing the case while being beaten by a cop. Did that happen frst, allowing the urchins to fnd the case, or did it happen last and the man losing the case is an associate of the runners from the previous two cards? And then there’s the critical question that runs through this all: Why is the case important?**

**One thing we know for certain is since the tarot became more popular, cases with fve stars have become more popular in the Sixth World. Some people just like appealing to the arcane nature of the design, while others are deliberately tweaking Sixth World Tarot enthusiasts. This latter group discovers the error of their ways when their case is inevitably stolen by tarot enthusiasts who think that this time, they’ve discovered** _**the**_ **case.**

**These days, though, the common thinking among Convergence members is that the case is symbolic. They believe it is a Pandora’s box of sorts, representing the destruction that could be unleashed if it is opened. They believe the people depicted on the cards are more important than the case, and they want to determine whether they should be associated with real people or if they, too, are symbolic. Several Seelie Court members are happy to join in this analysis, so Convergence seers have become regulars in court discussions.**

**They set up in the DeeCee area for direct access to the seat of UCAS government as well as the area’s signifcant economic engine. What’s more, the density of cities on the UCAS East Coast gives them fairly easy access to Baltimore, Philadelphia, Norfolk, and New York, and they have touchpoints and offces in all of those cities.**

**The showcase, though, is the DeeCee-area building. Naturally, it has a secure dedicated host, providing all the toys the group’s hackers need to do their thing. But let’s be honest, that’s not the most diffcult thing to provide, as all you need is a bunch of cash and access to a high-end electronics store. No, the really impressive parts of the building are the world-class divining features.**
