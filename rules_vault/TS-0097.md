## **Persons of Interest**

**Any good case has criminals you are looking for, but it will also contain witnesses and victims who can provide information. Finding them is often done with legwork through hitting the streets, checking cameras, or fnding connections. People in general have behaviors that may make it easier or more diffcult to get information from them. Use the Contact Keywords (p. 95,** _**Smooth Operations**_ **) or Qualities (p. 70–79,** _**SR6**_ **) to help indicate their strengths and weaknesses.**

**Accomplices are people who have aided the primary suspect. There are times when they’re an active assistant and others where they have accidentally aided someone. Typically, you will be led to this type of person through a paper trail or being seen at the crime scene. This includes getaway drivers, inside men, false witnesses, planners, and other types where they’re not the principal criminal.**

**Another type of person of interest is someone who provides an alibi. These individuals need to be found, and then additional legwork is needed to ensure that the story lines up and there are other**

**bits of supportive evidence. Magic and Matrix manipulation makes this extra challenging for detectives and for teams to explore.**

**During an investigation, sometimes one of the core parts of building a case against the expected criminal or scapegoat is by fnding character witnesses who can speak about the principal. Are they trustworthy? Did they blow up mailboxes when they were a kid? From a planning standpoint, it’s useful to design these types of contacts based on how easy they are for the investigators to fnd and what type of relationship they have with the principal.**

**Most law enforcement service professionals have built up a set of confdential informants who have inside knowledge about criminal activities and various ins and outs of the business of vice. They act as leads. At the start of an investigation, they can provide the detectives or runners with a place to start. Second, as the investigation leads on, that pressure can lead to more information coming to light that the informant will be privy to.**

**Finding a witness who has direct knowledge of the circumstances of the crime or facts is considered essential for many cases. Even this type of person of interest needs to have their story verifed, which requires legwork. Once verifed, a witness is crucial to establishing the key elements of any investigation.**

**Suspects are the main target of any investigation. Some of those suspects will be criminals and others will be falsely accused of the crime. When designing a case, having plausible suspects provides challenges for the team to overcome. Each of those potential criminals has a different story and evidence that will exonerate them. Knowing how these connect means that scenes and story can provide means for the team of detectives or runners to fnd the clues.**

**The victim is the person who’s suffered the most harm as the result of a criminal act. It’s easy to think of them as someone who was subjected to some violent crime where the only evidence they can provide is their mortal remains. But there are other times where theft or fnancial crime has occurred, and the victim has the most information for the team. In many cases the injured party knows the aggressor.**

**The common theme with all these different types of persons of interest is that their stories and information need to be verifed. Hard evidence is much more reliable and tells its own tale. But the core milestone in a case is only confrmed once you can verify that person’s clues.**
