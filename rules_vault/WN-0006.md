## On Track

by Jason M. Hardy

Jugaad was absolutely going to read the file on the datachip in her pocket the second she wasn’t being chased. She wasn’t optimistic that would be anytime soon.

She really wished she hadn’t worn bright red today. But this was supposed to be a corporate job—meet some people, shake some hands, set the stage for things to come. Calm, sedate. Not _this._

She ran down Park Avenue. She passed a dozen scooters, knowing the minute she hopped onto one, her pursuers would either have it shut down or tracked. But it still might have been worth it to give her a break from running. She had been running for half an hour. Or an hour. Or forever. Something like that.

Her height was an advantage and disadvantage. She couldn’t get the overhead view of the crowd, couldn’t plan by looking ahead. She was in a forest of waists and legs. But she could move and slide through that forest better than her pursuers, slipping around and between, and sometimes even through the legs of passers by. The pursuers were orks in black suits. They looked intimidating and had pistols drawn, so a lot of people moved out of their way. But this was Manhattan. Some people refused to back down or change their course from anything, and the guys chasing Jugaad were not the most agile creatures on the planet, so some shoulder-bumping and other contact was inevitable. That slowed them down a touch, which let her increase the gap between them and her.

It wouldn’t be enough, though. Reinforcements would show up—maybe other guards, maybe NYPD. They might well show up in front of her, cutting her off immediately.

So she had to do more than increase the gap between her and her pursuers. She had to disappear somehow.

If only there was a crowd of hanumen wearing red for her to blend into.

She was in the lower fifties now. Getting closer to Grand Central. Which might be a bad idea. Lots of crowds to get lost in, but lots of cops. Probably lots of places where they could head her off at the pass. Seemed like a good idea to go someplace else. Go west, things get really touristy. Go east, things get ritzy.

She chose west for the reasons New Yorkers hate tourists—they’re unpredictable and mess up the city’s flow. She hoped they’d mess up everyone but her.

Down 52[nd] she went, past Madison, to Fifth. Just north of St. Patrick’s, not far away from the Empire State Building. And restaurants and stores with ridiculous prices everywhere.

She got some stares when she whipped around the corner, but that was nothing compared to what happened when the orks came after her. Screams erupted,

people ducked for cover and otherwise tried to run for shelter without really knowing where shelter would be. It was beautiful chaos. Good thing they weren’t in the Bronx, where two orks running with guns wouldn’t have made anyone blink.

A few people ran into the street, so autonav systems of nearby vehicles dutifully stopped traffic. Even xGuide can’t do much more than keep Manhattan traffic from being one step away from a major snarl, and that step had been taken. Horns honked, voices yelled, and people loudly wondered just what in the hell was going on here.

Jugaad took the opportunity to go into the street. She slid under a couple of cars, then went on top of one to get a look around, then went back down again. The orks hadn’t lost sight of her. She’d opened up the gap even more, but they still knew where she was trying to go. Their anticipation was superb, like they could see her when she was out of sight.

Like they were watching on all the cameras planted all over town.
