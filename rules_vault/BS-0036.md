## Obsolete Cyberware

**>** I found this old article from back in the day—it’s sort of related. **>** Bull

When you have a piece of cyberware installed, you are taking a risk and leaning on a lot of hope. You are hoping that the cyberdoc knows what they are doing, you are hoping that the piece of ’ware you’re getting wired into you is not defective, and you’re hoping that it hasn’t been tampered with by your many enemies. Once the implant is actually inside you and you’ve made sure it works, a lot of people stop worrying.

This is a mistake.

Anyone with ’ware knows that anything implanted needs to be maintained. Sometimes that’s as simple as cleaning your cyberarm and oiling the joints; other times it requires specialized diagnostic equipment that can only be found at very expensive facilities. The point is, when you have ’ware, it’s a part of life to go and get it serviced. This is fine when the ’ware is new and the manufacturer is selling service kits and replacement parts. Even after a year or two it’s usually okay … popular cyberware often has excess stock lying around for years, and a cyberdoc may be able to sell some gently used ’ware that other people have had replaced. But the more exotic and unique the equipment, and the older it is, the more likely that your cyberdoc just won’t be able to help you. This is where people really feel their dependence on supply chains and corporate manufacturing—if that cyberlung that you’re sporting is out of warranty and can’t be serviced, then it’s upgrade-or-remove time. Having obsolete or out-of-warranty equipment is already really bad. If that equipment is something that you rely on to do your job, or stay alive, when it’s wired into your spine, that’s a real problem!
