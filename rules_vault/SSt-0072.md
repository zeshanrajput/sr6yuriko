## **Piles of Nuyen**

**The biggest barrier to being a rigger—other than talent, skill, and experience, that is—is that everything costs a lot of nuyen. The control rig implant is expensive, but also very invasive—ideally, you should be going with higher-grade implants in order to make room for other goodies that can improve your performance as well as make you more survivable when you aren’t jacked in. Then you need a rigger command console, or RCC for short.** _**Then**_ **you need a vehicle or two, then a decent selection of drones. Even then, you’ll still need thousands of nuyen to invest in autosofts and pilot upgrades so that whatever you aren’t jumped into can be relied on to successfully complete their required tasks. If you’re starting from scratch, fgure on needing at least a couple hundred thousand nuyen on the low end—ideally closer to a half million, and even then you’ll still want for more. Being a rigger means constantly blowing through ridiculous amounts of nuyen—and it never stops, never gets to the point of “enough.” There will never, ever, be a point in time in which you have everything you need. You’ll be constantly making do with what you have, trying to expand those capabilities whenever you get the chance, and—more often that not—recouping expenses from combat losses. Street sams and adepts can heal up, mages can summon more spirits, and cyberdecks are**



**generally pretty cheap to repair. But drones—even tough combat drones—are relatively fragile, and everyone but you will consider them to be expendable. Too often they get taken offine somewhere they aren’t readily recovered, and you’re stuck with having to replace them. If you think you can negotiate a bigger cut from your team to replace lost hardware, well, good luck with that—in my experience, other runners will never respect the risks that you take, all while they take you and your drones for granted. My attempts to educate them on this point have all fallen as fat as my poor lost multitudes of drones. I’ve gotten better at managing this over the years, but it’s a lesson—no, many lessons—that you only learn with experience and at great expense.**

**I’d say the other great drawback to being a rigger is that only people with a control rig implant can relate to you. Your experience is so different from everyone else’s that they can’t possibly understand what it’s like for you—all they see are a fraction of the results that come from your labor. Unless you’re saving their bacon—which happens quite a bit—they forget about you. And once they get used to running with you, they quickly forget how much effort, risk, expense, and attention you’re putting in to provide them with the benefts they’ve come to rely on. The frst few times they’ll respond with astonished gratitude, but after that the only feedback you get from them is bitching and moaning when they don’t think you’re doing enough. It sucks, but that’s what people are like— they only appreciate you when they’re aware of what you’re doing for them, and even then only once or twice before they come to expect it as their due. That’s probably the biggest reason I prefer not to work consistently with the same team—it’s better when you switch things up so they don’t have time to get too used to you.**
