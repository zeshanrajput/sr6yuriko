## O

There were others on her trail now. Police were heading up Fifth Avenue. Her position was increasingly precarious. She really needed to get proactive on this bitch.

The police were south of her, and south was where she needed to go. So creativity was needed.

She went west. The orks, of course, followed. Ideally, so would the police.

She scanned the street in front of her. Office building. Italian restaurant. Office building. Parking garage. Automat.

Ah, automat. That might do.

She ran toward the building with its purple neon and retro aesthetic. The word “Automat” flashed outside, along with the outline of a small quad-rotor drone. Unlike its twentieth-century counterpart, the automat was now fully automatic. Drones prepared the food and placed it in the compartment where it sat on display, mouth-watering in ways soy packets could never be. For a small extra fee, you could also have a drone bring it to you, instead of having to walk over to the door and open it yourself. Either way, the scanners across the place made sure you were charged the moment your plate hit the table.

The real trick for Jugaad was getting in with enough time.

She had been in this automat before. She’d have to get all the way across the place before the orks got in for this to work. Right now, they were too close.

Luckily, there was a scooter. In half a dozen strides, Jugaad had rented and unlocked a scooter. She pulled the throttle and pushed the scooter ahead. It didn’t continue long once she let it go, but it did what it was supposed to. Two pedestrians dodged out of the way, right into the orks. The orks managed to not crash into them and down to the ground, but they were tangled for a moment. Which was all she needed.

Jugaad dashed into the automat and straight for a customer who was removing food from one of the compartments. She jumped through the door before it closed.

In the automat’s rear area, drones moved around, putting more food in place, carrying empty trays. None of them cared that Jugaad was back there. They wouldn’t, as long as she didn’t interfere with business.

She could look for a back door out from here, but if she were a betting woman, she’d put money on the fact that cops were walking up to that door right now. In fact, her plan largely depended on it.

She turned and looked through the automat doors. The orks had entered the automat. It was time to move. She drew her gun, then gave herself a running start, then leaped up to the third level of compartments. Her gunless hand and two legs hit the bottom of the compartment, and she pushed off, hard and forward. She fired a few rounds to weaken the door as she did so, then burst through it.

That was enough to startle the orks, but starling them would not be enough. She was at the level of their heads, so that’s where she aimed.

It took more than one gel round to the forehead to bring down most orks, so she gave them three apiece. It was rapid firing, faster than they could bring their weapons around. They staggered back, hit a table, and started falling.

Jugaad didn’t need to stay around long enough to watch them go down. She dashed out the door and headed south.

While she ran, she switched SINs on her commlink, moving to one that was worse quality but hadn’t been recently used to get into a prominent corporate building. She didn’t like to use it much, but it should be good enough to get her on the subway.

She looked back. Pursuit had re-assembled, but at least it was all behind her.



After a few blocks, she descended into the subway. What had once been the 7 train had finally been dug out and rebranded as the Midtown Shuttle, moving tourists back and forth under 42[nd] Street. It gave them a little more dirt and grime than the El did, and they loved it for that.

There was no train at the station at the moment, and she wasn’t about to stand around waiting for one. So she ran to then end of the platform, jumped down onto the tracks, and headed east. She would have worried about an MTA worker hassling her, but that would have required them to care, so she was pretty sure she was fine.

Except for the fact that two orks in black suits had just emerged onto the subway platform. They looked a little dazed, but they were still moving.

Drek. The constitution of orks was a wonder—an annoying, boneheaded wonder. The good news was that she had just left the range of the cameras that had been bedeviling her entire journey. The bad news was that if the orks followed her much farther, she’d be revealing a secret that some people close to her very much didn’t want revealed.

It was dark in the tunnels, of course, but she once again cursed the fact that she was wearing bright red. If she had a black outfit, this would be a piece of cake. Now, even in the dim light, she would be at least a little visible.

She listened for the rumble of a train. She wasn’t sure what she would do if one were coming, but it would at least add some unpredictability to the situation. But luck wasn’t willing to grant her that.

Lacking any other idea, she ran forward. A look over her shoulder told her the orks were following.

She looked back ahead. Her advantages here were speed and size. She just needed the right spot.

She was quick, but the orks were clearly well conditioned. She was gasping for air, and they seemed like they never slowed down. Even though they must have nice new bruises rising on their heads. She really couldn’t let them catch up to her—how embarrassing would it be to be outrun by an ork?

There. Right side. Opportunity. Not as small as she would like, but workable. Bright red cloth flashed into the gap. After a few seconds, the orks approached the opening in the tunnel wall and slowed. They moved carefully, like they didn’t want to be ambushed. They poked their heads into the gap, guns ready. Then they went through the gap.

In the tunnel they had moved out of, dozens of meters away, Jugaad moved from a walk to a jog to a sprint. She knew she didn’t have long before they found the pants she had thrown into the gap. She had to make use of the time she had.

Then there was another gap to her left. She went

through it. They wouldn’t have eyes on her. Or cameras. She didn’t let herself believe it for a few minutes, but it seemed she had finally lost them.


## O

There is always a way out of a tunnel. As heartless as they can be, neither governments nor corporations are anxious to have people burn up or suffocate in case of a train fire. After all, some of their people take the subway, too. So there are tunnels out that also serve as ventilation shafts. When it’s an isolated subway lines, the tunnels are simple affairs. When line after line after line intersects or comes close to each other, so do the tunnels. Which makes something like the ground under Grand Central a complex warren

The point is, Jugaad could make it from the 7 subway tunnel to the tangle of semi-blockaded tracks without entering any public place or being caught on any camera, which was good both because she didn’t want to be seen and because she wasn’t wearing any pants.

She wound her way through the used tracks and then crossed to the unused ones. Stories say that track 61 connected to the Waldorf-Astoria. Wealthy guests at the hotel (and did that place have any other kind of guests?) could go from the hotel directly to their own train line, thereby avoiding contact with the unwashed masses. With transit becoming more mass, though, that sort of specialized service was discontinued. Track 61 has not been used since the twentieth century, but as luck would have it, it hadn’t collapsed in the Quake, and it held a subway car as obsolete as the track itself, which just sat there, collecting dust.

The car was an old R15, a simple affair with bars to grab near the ceiling and long benches running parallel to the train walls. The benches had been given extra padding but no extra width, so they served as a set of isolated beds for the weary who needed rest. A Faraday cage had been built into the walls of the car. It was an isolated safehouse under the heart of the busiest city on the continent. Though Jugaad found that she didn’t get much sleep when others were there—everyone always thought they could go ahead and pet the monkey just because she was lying there. If she rested on the train, it would be with one eye open.

But she didn’t come here to rest. She had only two important tasks to handle: open the file and figure out how to get pants. And the first task was far more important in her eyes.
