## **>** Bull

It used to be that most animals—including the paranormal kind—had a strong negative reaction to almost any cybernetic or bioware augmentations. For the first couple of decades it washeavily researched, and implanting critters almost always resulted in extreme aggression or self-destructive behavior. Over the years, the designs improved and had a less severe effect on the minds and instincts of the augmented critters. Instead of turning them destructively psychotic, it just made them more difficult to train and work with. Heavily cybered critters have generally been restricted to the purpose of creating barely controlled guard monsters—difficult and unpredictable but not randomly aggressive.

A recent breakthrough by Evo has vastly improved the interface between cybernetics and animals. I understand that the basis for this tech comes from the development of cyberjacks, which allow deckers to use their own grey matter to boost their processing capabilities. These new implants are nothing like cyberjacks in function but use the advances in brain-to-technology interfacing to better integrate the functions of augmentations into something that’s more instinctively accepted and controllable. The result is that animals can be implanted with a much greater range of possible augmentations and make better use of them without becoming aggressive, self-destructive, or even suffering much discomfort. These Evo critterjacks make it much easier for an animal to adapt to their new capabilities while also making them more susceptible to being controlled by cybertech. You can expect to see many more augmented critters in use as a component of security systems, but other applications have begun to hit the consumer market. So far, there’s very little legislation to rein this practice in, and I doubt there will be any significant government interference. As much as I hate it, this tech is going to become more widely used—not less.

Even more detestable—and far less likely to show up on the consumer market—are the advances in bio-drone technology. The newest cyber-harnesses are less invasive and more effective than ever before, largely thanks to Evo’s critterjack technology. A cyber-harness effectively turns a critter into a drone that can be directly controlled via specialized autosofts, or even jumped into by a rigger. The actual critter brain is suppressed in a simsense experience while their bodies are hijacked, completely unaware of their actual surroundings and activity. Even paracritters can be dominated in this way, though any purely magical senses and powers are as yet undetectable, or perhaps untranslatable, by the cyber-harness. You can’t use a barghest’s paralyzing howl using a cyber-harness, nor can you see what’s going on in astral space, even though barghests are dual natured—they’re still vulnerable to astral threats. In most cases, paracritters aren’t considered ideal candidates for significant augmentations or cyber-harnesses. There are two reasons for this: Augmentations have a negative effect on a critter’s magical capabilities,

and cyber-harnesses are no good for directly controlling and activating their Awakened powers.

As far as I know (and I’ve been watching), no one has as yet developed cyber-harnesses that are designed to turn metahumans into bio-drones. I’d be very surprised if there weren’t any secret projects out there working toward that, but if so they are being exceedingly careful to not let any word get out. Sure, it would be nice to have a workforce entirely controlled by a rigger-manager, but the negative PR probably isn’t worth it. My experiences with metahumanity (and our general lack of a moral compass) makes me believe that it’s only a matter of time before somebody does it, though.

Cybernetic augmentations of critters are most often used on mundane animals. They’re cheaper and easier to work with than paracritters, and experimental results are much more reliably repeatable. There’s a whole lot of crazy out there, so don’t be too shocked if you run into augmented cockatrices that track you by your Matrix signals or barghests that can spot you via ultrasound.

So what augmentations are adaptable for installation on animals and other critters? As far as bodyware goes, almost anything. Passive augmentations like bone lacing and dermal plating are especially common for security critters. Anything that directly interfaces with the brain or nervous system requires a critterjack system to coordinate properly with the critter’s natural mind and body. This includes muscle replacement, wired reflexes, cyberlimbs, and such. Sensory augmentations and other headware also need to be tapped into a critterjack to be truly effective, which makes the critter able to use and understand information from its improved senses just as easily as their natural senses.

The only cybertech that is generally considered incompatible are control rigs, cyberjacks, cyberdecks, and commlinks—critters just don’t seem to be able to make sense of them, so they’re just a waste of resources. I’d bet that someone out there is working on making a dolphin that can hack the Matrix, but I’ve yet to find out about it. Most cosmetic augmentations are also incompatible with critters, though certain customized variants might exist as prototypes.

If a particular augmentation has a different equivalent for critters, the metahuman version is not available—only the critter version applies.

As for bioware, well, that’s all on the table. Some special treatments and training may be required to allow a critter to accept bioware as its own body, but the only serious barrier is the expense—and megacorps have plenty of nuyen to work with. Essence loss from bioware and genetech can still take a toll on a critter, making them more difficult to train and work with, but Evo’s critterjacks are capable of effectively calibrating a critter’s brain to adapt to even these augmentations.
