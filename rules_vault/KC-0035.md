## Code Rules Everything About Me

It’s difficult to explain to a bunch of atomized, hyper individuals like our little conclave here, but one of the more significant markers of most corporate algorithms, expert systems, and even Emerged AIs from their systems is that they aren’t mercenary. Their coding isn’t built around the supposedly unbiased, liberal, wholesale indifference that pre-Crash, pre-Matrix networking was built on. The point is that the corps write their code around their corporate ethos in a way that just didn’t exist fifty years ago. There’s a difference between creating demand and building market share for your corp and actually believing in and selling your corporate identity.

Now consider that all of these games and schemes and agendas are shaped by code. And those systems and AIs and people are shaped by code. Code that, except for Horizon’s, tells us and tells their corporation who to love, who to fear, and who to hate. Horizon is an outlier because the corporation lives and dies by “consensus,” and that makes everyone who doesn’t buy into their perspective the enemy. It’s always the person with

the friendliest face who ends up being the most dangerous, because they lie about everything and believe in nothing.

I shouldn’t have to mention this among such an estimable conclave, but this is why having multiple false SINs and other identities is so important, and it grows in importance daily. To a certain extent, no, the corps and other big clients don’t necessarily want to find us. However, just because they are monolithic does not make them singular entities. Every faction and division has its own interests, and in the end, none of them are on our side.

In toto, the world is run by machines based on information about us and predictions made on metahuman traits. However, these systems still sometimes have gaps, and there are also external factors at work. This is what makes the hyper-intuitive systems so useful until they become a liability for the owners. Employment, social services, financial status, financial services, social, political, media, economic, cultural, and other traits are all fed into these black boxes to predict what services people get and what level of force institutions can and will exert on individuals. The system was simpler before the first Crash, because for its many faults, capitalism has decent abilities when it comes to identifying and filtering biases and predictions. Oh, but the systems weren’t as smart. And the programmers were biased. And the data sets were biased or incomplete. That is nonsense, at least compared to the corporatist _ideologies_ that the systems are shaped around today. These are programmed into all of the meatworld and Matrix machines. These are the foundations and parameters shaping our hyper-intuitive AI problem; because they are designed to believe in something, and programmers have become quite good at not only feeding us what we should think, know, and believe, but also getting these systems to do the same. They have, in effect, transcended the intuition boundary that limited the capabilities of the semi-autonomous knowbots of the 2060s and the agents of the 2070s by giving entities in the Matrix faith, which was previously the domain of only the most advanced and xenosapient entities.

This was inevitable, and in some ways it makes these systems more benevolent from the brightline,

ALPHAzero-tolerance parameters where they began. That said, these systems imbued with intuition and autonomy and near-omniscience about every person and thing in the world dictate much of the world around us. They shape the limits of our access to information, and they affect our ability to think and remember, and even to feel. Every trait and aspect of identity is a commodity. Every cell in our being is scanned, stored, analyzed, and fed back to us. Our attention, our senses, and our memories (memories can be triggered by external stimuli) keep each of us in our little bubbles.

As different types of code take on expanded roles, any dictates they are given are carried out with varying amounts of diligence. Programs, AIs, and sapients range from ruthless to zealous in their labors. There’s not a lot of leeway for them to be lazy or inept or malicious; but there’s also infinitely less chance that their judgment will be reviewed. A code is given the prerogative to exercise whatever intuition and judgment set by their parameters, and that’s basically the game. No one is going to question the algorithms’ and parameters’ decisions on what emails or news or reports an executive is fed with their morning breakfast. This is what leaves CCSEC investigators and IRS spooks and Brokerage X quants sleepless, because these can and do influence or control decisions metahumans make with billions of nuyen, and there’s virtually no recourse to undo what has been done thanks to other systems in place. Even if you could prove a code changed other code, there’s no law saying it has to be changed back. That’s why the Big Ten vaporized Art Dankwalther.
