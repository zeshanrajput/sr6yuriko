## Facial Recognition

By now, we should all know that every runner needs at least _some_ social skills to deal with situations that are inevitably going to come up. For example, knowing when or even _how_ to lie—yes, there’s an art to it. Or, and I can’t believe I have to say this, knowing when to be polite and professional when the situation calls for it.

Even if your role on a team is say, combat or hacking, a small amount of etiquette can be the difference between things going sideways or sliding by without a problem. And when you have someone who’s an expert in the art of dealing with people, you’ll see how social skills can change a situation. Now, I’ll cover the basics of being a team’s face here, but High Road will later explore the concept of being the mastermind behind a plan and using the whole team’s skills to execute runs.



So, with all that out of the way, what exactly does a face do?

The most common answer you’ll get is negotiating with Mr. Johnson, typically for a higher payout. But not always. Negotiating for extra weapons or support can dramatically change a run’s dynamic. Alternative types of compensation are sometimes easier to get out of Mr. Johnson, and a skilled negotiator will help you extract every bit of compensation you can get.

But I digress.

**>** Sometimes you want to leave things on the table if you’re trying to create a relationship. Raking someone over the coals through multiple runs is a good way of making them feel used. Getting them to recognize that you’re not trying to extract every bit of compensation versus just appearing incompetent is the trick.

**>** High Road

**>** Even if you drive a hard bargain, there’s always cred left on the table. And if you’re working with corporate Johnsons, there’s definitely going to be some left on the table. I’ve known teams who’ve taken the initial offer but later sold off some paydata acquired during the run. They’re hunted now, but the brief payday was nice. Anyway, don’t expect Mr. or Ms. Johnson to provide **all** the rewards from a run is all I’m saying.

**>** Mr. Bonds

**>** Or don’t be a stooge for the corps. Local communities and neighborhoods have real needs. And when you grab every nuyen or any asset you can, that’s less for those who deserve it. Yeah, you’re putting your life on the line, but don’t forget about the value of favors owed. You may need help someday, and those communities and neighbors will be the one to pull your hoop out of a fire.

**>** Old Crow

The other answer I often hear is that faces are there to get into the good graces of whoever is vital to the run. That could mean chumming up to gang members by offering to grab them something they need or want, or befriending some bored and underpaid security guard at the site you’re about to hit. Setting up such relationships and contacts is also a part of the job, which also requires knowing when to use subterfuge, a touch of etiquette, or straight-up bribery.

Those two answers tend to be the most common (and overly simplistic) I hear. But like I said before, that’s just the tip of the iceberg in how someone with a natural aptitude for working with people gets the job done.

The key in that regard is about the things you know and how you use that knowledge. For example, I’ve worked with Buckshot, a gregarious exvet whose sheer presence and ability to lead were able to turn the tide of a firefight on more than one occasion. We can also look at Diva, a former child actor whose skills in acting and cosmetics, along with extensive knowledge of the trid industry, made her vital to the success of several runs around LA.

- I’ve also worked with both Diva and Buckshot. What’s interesting is they’re able to accomplish the main parts of their job, like negotiation, smoothing things over, and lying through their teeth, but in such different ways. Diva is like silk; smooth and pleasant. Buckshot exudes the feeling that he’s telling you exactly how it is and doesn’t have time to frag around.

- Kat o’ Nine Tales
