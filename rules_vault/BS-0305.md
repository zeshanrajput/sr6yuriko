## History of Genetech

Metahumanity has been practicing genetic manipulation since before it was metahumanity. Ancient humankind developed breeding programs for

plants and animals to promote traits they valued while breeding out the undesirable. This was very successful, but it has the distinct problem of being very slow. A little over a century ago, humanity started trying to speed the process up with active genetic manipulation. These rapid changes led to both increased crop yields and early treatments for genetic diseases such as cystic fibrosis and Down syndrome.

What started as horticulture and healthcare quickly became a way to make a profit. People with money saw genetech as an opportunity to live longer, have better eyesight, retain their hair, build muscles easier, and add a host of other improvements that the lucky among us are born with. Research nuyen is poured into programs that promise designer babies, the elimination of colon cancer, easier weight loss and muscle growth, and more. This only accelerated with Unexplained Genetic Expressions (UGE) and the Awakening. The rich looked for ways to correct their babies that were born with pointy ears or short limbs. They also looked for ways to control who could have magic.

The basics of modern genetics are built around manipulating our DNA and the base pair building blocks that define who we are. Decades of research in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries led to discovering ways to read our basic code. Scientists began to unravel which of the three billion base pairs in a metahuman do specific things. The surveying of the entire genome is incomplete even now, but that hasn’t stopped researchers from trying to figure out what makes us trolls, humans, Awakened, or Emerged. Once scientists gained a rough idea as to which parts of the genome did specific things, they began to tinker with DNA. Early techniques used molecular markers and polymerase chain reactions to build strings of new genetic material and retroviruses to insert the material into cells. With the introduction of nanotechnology, scientists had greater success in both extracting DNA from cells without damaging it and assembling genes in the desired order.

Research along this path was slow, and became a game of guess-and-check as to whether the change wasn’t going to create some other horrible disease. Remember that I said there were three billion pairs? That really made it tough to figure out how any single change impacted a person. Couple this with the fact that early techniques were imprecise and slow to change. Nothing like going in for revitalization and coming out color-blind, and that’s just a minor inconvenience compared to all the other nasty things that could happen.

The discovery of HMHVV changed the field, as the ability for the disease to radically change the genetic structure of an Infected individual in such a short order tantalized the science community. So while previous drops have talked about genetic alteration being used for only a few select treatGENEWAREment options like revitalization or leónization, it was actually way more pervasive than that. The problem with using HMHVV as your transmission medium is that folks become paranoid about using gene therapy. Even with great branding, rumors still leak out.

The industry addressed those fears and started relying heavily on nanites to handle tasks such as splitting, compiling, and integrating DNA changes back into cells. Shortly before CFD hit, nanotech had become the core part of creating geneware and gene therapy, so much so that even the CFD scare didn’t really stop its use. During the time when much was unknown about CFD, corporate marketing personnel just worked hard to obscure any relationship between gene-, bio-, and cyberware to nanotechnology. Currently, nanites are still used extensively for geneware construction, but as with other types of nanotech, it’s done using simple, hard nanites with fixed programming.
