## **Special Enforcement**

**If a force includes special enforcement units, they will usually be operating in a slightly grey area, and focus on known or suspected criminals. With the intended targets being less than upstanding citizens, special enforcement commanders are willing to look the other way for minor issues like warrants or a suspect’s civil rights, just as long as the team is producing results. These units can be thought of as either beefed-up patrol units or watered-down tactical units. The standard team**

**Patrol is always stretched too thin. I don’t care what company or agency you’re talking about, it’s always the same. You’ve got people out on disability, you’ve got sick calls, the whole shift is running to one high-risk call when another comes in and now you’ve got to dispatch a skeleton crew out to the new one. It’s a mess. That’s why you see patrol sergeants go bald so quick. It’s a sad truth, but patrol in this day and age is an entirely reactive entity, not a proactive one. Beat cops can barely get lunch down before someone zeroes their dry cleaner; you expect them to go to hot zones and weed people out? In comes the Enhanced Enforcement Squad, or Crime Suppression Unit, Street Crimes Unit, maybe a Gun Task Force, the unit could be named anything really. But its purpose is what’s important, and that purpose is to be an outand-out proactive entity.**

**Lone Star makes extensive use of these outfts, typically referring to them as the Street Crimes Unit. Plainclothes Stars, four to a cruiser, are tasked with patrolling specifc areas and dismantling crime that is trending up. Robberies are on the rise? Better arrest everything that moves in the neighborhood after a certain hour. Specifc gang causing trouble in a habitat block? Kick in every door and put bracelets on anyone with apparel that might match the reported gang colors. You get the idea. Stars in an SCU are typically referred to as “bail-out boys” by the locals, as they are known to bail out of a vehicle in pursuit of their quarry.**

**Knight Errant has made use of Crime Suppression or Gang Suppression units as needed. Suppression being the operative word for these KE teams. These units are much less subtle than Lone Star’s SCU, if you can believe it. When Knights are put into a “suppression-anything,” that unit almost always ends up as a glorifed kill team. Load up a Citymaster, give them a special unit name, and point them in the general direction of the troublemakers. Problem solved.**

**Neo-PD makes use of units like these as well. They tend to mesh well with the plainclothes shtick and the street-level investigations that these units typically specialize in. Neo-PD’s Crime Abatement Unit is far less aggressive than comparable units from other security forces. The CAU is typically assigned to protect high-revenue areas for cities that Neo-PD contracts with, in an efort to stem the tide of robberies, prostitution, and other unsightly inconveniences that a municipality might want gone.**

**—James Takeda, Professor of Sociology at Texas AM&M University and former LSSS Deputy Chief**

**is usually organized in a squad of six to twelve offcers deployed in up-armored Bulldog vans, or if they’re lucky, a Roadmaster. These teams are all issued full body armor with either stun batons and riot shields or assault rifes, and at frst glance can be easy to mistake for TacDiv offcers.**

**Special enforcement is typically the division to employ freelance outside contractors. Deniable assets that can be kept off the books and at arm’s length from the force, but nonetheless are essential to getting certain tasks accomplished.**

**Enhanced Enforcement Squads offcially exist to provide additional patrols in areas with increased criminal activity. In practice they will be assigned to cover any area with a suspected criminal element that command wants to root out. They stop everyone who looks out of place on some minor pretext, check their SIN, and do a physical search. If anything shows up, it’s a quick trip to a holding cell. These patrols can be a real problem for runners who don’t bother to blend into the local neighborhood. They can expect a “stop and frisk” by any enhanced enforcement team that spots them.**

**Anti-Gang Units are even less subtle than enhanced enforcement squads. These units roll out with the mission of stopping and arresting any gang members they identify. Of course, a lot of combat results from suspects “refusing a lawful order” or “resisting arrest.” Anti-gang units are notorious for both property damage and collateral civilian damage, and as a result they are often relegated to operating in the poorest neighborhoods. Both because that’s where the targeted gangs are most likely to be found and because any damage caused by overzealousness can be brushed under the rug.**

**Irregular Assets cover a wide range of subcontractors not offcially on a force’s payroll. These deniable assets are even less constrained by the rules than the normal special enforcement units**

**An “irregular asset,” huh? What a colorful way to describe that. It’s got a nice ring to it, like I’m some trenchcoated mug hired to help the detectives with a case. Wouldn’t that be nice?**

**I’ll be brief. An “irregular asset” is a hired gun, pure and simple. Whether it’s Scotland Yard, the Seattle Metroplex, or Neo-Tokyo, everyone needs wetwork done. Organizations need plausible deniability, and that is where an irregular asset comes in.**

**I’ve been hired by both law enforcement and security frms to do all manner of unsavory things. Distribute arms to gangs in order to fuel a proxy war, silence a journalist who’s looking too closely into something that they shouldn’t, I’ve even been contracted to zero badges who weren’t toeing the line well enough in their colleagues’ eyes.**

**Enforcement for the enforcers, that’s the job.**
