## Courier

The Sixth World entered the information age decades ago. Everywhere you go, an amount of information equal to the entire compiled knowledge of the human race previous to 1600 is shooting past your face at incredibly high speeds. Bars, workplaces, even relationships are all virtual now. So who needs to move physical objects from one place to another these days?

More people than you’d think. You can’t ship magical reagents over the Matrix. Planes, ships, cars, and motorcycles all come into use for that, depending on the volume. Delivery food remains in high demand—sometimes that’s completed meals, sometimes that’s select ingredients. There also is a good market for gems and jewelry being moved across various sprawls.

Then there’s the information people don’t want to put on the Matrix. Plans for prototype devices. Plans for illicit activity. Plans you do not want any hacker to accidentally wander into,

ever. Sometimes these are recorded on non-wireless storage devices; sometimes they’re actually on paper—paper that will be burned or eaten after they’re read.

All of this needs a courier. Wait, you say, hold up—what you mean is, all of this needs a _drone_ . Why put a person on the job—a person who has demands such as food, shelter, and payment— when you can have a machine do it? Let us count the reasons:

1. **Couriers aren’t hackable (though they are bribable).**

1. **Drones might be as good as couriers in responding to traffic, but couriers can read a situation better. Is that DocWagon unit blocking the street legit or staged to interfere with your job? A skilled courier will know.**

1. **People have slightly more compunction about shooting down a courier than shooting down a drone.**

1. **Couriers give better information about what they saw on the way, in case you need to know.**

1. **Couriers aren’t hackable. I know I said that already, but it’s really important.**

Drones, of course, have the reputation of being faster, often being able to fly from point A to point B without interference, while living couriers often have to use the streets. This makes them dedicated to speed and incredibly creative about how they find a route. Bicycle couriers in some sprawls exhibit the wheeled equivalent to parkour, doing things like running their bikes (or riding them) through the lobby of a building if it provides a shortcut, hitching a ride on the back of delivery trucks, hopping over curbs to cut a sharp corner, and more. GridGuide, needless to say, has no power over them. They choose their own route and speed, forever and always. If they can’t beat a car across town, they quit.

While a rigger courier might not let drones carry their package, they can still use them. They provide eyes in the sky, armed interference, and other functions. They also finely tune whatever vehicle they use, making sure it won’t fall over when they need to pay attention to other things, like someone leveling automatic weapons fire at them.

A standout courier is an obvious candidate for the shadows. In fact, their slide to the shadows is often easy—they carry some piece of illicit cargo and make friends of the people they work with and enemies of law enforcement and rival factions. Next thing they know they’re doing jobs for their friends and fighting their foes, and boom, they’re shadowrunners. And they’re still the fastest thing in the sprawl.
