## The Motives of the Black Lodge

After that header and what I said about trying to be more direct, what I’m going to say next will seem like a dodge, but I hope you’ll bear with me. The full motives of the Black Lodge can never be known. More than anything, the Black Lodge exists to advance the goals of its leaders, and those goals are idiosyncratic and shifting. Power serves as both an end and a means for the Lodge. Power is nice to have, as it secures one’s position and keeps people from ordering you around, and it also helps advance any other goals you have. Many of the positions of power the Black Lodge has seized are in anticipation of needing power to advance a goal, rather than having a specific goal they are trying to advance. Once you start playing the game of power, you have to play by power’s rules, including the most important one: Anyone who has nearly as much or more power than you is a possible threat.

- This is one of the most significant errors people make when they talk about the wealthy elite of the world. They talk about how long it would take for them to spend the money they have and how much money counts as “enough.” That’s entirely different from how the wealthy think about money. Sure, buying things is nice, and they do it all the time, but the money is more about power and self-esteem than it is about buying stuff. If someone else has more money than you do, they are a possible threat to your power and also a constant reminder that no matter how much you might have accomplished, you could have done more. You don’t accumulate more wealth because you need to buy more. You accumulate more wealth to win.

- Sunshine

Since the Black Lodge is interested in power, they are also interested in threats to the power they have accumulated. They usually classify threats in three categories: threats they can co-opt, threats they can deal with, and the worst category—implacable threats.

The first category includes areas where you can grow your power—offices people can be elected to, corporations that can be taken over, that sort of thing. It also includes people and corporations that can fall under your sphere of influence through the usual means. Not everyone or everything can be directly (or indirectly) controlled, though, so you have the second group, where negotiation and bargaining carries the day. This is a critical part of the job of Black Lodge-aligned individuals who are in politics. If you have, say, a defense secretary who is aligned with the Black Lodge, you have direct influence on how the military functions. But if your secretary is good, they should also be able to influence things like foreign policy and infrastructure spending just by being in the right conversations. Additionally, they have a large budget that can be

a boon to many companies, so doling it out carefully can expand the sphere of influence.

Then there are the implacable threats. These are threats you cannot co-opt, cannot influence, cannot do anything about other than resist and fight. It has not escaped the Black Lodge’s notice over the years that multiple threats of this nature are metaplanar in origin, bug spirits and shedim being the two most notable. This has led the Black Lodge to having something closer to a mission statement than they’ve ever had, and it’s a kind of metaplanar isolationism. Whoever controls this plane should be from here. Beings from other planes can visit, but they should not have undue influence.

The big question here is how you define “undue influence,” and that’s where the idiosyncrasies of the upper levels of the Black Lodge come into play—they define it however they want. What unites them all—and you have to believe me when I say it is unusual for the members of the inner circle to be on the same page—is that Dis is a threat and needs to be stopped.

So far, so good. I think we all agree that Dis is a threat and should be stopped, and the only groups and organizations who believe otherwise are some combination of deeply compromised and downright evil. But then you see the Black Lodge ferociously going beyond their normal measures, which are brutal enough, and you ask yourself what so many soldiers have asked in wartime—if we must fight like this, what are we winning?

I promised I would be specific, and not try to hide. So let me talk about the situation I know best.

True to their methods, the Black Lodge is using people in the groups where they can grow power to confront the people they need to fight. This means trying to sway groups on the fence to the anti-Dis side.

As you may know, the Black Lodge had gained a considerable presence in the UCAS government but suffered setbacks a few years ago as several representatives and officials with Lodge ties were exposed and removed from power. Additionally, subsequent events left the UCAS significantly less powerful than it had been, as it lost Ares and a lot of territory.

Is the government still worth influencing? I believe so. The government budget is hundreds of billions of nuyen, the military remains a force, and there are still important cities within its borders, like New York and Philadelphia. And the Lodge still has one important asset: Richard Franklin, who remains chair of the House Intelligence Committee, making him a great source of influence and information.

Franklin is one of the best. Assertive, calm, and great at covering his tracks. But the longer you do this, the greater the risk, simply because there are ongoing opportunities for exposure.

And the Disians found one. We received word that a junior member of the committee was going

RESISTANCE, ATto reveal that Franklin had Black Lodge ties at a public hearing. We couldn’t allow that. We didn’t want to lose any sources of intelligence, so Franklin had to stay where he was.

She was found dead in her apartment the next morning. Overdose was the verdict. Her conscience was still bothering her, people surmised.

Was that it? Was that bad enough? Not quite. The coroner gave a verdict of overdose without a full examination, because the body quietly slipped away from the morgue. She lies in a basement somewhere—I swear I don’t know where. In a metaplanar war, the Lodge felt her body was too valuable to waste. So, they took it and gave it to a spirit they thought could make use of it.

She was truly dead when they took her body.
