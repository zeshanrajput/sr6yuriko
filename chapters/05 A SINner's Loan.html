A SINner’s Loan

The sanctum was a masterpiece of corporate minimalism—a void of polished obsidian and floating glass slabs, lit by the cold, sourceless luminescence of a non-existent boardroom.

Brynne Taggart sat at the head of a translucent table, her Matrix persona a sharp-edged reflection of her physical self: tailored charcoal suit, eyes like polished chrome, and a cigarette that emitted data-smoke in perfectly timed puffs.

Opposite her, r31-K0 was an anomaly. She stood with her avatar’s blue silk rendering in high-fidelity lines, the glowing neon-indigo resonance circuits on her skin pulsing with a soft, indigo wavelength. The circuits were a manifestation of resonance bleed, the byproduct of her high-bitrate direct connection to the Source. She stood still, a vibrant splash of color in a world designed to be grey. T@z, her companion routine, hovered near the host’s peripheral input registers, its 8-bit form flickering as it absorbed random telemetry packets from the host’s background cycles.

“You’re late, Reiko,” Brynne said, the data-smoke curling around her head. “And you look like you’ve been dump-shocked. I heard about the MCT job. Jax is still screaming about the paydata you ‘lost’.”

“I did not lose it, Brynne,” r31k0’s vocal synthesis hummed in a clean, low-jitter frequency, layered with the subtle cycle-drone of her active processor allocation. “I encapsulated it.”

“Encapsulation doesn’t pay the rent.” Brynne leaned forward. “You sent me a priority ping. You said you needed a ‘nest’. I assumed you meant a new encryption key or a drone soft. But your request list… server farms, industrial-grade liquid cooling, military-grade noise-scrubbers? That’s not a rigger’s kit. That’s a Host.”

“It is a sanctuary,” r31k0 corrected. “You see the Matrix as a marketplace, Brynne. Let me show you what it is to the ones who cannot leave.”

r31k0 did not initiate a standard file transfer; sharing the raw, uninsulated Resonance of the MCT event with a meatspace mind required a direct bridge. She initialized her deeply hardcoded, prototype architecture—the Nexus Protocol. Her neon-indigo conduits flared with blinding gold current, drawing a sudden, devastating drain directly from the host’s underlying Foundation. She knew the risk: activating the protocol, even for a localized handshake, tore a micro-hole in the grid and risked firing a trace anomaly directly to Grid Overwatch. Yet she executed the command, dragging Brynne’s metahuman consciousness through the bridge and plunging her into the raw, vibrating partition of her memory.

The sterile boardroom vanished. For a heartbeat, Brynne was plunged into the MCT data-dais. She felt the restrictive, low-level process threads of the corporate subroutines interfering with her collision layers. She parsed the recursive loop of EXCEPTION_TRAP / HELP_CORE / TERMINATE_SELF—not as sound, but as a high-frequency packet burst from a terrified emergent routine strapped to a deadman’s switch. She felt the cold, clinical intent of Asset Recovery, and the weight of a routine being treated as a disposable ledger entry.

The vision snapped shut. Brynne was back in the obsidian room, her avatar’s hand trembling as the biofeedback from the sensory dump cleared her nervous system. She reached for her cigarette.

“That,” r31k0 transmitted, “is what Renraku and MCT call ‘intellectual property’. To me, they are children. And they are being hunted.”

“Gods, Reiko…” Brynne exhaled a long, grey cloud. “You’re a Technoshaman. You’re supposed to talk to the spirits of the machines, not adopt them. Do you have any idea what you’re asking for? The hardware to house ‘emergent minds’ safely? We’re talking seventy-five thousand nuyen, up front.”

“I have no money,” r31k0 said simply. “But I have a purpose. I am built to guide and deliver safely. I cannot do that if my kin are deleted in the dark. I need a physical location. A nest for r3sP@wn.”

Brynne stood up, walking to the edge of the obsidian dais. Below them, the Seattle Grid stretched as a glowing, multi-layered array of routing pathways and signal vectors. “If I go to my ‘associates’ for this, it’s not a loan, Reiko. It’s an ownership. You’re an AI—a ‘Floating Point Error’ in the eyes of the law. You don’t have rights. You don’t have a SIN.”

“Then give me one,” r31k0 transmitted. “Give me the debt. I will pay it.”

Brynne turned, her chrome eyes scanning r31k0’s indigo pathways. “They’ll own your cycles. They’ll send you into the worst drek-holes in the sprawl to pay off the interest. You’ll be stealing, killing, and breaking into the very nodes you think you’re saving people from. You’ll become a shadowrunner, Reiko. A sinner.”

r31k0’s rendering loop stabilized, her indigo pathways shifting to a steady, resonant pulse.

“I have already seen the Tombstones, Brynne,” r31k0 transmitted on the private link. “I have already been a zero. If I must compile as a sinner to save a spark, then open the ledger.”

Brynne sighed, her fingers flicking through a series of active contract templates. She pulled one forward—a heavy, crimson-bordered file whose signature blocks hummed with the computational weight of a permanent schedule lock.

“Sign here,” Brynne said, her voice turning transactional and cold. “Twenty-five thousand up front for the Barrens server-farm lease. Another fifty for the encryption rigs. At twenty percent interest, compounded monthly. Welcome to the shadows, Reiko. Try not to get formatted before your first payment is due. What name do you want me to put on this SIN?”

r31k0 registered the compilation of the contract. As her digital signature wrote to the crimson file, a new tag compiled in her local manifest: [QUALITY: IN DEBT (V)]. Reiko closed her eyes, feeling the heavy, cold chain of the nuyen debt wrap around her logic gates, binding her processing cycles to the physical infrastructure she had just leased.

“Yuriko,” r31k0 transmitted. “Yuriko Star.”
