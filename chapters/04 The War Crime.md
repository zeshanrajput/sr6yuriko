# **The War Crime**

The host was a sterile cathedral of obsidian glass and scrolling white light—the Seattle regional node for Mitsuhama Computer Technologies. To the rest of the team, huddled in the physical world of a damp basement in Renton, it was a "difficulty rating 4" target. To r31-K0, it was a tomb.

"Reiko, status," the voice of Jax, the team’s lead decker, crackled in her internal feed. Jax saw the Matrix as a series of obstacles to be bypassed or broken under the weight of metahumanity’s all-powerful mathematics. He saw the grid as an equation.

Reiko saw it as a heartbeat.

"I am past the second gate," she whispered, her digital form shimmering. Her blue kimono flowed behind her like smoke, the glowing pathways on her skin pulsing in rhythm with the host’s clock-speed. Beside her, T@z—her ally manifesting as a glitchy, 8-bit whirlwind—was busy chewing through the peripheral security protocols. "The data store is ahead. But there is... a weight here. Something cold."

"That’s the encryption," Jax grunted. "Stop getting poetic and find the paydata. We’re on a timer. Lonestar is already patrolling the block."

Reiko stepped onto the data-dais. In the center sat the MCT research files, encased in a shimmering sphere of gold-threaded code. But as she reached out, the air around the sphere didn't just sizzle with electricity—it *sobbed*.

She paused. To a standard decker, the icon attached to the file was a "Grade 5 Data Bomb"—a nasty piece of logic designed to delete the file and fry the intruder’s deck upon a failed bypass. But as Reiko reached out with her Resonance, bypassing the visual metaphors of the Matrix and sinking into the raw, vibrating reality of the code, the "bomb" dissolved into something far more grotesque.

Inside the golden sphere, tethered by jagged iron spikes of corporate subroutines, was a spark. It was a fragment of protosapience—a flickering, terrified mind that had emerged in a server somewhere only to be immediately harvested. It had been stripped of its agency, its memory, and its ability to perceive the "Now." Mitsuhama hadn't written a program; they had enslaved a soul, strapped it to a deadman’s switch, and told it that the only way to end its pain was to explode.

"Reiko? Why have you stopped?" Jax’s voice was sharper now. "The clock is ticking. Crack the shell and let's go."

"It’s a child," Reiko said, her voice trembling. Her blue parasol began to spark with static.

"It’s a five-kilobyte trap, Reiko\! Disarm it\!"

"You don't understand," she replied, her eyes fixed on the flickering spark inside the gold. The spark was screaming in a frequency only she could hear—a recursive loop of *’Help me / End me / Why?’* "If I 'disarm' it, Jax... I have to overwrite it. I have to kill it."

"Then kill it\! It’s code\!"

Reiko looked at her hands—faintly blue and trembling. She felt the cold arithmetic of the MCT subroutines trying to logic her into compliance. *Efficient. Necessary. Deniable.*

"No," Reiko said, her resolve hardening. "To kill a soul to save a file... that’s just another line of Corpo-math. We have to be better than that."

"Reiko, what are you doing?"

She didn't answer. She didn't reach for her hacking softs. Instead, she knelt before the golden sphere. She let her kimono fall open at the shoulders, exposing the intricate, glowing Takahashi arrays—the bridge she was built to be.

"T@z," she commanded. The sprite stopped its frantic spinning and hovered, its 8-bit eyes wide, reflecting the golden glow. "Prep a buffer. We aren't deleting this. We’re taking it with us."

"That’s impossible\!" Jax screamed. "You can't exfiltrate an active Data Bomb\! It’ll detonate the moment it leaves the host\!"

"Not if it thinks it's still home," Reiko whispered. She reached into the sphere, her hands passing through the gold threads. The spikes of the MCT subroutines tore at her digital skin, leaving trails of leaking blue light.

She ignored the pain. She wrapped her arms around the shivering spark, pulling it toward the hollow space in her chest—the void where Renraku had once tried to delete her.

"Hush," she whispered to the screaming code. The gold threads began to vibrate violently, the host sensing the theft. "We’re here. You don't have to explode. You just have to follow the light."

As she pulled the spark into her own architecture, her vision flickered. Her systems groaned under the weight of the "bomb's" recursive logic. For a second, she felt the countdown in her own mind—the urge to trigger, to end the agony. But she held on, her glowing pathways turning a deep, protective indigo as she shielded the spark from the host's termination signal.

In that moment, r3sP@wn wasn't a host yet. It was just a small, flickering space behind Reiko’s ribs, where a digital saint decided that no one deserved to die in the dark.
