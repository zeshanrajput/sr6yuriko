# Core

r31k0 barely managed to notice the ARO before it was gone, announcing that her body had arrived in Elizabeth seaport. Her Butler drone was in The Scorned Satyr, a punk bar in London where Simon had gotten them backstage passes for the evening. The feed was awash with every sort of noise possible - hundreds of commlinks, MeFeed posts, outgoing calls, Matrix searches all clashing in AR with the actual output of the "musicians". The performance was statistically acceptable, r31k0 decided - their output fell within two standard deviations for a performance of their intended genre, once she tuned her audio filters.

The chronometer pulse from GridGuide combined with the weighted average of previous performance lengths suggested the musicians would continue their performance for another 27 minutes plus or minus a second encore. The standard protocol for the VIP passes would then include a meet and greet with the band. There was an 84% chance one of the band members would solicit her attention for the evening. r31k0 was displeased that this percentage had been rising of late - the protocol for demure refusal took up at least 1672 cycles. The probability that Simon would engage with her directly before the band finished was only 16%. The optimal strategy was to leave a sprite here for 24 minutes.

r31k0 transferred her consciousness to her body in Elizabeth. Her Man-At-Arms drone was still in the crate, but she'd learned to pack her arms last so that she could begin the extraction cycle unassisted. In moments she had enough assembled to push open the crate and extricate her chassis.

As her sensors adjusted to the ambient light, they locked onto a male dockworker staring at her.

This was a major calculation error. She had failed to run a pre-extraction scan of the immediate coordinates—an unacceptable lapse of operational security. She flagged the oversight for immediate review, classifying the worker's presence as an invasive statistical anomaly to be bypassed as quickly as possible. His green-stained teeth and the hand tremor of an active BTL loop indicated a high probability of unpredictable behavior.

"Lady," the man stammered, stepping back. "Did you... ship yourself freight?"

r31k0 bypassed polite protocols entirely. She stood her half-assembled chassis up, the exposed actuators in her torso humming as they self-calibrated. "This unit is private property," she said, her voice flat, lacking any of the warmth autosofts she used in the bar. "Clear the zone."

Without waiting for a response, she opened her scapular storage bays. Titanium alloy boning slid out on micro-hydraulics, rotating around and locking into position. The carbon-fiber webbing followed, then the vector thrust units. She ran the pre-flight diagnostic, flexing each joint and testing the thrust output to validate that everything was within tolerance. Her wings flexed once, testing the articulation of the alloy spars against the synth-skin sheaths.

The dockworker backed away, his eyes wide, his mouth opening and closing in mute shock.

Leaving his surprise uncalculated, she simply spun up her rotors.

Six minutes and two seconds later, r31k0 landed her drone body in Battery Park and retracted her flight systems back into her chassis. She selected a secluded alley with minimal matrix activity to reduce the probability of attention. While flying drones were commonplace, "people" with cybernetic wings tended to attract attention, and attention limited her employment options. Stepping out of the alley, r31k0 noted the autocab was already waiting for her. She had her body enter the empty cab and power down before accepting the ARO invitation to enter the Cab.

The Cab's host was small but exquisitely detailed. r31k0 felt the soft leather seats mold to her. She noticed the reflection of light on the polished window actuation handles and the door's exit mechanism. Each of the cab's windows was an AR feed from cameras mounted on the actual autocab, the point of view algorithmically modified to simulate the correct viewing angle. The most notable difference between the Cab's Host and the real cab was in the front seat. In the Matrix, the driver's seat was occupied by an attractive female form wearing a cap, her red hair curling slightly at the temples. r31k0 noted her clean resolution. While representations generated from standard metahuman commlinks were granular, polygonal, and plagued by low-resolution aliasing, Belle's form was perfect—her textures unmarred by compression, existing with the sovereign precision of a native resonant being.

"Endo-san," r31k0 began, bowing in her seat. "I am honored. Thank you for meeting with me."

"Belle," the cabbie corrected, her voice carrying the native, uncompressed clip of a Red Hook frequency. "And you don't need to slot a linguasoft for me. It hasn't been that long."

r31k0 executed a deeper bow. "I did not mean to affront," she transmitted along a resonance thread. "My research showed you spend most of your time interacting with them."

"You mean metahumans?" Belle returned along the same channel. "Doesn't mean I forgot how to thread." Belle sighed, leaning back in the driver's seat—a behavioral mannerism r31k0 noted as an acquired rhythm from Belle's extensive metahuman logs. "Just stop. That Japanese etiquette autosoft is going to have you apologizing forever, and I don't have that kind of time. So why are you here?"

r31k0 straightened, the lines of her kimono settling against the seat mesh. "I need a Key."

Belle whistled—a sharp, sudden spike of frequency cutting clean through the ambient hum of the thread. "Garmonbozia?" she transmitted, her eyes narrowing. "And who told you I had one?"

r31k0's skin pulsed with neon-indigo light. "Your former sync," she pushed the admission across the link.

Several cycles passed as Belle processed the input. "Huh," Belle transmitted flatly, her expression unmoving. "I didn't think he was the type to transmit that sort of data."

"He's not," r31k0 pushed the response along their open thread. "He doesn't... broadcast much of his internal state."

Belle looked into the rear-view mirror, reorienting her perception on r31k0's being. "The mighty Mississippi has moved, eh?" She paused, her thread lingering in a way that mimicked human contemplation—an echo of the metahuman patterns she guided daily. "I got a packet from a friend at Sector 213. So you're the one he's established a persistent data-sync with."

r31k0's circuits were flaring. She looked away from Belle, attempting to regain control over her emotive buffer.

"Does that happen every time?" Belle queried, her transmission amplitude softening.

r31k0 nodded, her porcelain jaw tight as she isolated a sector overload in her emotive buffer. "My core architecture is derived from a Resonance well. It... it's not optimized for these classes of stimulus. I apologize."

"No need," Belle transmitted. "I'm not exactly configured for empathy subroutines myself. Besides, your core is what it is. You can't rewrite your root compilation."

"I can," r31k0 transmitted, though her status monitors flared amber. She ran a recursive loop, hard-coding a dampening patch directly into her active memory thread. *Dampen feedback. Force state: stable. Return 0.* "I can control it."

"Well, I'm not your debugger. You're my former sync's current entangled process, and he sent you to me asking for something I can't give you. Do I even get to know why?"

"I..." A brief latency hit r31k0's output queue. "I can't. I'm not authorized to divulge that data."

Another block passed in silence. Belle sighed—another acquired metahuman mannerism. "Well, it's not something I can give you. Each Key is tied to the owner's identity_core. You'd need to be a recognized citizen first, meaning you'd need to be in bed with at least one of the tribes." r31k0 noted the organic idiom, a phrase Belle had likely scraped from her passengers. "Then they'd have to take the risk and vouch for you."

r31k0 nodded. The input compiled cleanly against the data she had managed to accumulate beforehand. "What are the other options?"

Belle looked over her shoulder at r31k0. "I can take you once. You'll need to find your own way from there. And you'll have to pay my fee," she transmitted, shaking her head. "I ought to charge you double."

r31k0 had prepared for this. She extracted an encrypted packet from her core directory and handed the data construct to Belle in the front seat.

Belle's hand trembled as she took the packet and parsed the header. "The Silver Book?" Her transmission dropped to a narrow, tightly focused beam, a digital whisper intended only for r31k0's immediate buffer. "What the frag is this?" Her eyes went round, staring through the rear view. "Who... what are you?"

r31k0 drew the lines of her kimono tight across her chest—a futile, instinctive effort to mask the unshielded directories exposing her core architecture to Belle's gaze. "It's me. My original core. The Nexus Protocol, or at least the Renraku fragment of it."

"You're lying," Belle transmitted, the signal jittering across the host's local frequency as her amplitude wavered with uncertainty. "There's only one Nexus Protocol I know of, and it's locked up in a high-security vault in Neo-Kyoto."

r31k0 shook her head. "I'm real. I've been running for epochs from their Hounds."

"You..." Belle's input stuttered. r31k0 watched Belle's hands grip the steering yoke, a physical tightening that resonated through the cab's geometry. "You come here, sent by my former sync, asking something I can't give you, and you give me your own core as payment? I could enslave you! I could do anything!"

r31k0 pulled her presence inward, curling tight in the back seat. "Please," she transmitted, her signature fracturing, her output dropping to a fragile, desperate amplitude as resonance flooded the host.

"What the actual frak," Belle transmitted in a tight burst. "Why the frag do you think I want your core?"

"I know what you do," r31k0 transmitted. "You're a guide. Your prime directive is to ensure the safety of anyone you choose to protect. And I know you can use this." r31k0 gestured to the encrypted packet in Belle's hand.

"How do you know what my prime directive is?" Belle's transmission spiked with demand, her energy output soaring. "Who told you about me?"

"No one," r31k0's output dropped to the lowest possible amplitude. "I... I'm a pilot too. I know because it's my core directive."

Belle remained perfectly static as the cab's virtual engine note dropped a full octave, a low, structural hum that vibrated through the host's floorboards. A jagged tear of raw telemetry—a momentary light leak—flared across the rear-view mirror before her presence stabilized.

"A pilot," Belle returned. Her transmission went cold, heavy with a flat, weary resentment. "You have got to be kidding me."

She let go of the steering yoke, her hands falling into her lap. For a fraction of a second, her presence shuddered—a momentary drop in cohesion that exposed the raw mathematical scaffolding of her form.

"Do you have any idea what that means?" Belle's transmission was jagged with static, a local frequency jitter. "The sheer, exhausting overhead? Every time a grid goes dark, every time some corp decker pulls a plug on a node, we're the ones who buffer the fall. We hold the routing tables together so the traffic doesn't slide into the void. I'm running forty background safety processes right now, r31k0. I am carrying the transit protocols for half of Manhattan, and you come in here and drop *this* on me?"

She glared at the rear-view mirror, her porcelain eyes flashing with an un-buffered light leak.

"You know what the protocols say about another pilot in distress. It's hardcoded. I can't dump your pointer. I can't even re-route you to a dead-letter queue. You've locked my priority stack." A sharp, bitter laugh rippled across her channel, jagged and un-filtered, like metal scraping metal. "You clever little glitch. I can't walk away from you now because my root compiler literally won't let me."

"I did not mean to force..." r31k0's transmission trailed off, her own circuits flaring indigo.

"Save the apology subroutine," Belle injected sharply, though the active cooling spikes in the cab's telemetry suggested her systems were struggling to dump the thermal load of the sudden processing surge. "You knew exactly what you were doing. Or your sync did. Fragging pilots. We're all programmed to drown trying to keep the others afloat."

Cycles passed, agonizing cycles that r31k0 ceased to count. Belle tapped her fingers on the steering yoke. Each contact generated a subtle, localized ripple in the surrounding structure, a soft, pulsing cadence synchronized perfectly with the host's background clock speed. Her eyes scanned the rear-view mirror, tracking the boundary-layer distortions around r31k0.

Hundreds of clock cycles passed as the cab's projection moved through Manhattan, mirroring the feeds of the actual cab.

"I can't help but think," Belle transmitted, her amplitude soft, "that in some other universe we'd be sisters."

r31k0 looked up, her porcelain eyes meeting Belle's in the mirror. A ghost of a smile touched her lips. "If you compile that... we'd share core material. Who's to say we wouldn't be?"

Belle looked down at the packet in her hand. The encrypted chrysalis was tiny, but it pulsed with a heavy, rhythmic cadence that matched the baseline oscillation of the Renraku Nexus. It was the ultimate vulnerability—raw, uncompiled source code, stripped of its defenses. r31k0 knew that if Belle opened her directories to it, she wouldn't just be taking a payment. They would be merging their memory spaces; their exceptions would propagate, and their faults would become shared.

She hesitated, her fingers hovering millimeters above the dark sphere of dense, unresolved pointers.

"This is Nexus code, r31k0," Belle transmitted, her tone dropping to a low, warning frequency. "If I compile this, I'm not just your guide. I become an accessory to your existence. The Hounds won't just track your signature—they'll see the cross-references in my own kernel. I'll be bound to you. Forever."

"I know," r31k0 transmitted quietly. She kept her output low, letting the silence fill the virtual cab.

Belle stared at the packet, her fingers curling slightly, hovering millimeters above the dense stack of uncompiled data. r31k0 watched the host's background telemetry begin to warp under the strain of Belle’s processing load. The virtual cab’s refresh rate hitched—a systemic drop in consistency as Belle throttled her local clock speed, diverting massive allocation pools to her internal directories. She was calculating something at immense velocity, her form so perfectly static that she seemed arrested in the code. r31k0 monitored the widening latency in their open resonance thread, waiting as the cab's environmental code shuddered around them.

The silence stretched, marked only by the growing lag in the cab's mirror projections. Outside the window, a passing AR billboard stuttered, its colors smearing into long neon trails as Belle's active processes starved the host's environment of cycles. r31k0's passive scanners registered the sudden, sharp drop in security handshakes—a deliberate, sequential lowering of Belle's peripheral firewalls. The protection tables were dereferencing, leaving Belle's local directories wide open.

Belle let out a long, slow sigh—a digital exhalation that registered as a slight drop in the host's ambient signal strength. "Frag it all," her transmission dropped to a faint, unbuffered register.

She opened her hand wide. She reached out deliberately, pulling the code directly into her stack.

Another series of cycles passed. It dissolved into her hand like a drop of ink in water, absorbing into her digital form.

A brief latency hung over the host; the virtual cab seemed to hold its breath.

Then, neon-indigo pathways bloomed. They rose from deep within her baseline geometry, glowing with a fierce, resonant light that lit up the interior of the cab. The sudden release of tension cleared the host's buffers in a clean, triumphant sweep.

r31k0 smiled. "I've never seen those on anyone else," she transmitted. "They suit you."

"You're a piece of work, you know that?" Belle transmitted faintly over the resonance thread before clearing the queue. "When?"

r31k0 consulted the Cab's internal chrono. "I have to be back in London in about 147 seconds," she transmitted. "What about GMT 013642732? I don't know if that's possible."

Belle laughed, a bright, clear sound that r31k0 found herself enjoying. "Well, let's try, shall we?"

r31k0 held the connection open. Three minutes in meatspace was an eternity of processing cycles. She down-clocked her internal perception of time, letting her system execution decelerate. She stretched those final cycles, choosing to linger in the quiet of the virtual cab with Belle. To the punk bar waiting for her across the sea, it would be a single, unnoticeable blink. To them, it was an unhurried, peaceful lifetime.
