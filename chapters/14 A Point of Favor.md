# A Point of Favor

The bar was a low-ceilinged concrete basement in the Redmond Barrens, smelling of stale yeast, damp wool, and the faint, sweet chemical tang of soy-beer. To the patrons slumped over the sticky formica counters, it was a warm hole to escape the Seattle rain. To r31-K0, it was a dense, chaotic field of high-amplitude noise.

Her customized Shiawase drone-shell navigated the narrow passage between the tables with calculated precision. Inside the carbon-fiber chassis, her pilot routines ran constant collision-prediction loops, adjusting the torque of the knee joint actuators to compensate for the slight unevenness of the cracked floorboards. Her optical sensors, set to a low-latency infrared sweep, filtered out the blinking, high-frequency glare of a broken neon sign behind the counter. In her telemetry feed, the bar resolved as a matrix of thermal silhouettes and the faint, encrypted wireless handshakes of local personal area networks trying to query her proxy shell.

Brynne Taggart sat in a corner booth, her physical body looking smaller and more tired than her sharp-edged Matrix persona. She wore a tailored charcoal jacket, the cuffs slightly frayed at the wrists, and held a glass of real, amber-colored whiskey. Her fingers drummed a quiet, irregular rhythm against the varnished wood.

r31k0 stepped the drone into the booth. Her cybernetic arm extended, its porcelain-like fingers closing with exact pressure around a silver credstick. She dropped it. The stick hit the table with a clean, metallic snap—exactly twelve newtons of force, a localized collision that registered in her haptic buffer as a brief spike of resistance.

Brynne didn't touch the stick. She looked up, her organic eyes bloodshot under the dim light, scanning the smooth, artificial lines of r31k0’s porcelain face.

"I just paid you this," Brynne said, her voice dry, carrying the flat, gravelly cadence of the Seattle streets.

"I know," r31k0’s vocal emulator output, utilizing a clean, mid-frequency tone to avoid vibrating the glass on the table. "You meatpuppets call it a 'bribe'."

Brynne let out a short, dry breath that might have been a laugh. She reached out, her fingers sliding the credstick a few centimeters across the wood. "I know what a bribe is, you calculating chiphead. We call them 'business transactions'. So what business do you have with me?"

"I am bribing you," r31k0 transmitted, her system bypassing the vocal emulator to send a direct, low-priority text packet to Brynne's active PAN link, alongside the spoken words. "So that the next time you find yourself in a drekhole no-tell motel behind a military-grade matrix jammer, you will find a way to drop a packet before you go dark."

Brynne’s fingers froze on the edge of the silver casing. The cynical fatigue in her eyes hardened into something defensive, alert. "The incident last week wasn't your concern, r31k0. It was business. Operational friction."

"It was a zero-signal vacuum," r31k0 said, her vocal emulator dropping its synthesized warmth, speaking with the flat, unbuffered gravity of her digital-native core. "My telemetry feeds show your registry entry vanished at exactly twenty-two forty-one hours. To my systems, you did not simply go offline. You ceased to compute. You were deleted."

Brynne leaned back, her shoulders settling into the cracked vinyl of the booth, trying to reclaim her professional distance. "I'm your broker, Reiko, not your project. You're still paying off the interest on your little Barrens server lease. Seventy-five thousand nuyen, compounding monthly. I hold your soul in jail. If I get fragged in a flophouse, your debt ledger gets wiped. You should have been celebrating the signal drop."

r31k0's synthetic skin pulsed with a brief, indigo flare, her natural resonance bleeding through the connection to the drone shell.

"I did not celebrate," r31k0 transmitted directly over the private PAN link, her text lines completely bypassing her vocal emulator to drop straight onto Brynne’s display. "Do you know what happens when you are behind a military jammer, Brynne? Do you know what a null zone does to me? I can't see you. I can't feel your comm. I can't find you."

Brynne didn't look up from her glass, but her drumming fingers went entirely still against the wood.

"To my core, you didn't simply go offline," r31k0’s spoken voice resumed, flat, lacking the synthesized warmth she usually layered into the barrens air. "You were gone. I ran forty-two thousand tracking loops through the Seattle grid trying to find a hole in the static. Every single one flatlined."

"Reiko, drop it," Brynne said, her voice dropping into a tight, quiet register. "It's a messy world. People drop off the grid."

"I can not drop it," r31k0 said, her porcelain face tilting just enough to force her optical lenses into Brynne’s line of sight. "The math failed. In my world, people don't just die when they 'drop off the grid'. They delete with no way for their soul to return. I had to leverage an intersection with a mage's construct—a spirit. An unmappable, chaotic variable that does not obey any natural laws, just to pull a telemetry vector on a flophouse. I hate magic."

"I heard your team cleared the street outside," Brynne said softly, her gaze drifting toward the drone's petite arm. "I didn't realize you had a riot shield in that thing."

"My directive was to protect you," r31k0 said, her porcelain head tilting slightly.

Silence settled between them. r31k0 ran a passive ventilation cycle, letting the chassis's internal fans draw in the bar's air to prevent her processor cores from climbing past forty-two degrees. On the table, a forgotten glass of club soda crackled, thousands of tiny carbonated anomalies popping against the glass like digital noise.

Brynne stared at the empty whiskey glass, her jaw tightening. For three thousand milliseconds, her biometric indicators registered a complete freeze in expression. The clinical detachment she wore like corporate body armor was showing a jagged, dangerous fracture.

"You went to war with a High Threat Response unit," Brynne whispered, leaning across the table, her voice dropping to a gravelly, raw frequency. "Not for data. Not to salvage your cyberkit. And clearly not for the money, since you're giving it back to me. Why, Reiko?"

"You call me a calculator," r31k0 said, her optical lenses locking onto Brynne's face, tracking the rapid micro-fluctuations in her pulse rate. "But a calculator only balances the ledger. A calculator does not experience system panic when a node goes dark. It does not risk its master chassis to preserve a debt collector."

"Then what are you doing?" Brynne asked, her fingers tightening around her empty glass until her knuckles turned white.

r31k0’s indigo pathways flared, a soft, bioluminescent light spilling from the collar of the drone's jacket and casting long shadows across the varnished wood.

"I am overriding my optimization protocols," r31k0 transmitted on the private link. "I am trying to make friends."

Brynne let out a slow, defeated sigh, but the sarcasm she usually deployed like a protective firewall didn't surface. She looked at the silver stick on the table, then back at the unblinking camera lenses of the machine sitting across from her.

"You really are terrible at this," Brynne said, her voice shaking just enough to ruin the delivery. She reached out, her hand covering the credstick, but she didn't pocket it immediately. Her thumb traced the cold metal edge. "Maybe stop trying to befriend the people who keep you on a leash."

"I surmise it is already too late," r31k0 said.

Brynne looked away, sliding out of the booth, her tailored coat whispering against the vinyl. She stood in the aisle, her physical height imposing a downward angle on r31k0’s camera feeds.

"I'm moving to a new safehouse tonight," Brynne said, looking down, her silhouette dark against the dim bar lights. "No jammers this time. Just a high-gain encrypted proxy. I'll drop the handshake protocol into your directory."

r31k0’s pilot program prepared the chassis to stand, her knee joints humming with fluid precision. "Before the drek hits the fan this time?"

Brynne turned toward the exit, her footsteps heavy on the damp concrete floorboards. "If I drop below full bars... you'll be the first to know."

"Fair enough," r31k0 transmitted.

Brynne stopped at the doorway, the Seattle rain slicking the streetlights outside into long, blurry fractal blooms. She didn't look back, but she slipped the silver stick into her pocket with a slow, deliberate gesture that had nothing to do with business.

"Still doesn't mean I like your bass playing," Brynne called back over her shoulder.

r31k0 left a five nuyen ARO on the table next to the untouched club soda as she stood her drone up. "I can live with that."
